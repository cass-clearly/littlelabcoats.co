## 2) How to teach science if you are not a science person
**Hook:** You do not need to be “good at science” to teach beautiful science at home

**Caption:**
A lot of parents think teaching science means having all the answers.
It doesn’t.

You really just need to do three things:
- notice something
- ask a question
- help your child test or observe it

That’s science.

You do not need a lab coat, a degree, or a perfect explanation on the spot.
You can say:
- “What do you notice?”
- “What do you think will happen?”
- “How could we test that?”

That shift alone makes science feel so much less intimidating.

## 3) 3 kitchen science wins
**Hook:** 3 easy science activities you can do this week with things already in your house

**Caption:**
If science has been feeling like one more thing, try one of these instead:

1. Sink or float test
Grab a bowl of water and a few random objects.
Ask: what do you predict, and why?

2. Shadow check
Trace a shadow in the morning and again later.
Ask: what changed?

3. Sprout watch
Place seeds in a damp paper towel and observe them for a few days.
Ask: what does a plant seem to need?

That is enough.
That counts.
That is real science.

## 4) Water cycle without a boring worksheet
**Hook:** A better way to teach the water cycle than handing over a worksheet

**Caption:**
The water cycle makes more sense when kids can see it as a story:

water warms up → rises → cools → forms clouds → falls → moves again

Instead of starting with definitions, start with a visual.
Then ask:
- Where is the water going?
- What is making it move?
- What happens after the rain falls?

That gives kids a mental model before the vocabulary pile-on.

## 5) What to do when your child asks a science question you cannot answer
**Hook:** When your child asks a giant science question and your brain is just elevator music

**Caption:**
You do not have to know the answer immediately.
Honestly, that is often the best part.

Try this instead:
1. Repeat the question back
2. Ask what they think
3. Pick one small way to observe, compare, or test
4. Write down what you noticed

That teaches something bigger than the answer:
it teaches how science works.

Kids do not need us to be walking encyclopedias.
They need us to model curiosity.

## 8) Force and motion with toy cars
**Hook:** You can teach force and motion with toy cars and five minutes of floor space

**Caption:**
You do not need a fancy setup for force and motion.

Try this:
- roll one car gently
- roll one car harder
- change the ramp height
- compare what changes

Then ask:
- Which car moved farther?
- What changed?
- What stayed the same?

That gets kids thinking about push strength, motion, and cause/effect in a way they can feel in their bodies.
