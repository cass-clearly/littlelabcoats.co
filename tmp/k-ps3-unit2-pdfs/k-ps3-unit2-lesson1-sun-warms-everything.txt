📚 Grade: Kindergarten
🔢 Lesson: 1 of 6

⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes

🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Sunshine & Warmth

Which will get warmest in the sun? Let's find out!

📋 NGSS Standard:

K-PS3-1 — Make observations to determine the effect of sunlight on Earth's surface

🎯 Learning Objective
Students will investigate the Sun Warms Everything, make observations during the lesson activity, and explain what they learned
using evidence from the lesson.

🎯 The Big Idea
The sun is a giant ball of energy — and that energy travels all the way to Earth as light. When sunlight hits something, it can
warm it up! But not everything warms up at the same rate. Dark colors absorb (soak up) more sunlight and get warmer faster.
Light colors reflect (bounce back) more sunlight and stay cooler. Wet things also behave differently than dry things in the sun.

🔬 The Science
Sunlight carries energy. When light hits a surface, some of that energy is absorbed (converted to heat) and some is reflected
(bounced away). Dark-colored surfaces absorb more light energy, turning it into heat — which is why dark colors feel warmer.

📦 Materials
🏠 What You Need
1 piece of dark-colored paper (black or dark navy)
1 piece of white or light-colored paper
2 small cloth scraps (same fabric, neutral or light color \u2014 gray, beige, or unbleached cotton)
Water to wet one cloth
A sunny windowsill or outdoor spot
Timer (10 minutes)
Student worksheet (included)
Crayons or pencils

🔬 Let's Get Started
Step 1 — Introduce the Question (5 min)
Ask kids: "If we leave these four things in the sun for 10 minutes, which do you think will feel the warmest? Which will feel
the coolest?"
Show them the dark paper, white paper, dry cloth, and wet cloth. Have them make predictions on their worksheet before
you start.
Step 2 — Set Up the Experiment (3 min)
Wet one cloth with water (damp, not dripping)
Lay all 4 items flat in a sunny spot — same location, same sunlight exposure
Start the timer for 10 minutes
While waiting, talk about what sunlight is and where it comes from

Step 3 — Observe & Feel (5 min)
After 10 minutes, have each child gently place their hand on each item (1–2 seconds each).
Ask: "What do you notice? Which feels warmest? Which feels coolest?"
Have them record their observations on the worksheet.
Step 4 — Share & Discuss (10 min)
Use the discussion questions below. Guide kids to notice: dark paper felt warmest, white paper a bit less, dry cloth
moderate, wet cloth coolest. Discuss why! Let them share their ideas first before you explain.

💬 Discussion Question
🗣️ "Why do you think dark colors get warmer in sunlight than light colors?"
→ Dark colors absorb more light energy and convert it to heat. Light colors reflect light away, so less energy is
absorbed. That's why wearing a black shirt on a hot summer day makes you feel hotter than wearing a white shirt!

🗣️ "Why did the wet cloth feel cooler than the dry cloth?"
→ The sun's energy was used to evaporate the water instead of heating the cloth. Water soaks up heat energy as it
evaporates — that's why sweating cools you down!

🚀 Extensions
☀️ Color Swap Challenge: Try more colors! Does red heat up more or less than black? Where does orange fall?
🌡️ Temperature Check: If you have a thermometer, measure the temperature of each surface before and after.
Record the numbers!
🌊 Real-World Connection: On a hot day, touch a black car and a white car parked in the sun. What do you notice?
🧊 Think Ahead: If dark things absorb more heat, what would happen if you put ice on a dark plate vs. a white plate
in the sun? (That's Lesson 4!)

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

The Sun Warms Everything — K-PS3 Unit 2, Lesson 1

Best in strong sunlight: This works best on a sunny day. If it's cloudy, use a desk lamp positioned 6–8 inches from the
items.
Cloth color matters: Use neutral or light-colored cloth (gray, beige, or unbleached cotton) so the dry cloth ranks
between the dark paper and white paper as expected. A dark cloth will warm like dark paper; a white cloth will rank
like white paper — either will skew the results.
Safety note: The items may be warm but should not be hot enough to burn. Paper and cloth in typical sunlight are safe
to touch briefly.
For younger kids: Let them just feel and describe — "warm," "warmer," "warmest." The ranking matters more than
exact temperatures.
Discussion tip: Many kids intuit that black gets hotter because they've felt it. Build on what they already know from
experience!
Assessment: Can the child identify which item got warmest and say something like "dark colors absorb more sun" in
their own words? That's the target.

Sentence Frame — Sample Answers

💡 Science Explanation for Parents

Dark surfaces absorb more electromagnetic radiation from the sun and convert it to thermal energy (heat). Light surfaces
reflect more radiation. Wet surfaces lose thermal energy through evaporative cooling — the same process that makes
sweating effective for cooling the body. This is the foundational concept behind K-PS3-1: sunlight warms Earth's
surfaces.

✅ What Success Looks Like

Child can rank the items from warmest to coolest (even approximately)
Child can explain "dark colors get more warm" in their own words
Child makes a connection to real life (black car, dark clothing, etc.)
