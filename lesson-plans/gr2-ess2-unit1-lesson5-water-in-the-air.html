💧 Water in the Air

Earth Science — Weather Detectives & Wild Storms | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 50–60 minutes 🌎 Subject: Earth Science — Water & Weather 🧪 Hands-on Hook: Mini water-cycle bag + warm/cold comparison
☀️💧☁️🌧️
Water can be hidden in the air, collect as droplets, and return as precipitation.
📋 NGSS-aligned focus:
Building evidence-based explanations for evaporation, condensation, cloud formation, and precipitation.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will observe water changing location and form in a simple model, then explain how invisible water vapor in the air can become visible droplets, clouds, and precipitation.

💡 The Big Idea

Water is not only in puddles, lakes, and rain. Some water is in the air as water vapor, even when we cannot see it. When water warms, some of it can move into the air. When water vapor cools, it can condense into tiny droplets. In the sky, those droplets help make clouds and precipitation.

🔬 The Science

Kindergarten often introduces the water cycle as a simple circle picture. Grade 2 should go farther by asking: What evidence do we actually see? A sealed bag on a sunny window can show water moving upward and collecting as droplets. A cold lid or plastic wrap with ice can show that water vapor in warm air condenses when it cools. Those are weather clues, not just a memorized diagram.

This lesson matters because weather depends on water moving between the ground, the air, clouds, and precipitation. When children can explain where the droplets came from, they are doing real science thinking.

💬 Key vocabulary:
water vapor evaporation condensation droplet cloud precipitation

🧰 Materials

  • 1 zip-top bag
  • Water
  • Blue food coloring (optional)
  • Tape
  • 1 clear cup or jar
  • Plastic wrap or a plate/lid
  • Ice cubes
  • Student worksheet page from this lesson

🧪 Two Quick Weather Models

Model 1 — Sunny window bagPut a little water in a zip bag, seal it, and tape it to a sunny window. Watch where the droplets collect later.
Model 2 — Warm water + cold topPut warm water in a cup, cover it with plastic wrap or a plate, and place ice on top. Watch for droplets underneath.

🔍 Let’s Get Started

Step 1 — Start the mystery: Ask, “If the bag starts with water only at the bottom, why do droplets later appear higher up?” Let the child guess first.
Step 2 — Build the sunny window bag: Add a small amount of water to the zip bag. Seal it and tape it to a sunny window. If you use food coloring, remind the child that the dye helps us see the water more clearly.
Step 3 — Build the warm/cold model: Pour warm water into a clear cup. Cover the top with plastic wrap or a plate and place ice on top. Watch carefully for droplets collecting underneath the top surface.
Step 4 — Explain the evidence: In the bag, some water moved into the air inside the bag as water vapor, then condensed into droplets on the cooler plastic. In the cup model, warm water added vapor to the air and the cold top made that vapor condense.
Step 5 — Connect to clouds and rain: Explain that clouds also form when water vapor in the air cools into tiny droplets. If enough droplets join together, precipitation can fall.
Step 6 — Trace the journey: Have the child draw arrows showing where the water started, where it moved, and where droplets appeared.

💬 Discussion Questions

Did the droplets leak through the bag or cup cover?Look for: no — the water moved as vapor and then condensed back into droplets.
Why do we call water vapor “invisible water”?Look for: it is still water, but we usually cannot see it in the air.
How is this lesson more than just drawing a water-cycle circle?Look for: the child used evidence from a model and explained where the droplets came from.
What must happen before precipitation can fall?Look for: water vapor condenses into droplets or ice in clouds, and enough joins together to fall.

🌟 Extension

Extra challenge: Put one water bag in a sunny place and one in a shady place. Which one shows droplets sooner?

Family extension: After a shower, look at the bathroom mirror. Is that another example of condensation? Explain why.

👩‍🏫 Parent / Teacher Notes

  • This lesson avoids repeating Kindergarten by asking students to trace the evidence path of the water, not just label the cycle.
  • Use the words first and next often. Sequence matters: water moves into the air first, then condenses later.
  • If droplets form slowly, keep the models set up longer and revisit them later in the day.
  • The exact model is less important than the explanation: water did not come through the plastic; it condensed from water vapor.

✂️ Parent Answer Key — Detach Before Giving Worksheet to Students

1️⃣ What Happened in My Models?

Sunny bag: water started at the bottom; droplets appeared higher on the bag; process = evaporation then condensation.

Warm water + cold top: warm water started in the cup; droplets appeared underneath the cooled top; process = water vapor condensed on the cold surface.

2️⃣ Draw the Water’s Journey

Look for arrows showing water moving from the liquid water source into the air and then collecting as droplets.

3️⃣ Explain It Like a Scientist

Strong responses include the sequence: first some water became water vapor, then the vapor cooled and condensed into droplets.

4️⃣ Connect to Weather

Clouds form when: water vapor cools and condenses into tiny droplets or ice.

Precipitation can happen when: enough droplets or ice join together and become heavy enough to fall.

✅ What success looks like

  • The child explains where the droplets came from.
  • The child uses the sequence of evaporation first, condensation second.
  • The child connects the model to real weather instead of treating it as an isolated trick.
