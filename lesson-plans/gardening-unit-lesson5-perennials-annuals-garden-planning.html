📅 Perennials, Annuals & Garden Planning

Garden Detectives Unit, Lesson 5

🌼 Ages: mixed-age family learners⏱️ Time: 45–60 minutes🪴 Unit: Garden Detectives📖 Lesson: 5 of 6
Hands-on gardening science
Kids learn why some plants come back like reliable garden friends while others are one-season fireworks, then design a beginner-friendly garden bed.

Big idea: garden planning gets easier when we match plant life cycles, space, sunlight, and family goals.

🪴📐📅

🧠 Key Vocabulary

Annual

A plant that usually completes its life cycle in one growing season.

Perennial

A plant that can come back year after year when the climate and care fit.

Life cycle

The stages a plant goes through from seed to growth, flowers, and new seeds.

Spacing

How much room plants need so they do not crowd each other.

Season

The time of year that changes temperature, light, and planting success.

Sun

How much direct light a plant needs each day.

Plan

A garden design made before planting so choices match the space.

Tradeoff

A choice where gaining one good thing may mean giving up another.

Science fit: Supports plant life-cycle awareness, environmental needs, and engineering-style planning with constraints and criteria.

🎯 Learning Goal

Students will use observations and evidence to design a garden bed with annual/perennial reasoning.

📦 Materials

  • Seed packets, plant tags, or the printable Annual & Perennial Plant Cards
  • Paper garden-bed grid
  • Colored pencils
  • Ruler, optional
  • Small objects as plant markers, such as pebbles, bottle caps, beans, buttons, or pom-poms
  • Sun/shade notes from your yard or windowsill

🧪 Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Meet annuals and perennials
Annuals usually complete their life cycle in one growing season. Perennials can return year after year when conditions fit. Show a few real examples from packets, tags, or the printable plant cards.
Step 2 — Plant card sort
Sort the supplied plant cards into annual, perennial, or not sure. Then add 'why we might choose it' notes: food, color, pollinators, easy care, fragrance, or repeated harvests.
Step 3 — Constraints briefing
Choose the garden space: pot, window box, raised bed, or wildflower patch. Decide sunlight, watering plan, and who will care for it.
Step 4 — Design the bed
Place tall plants in back, short plants in front, spreaders where they have room, and pollinator flowers near the edges.
Step 5 — Defend the plan
Students explain two choices and one compromise: 'I wanted ___, but because ___, I chose ___.'

🌈 Mixed-age roles

Age 4 — Garden Sticker Planner

  • Choose favorite plant colors or foods
  • Place tall/short plant symbols on a simple bed
  • Say what the garden needs: sun, water, space

Age 9 — Bed Designer

  • Sort annual/perennial examples
  • Plan with sunlight and spacing
  • Explain one design tradeoff

Age 12 — Constraint Manager

  • Balance beauty, food, pollinator support, cost, and maintenance
  • Flag plants that spread or need extra care
  • Write a care calendar for two weeks

💬 Discussion Questions

Why might a gardener choose annuals if they only last one season?
Why are perennials useful in a garden plan?
What constraint changed your design the most: sun, space, water, time, or plant size?

🚀 Extensions

  • Price a tiny garden: compare seed packets vs starter plants and make a budget.
  • Create a two-week watering and observation schedule.
  • Research which perennials are hardy in your local USDA zone.
  • Use the printable plant cards to build two versions of the same bed: one food-focused and one pollinator-focused.

👩‍🏫 Parent / Teacher Notes

  • In mixed climates, annual/perennial labels can be fuzzy; the planning habit matters more than memorizing lists.
  • Keep the plan small. A container herb/pollinator pot is more executable than a giant imaginary farm.
  • This lesson raises the reasoning demand so Lesson 6 can become a real wildflower/pollinator design capstone.

✅ Parent Answer Key / Coaching Guide

  • Annual/perennial status can vary by climate; accept 'not sure' when students use evidence like seed packet or plant tag.
  • A strong design includes sunlight, spacing, plant height, and a care plan.
  • A strong tradeoff names a real constraint: limited pot space, full sun, toddler/pet access, watering time, or plant spread.
  • Older learners should move beyond 'pretty' into explicit criteria and constraints.
