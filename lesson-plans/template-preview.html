🚢 Sink or Swim? Exploring Density! 🛟

Physical Science — Properties of Matter

📚 Grades: 3rd – 5th ⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes 🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Matter & Density
🚢💧🪨
Why do some things float and others sink? Let's find out!
📋 NGSS Standard:
5-PS1-3 — Make observations and measurements to identify materials based on their properties
3-5-ETS1-1 — Define a simple design problem reflecting a need or want

🌟 The Big Idea

Whether something floats or sinks depends on its density — how much matter is packed into a certain space. Objects less dense than water float; objects more dense than water sink. In this hands-on experiment, students will test household items, make predictions, collect data, and discover the hidden property that determines buoyancy!

💡 Key vocabulary: density, buoyancy, mass, volume, matter, float, sink, predict, observe

🔬 The Science

What is density? Imagine you have two boxes that are exactly the same size. One is filled with feathers, the other with rocks. Both boxes take up the same amount of space (volume), but the rock box is much heavier (more mass). The rock box is denser — it has more "stuff" packed into the same space.

Density is calculated as: Density = Mass ÷ Volume. Objects with lots of mass packed into a small space are very dense (like a rock). Objects with little mass spread out over a large space are less dense (like a foam ball).

Why do things float or sink? Water has a density of about 1 gram per cubic centimeter. When you drop something in water, it "compares" its density to water's density:

  • If the object is less dense than water (density < 1 g/cm³) → it floats!
  • If the object is more dense than water (density > 1 g/cm³) → it sinks!

This is why a huge metal ship can float (it's hollow inside, so its overall density is less than water), but a tiny metal screw sinks (solid metal is very dense). It's not about size or weight — it's all about how much "stuff" is packed into the space!

🔬 Let's Get Started!

Step 1 — Set Up Your Station (3 min):
Fill your container with water (about ¾ full). Gather your test objects. Set out the Student Worksheet and a pencil. Lay down a towel for spills.
Step 2 — Make Predictions (5 min):
Before testing, have students look at each object and predict whether it will float or sink. Record predictions on the worksheet. Ask: "Why do you think this will float/sink?" Encourage reasoning beyond "it's heavy" — push them to think about size, material, and how tightly packed it feels.
Step 3 — Test Each Object (15–20 min):
One at a time, gently place each object in the water. Observe: does it float on top, sink to the bottom, or suspend in the middle? Record results on the worksheet. After each test, ask: "Was your prediction correct? If not, why do you think it behaved differently?"
💡 Tip: Some objects might surprise you! A grape might sink, but a raisin might float (less dense when dried). An orange with its peel floats, but a peeled orange sinks (the peel has air pockets). These are great teachable moments!
Step 4 — Look for Patterns (5 min):
After testing all objects, look at your data together. Ask: "Do you notice any patterns? What do all the floating objects have in common? What about the sinking objects?" Guide students toward the idea that it's not just about weight — it's about how "packed" the material is.
Step 5 — Introduce Density (5 min):
Explain the concept of density using the "feathers vs. rocks in a box" analogy from The Science section above. For 5th graders, introduce the formula: Density = Mass ÷ Volume. Show how a small, heavy object (like a marble) is denser than a large, light object (like a foam ball).
Step 6 — Optional: Measure Density (10 min for 5th grade):
If you have a scale and measuring tools, calculate the actual density of 2–3 objects. Weigh the object (mass in grams). Measure its volume (use water displacement method: fill graduated cylinder, drop object in, measure how much water level rises). Divide mass by volume. Compare to water's density (1 g/cm³). Does the math match your observation?
Step 7 — Discuss & Connect (5–10 min):
Use the discussion questions below to deepen understanding and connect to real-world applications.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🧂 Salt Water Challenge: Dissolve ¼ cup of salt in your water. Retest objects that barely sank before. Do they float now? (Salt increases water's density, so less-dense objects float more easily!)
  • 🏗️ Build a Boat: Using only aluminum foil, design a boat that can hold the most pennies without sinking. Test different shapes. Which design is best?
  • 🥚 The Floating Egg Trick: A fresh egg sinks in tap water but floats in very salty water. Try it! Explain why using what you learned about density.
  • 🌊 Layered Liquids: Pour honey, dish soap, water, and vegetable oil into a clear jar (don't stir!). They'll separate into layers based on density. Drop in small objects and see which layer they "float" in!
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "Who Sank the Boat?" by Pamela Allen or "Mr. Archimedes' Bath" by Pamela Allen
  • 🌍 Real-World Connection: Research how submarines control buoyancy by filling/emptying ballast tanks with water. How do they sink on purpose and then rise back up?
  • 📊 5th Grade Math: Calculate the density of five different objects. Graph them on a bar chart. Draw a line at 1 g/cm³ (water's density). Which objects are above the line? Below?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Mess level: Low to moderate. Water will spill a little — towels are your friend. Do it over a sink or outside if you're worried.
  • Keep it hands-on: Let kids touch and examine objects BEFORE testing. The tactile experience (heavy vs. light, solid vs. hollow) builds intuition about density.
  • For younger kids (3rd): Focus on observation and pattern-finding. Skip the density formula. Use simple language: "packed tightly" vs. "spread out."
  • For older kids (5th): Push the math! Calculate actual densities. Graph results. Challenge them to design experiments (make clay float, test salt water, etc.).
  • Common misconceptions:
    • "Heavy things sink, light things float" — FALSE! A huge log floats, a tiny pebble sinks. It's about density, not weight.
    • "Big things float, small things sink" — FALSE! A marble (small) sinks, a beach ball (big) floats. Size doesn't determine buoyancy.
    • Clarify: Density = how much matter is packed into a space. A tiny rock can have more "stuff" packed in than a giant foam block.
  • Surprising results (teachable moments!):
    • Orange: With peel = floats (air pockets in peel). Peeled = sinks (no air pockets).
    • Grapes: Fresh grapes often sink. Raisins (dried grapes) may float because they've lost water weight but kept volume.
    • Ice: Always floats! Ice is less dense than liquid water (rare property — most solids are denser than their liquid form).
    • Diet soda vs. regular soda: Diet floats, regular sinks! Sugar makes regular soda denser.
  • Time-savers: Pre-select and wash all test objects before the lesson. Have the water station set up and ready. This turns a 60-minute lesson into a crisp 45-minute one.
  • Assessment: Can students correctly predict new objects after testing the first set? Can they explain WHY something floated using the word "density"? That's mastery.
  • Extension for advanced learners: Research Archimedes' Principle (an object displaces water equal to its own volume; buoyant force = weight of displaced water). This is high school physics, but curious 5th graders can grasp the basics!

Sample Answers to Discussion Questions

For All Students:

  • Q: What do floating objects have in common?
    A: They're all less dense than water. Many have air inside them (hollow objects, porous materials like wood or cork). They might feel light for their size, or they might be made of materials that naturally float (like plastic or wood).
  • Q: What do sinking objects have in common?
    A: They're all more dense than water. They feel heavy for their size. They're often made of solid materials with no air pockets (like metal, glass, or stone).
  • Q: Can something heavy float?
    A: YES! A huge ship weighs thousands of tons but floats because it's hollow inside (full of air). Its overall density (including all the air space) is less than water.
  • Q: Can something light sink?
    A: YES! A tiny pebble is light but sinks because it's made of dense rock. A paperclip is light but made of dense metal.

For 5th Graders (additional):

  • Q: What is density?
    A: Density is how much matter (mass) is packed into a certain amount of space (volume). Formula: Density = Mass ÷ Volume. If lots of matter is packed tightly, it's high density. If matter is spread out, it's low density.
  • Q: Water has density 1 g/cm³. If rock is 2.5 g/cm³, will it float or sink?
    A: SINK. The rock is 2.5 times denser than water, so it will sink to the bottom.
  • Q: Why can a metal ship float but a paperclip sinks?
    A: The ship is hollow — it has air inside. When you calculate its overall density (metal + air), it's less than 1 g/cm³, so it floats. The paperclip is solid metal (density ~7–8 g/cm³), so it sinks.
  • Q: How could you make clay float?
    A: Shape it into a bowl or boat! When you make it hollow, you trap air inside. The clay + air combination has an overall density less than water, so it floats. (Solid clay ball = sinks. Hollow clay boat = floats!)
  • Q: What happens in salt water?
    A: Salt water is denser than fresh water (about 1.025 g/cm³ instead of 1.0). Objects that barely sank in fresh water might float in salt water! This is why it's easier to float in the ocean than in a pool.

Common Surprises & Teachable Moments

  • Orange with peel floats, peeled orange sinks: The peel is porous and full of tiny air pockets, lowering the overall density. Without the peel, the fruit flesh is denser than water.
  • Ice always floats: Water is one of the rare substances where the solid form (ice) is LESS dense than the liquid form. This is because water molecules arrange in a crystal structure when frozen, creating space between them. Most materials are denser when solid!
  • Diet soda floats, regular soda sinks: Sugar is dense! Regular soda has so much dissolved sugar that its overall density is >1 g/cm³. Diet soda uses artificial sweeteners (much less mass) so it's lighter and floats.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

  • Object suspends in the middle (not floating or sinking): Its density is EXACTLY the same as water! This is rare but cool. It's called neutral buoyancy.
  • "But the heavy rock sank!" — Redirect from weight to density. Compare a heavy wooden log (floats) to a light marble (sinks). It's not about weight; it's about how packed the material is.
  • Students confuse mass and volume: Use concrete examples. A brick of foam and a brick of metal are the same volume (size) but very different mass (weight). The metal brick is denser.

Extension Activity Guidance

Salt Water Challenge: Dissolve ¼ cup salt in 4 cups warm water. Test objects that barely sank in fresh water (like a raw egg, a grape, or a raisin). They may float now! Salt water has a higher density (~1.025 g/cm³), so objects with density between 1.0–1.025 will sink in fresh water but float in salt water. This is why swimming in the ocean feels easier — your body is more buoyant!

Layered Liquids: Pour these in order (don't stir!): honey (bottom), dish soap, water, vegetable oil (top). They separate by density! Drop in small objects and see which layer they land in. A paperclip sinks through everything. A plastic bead might float on the water layer. A grape might sink through oil but float on soap. Density determines position!
