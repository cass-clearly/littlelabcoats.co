✍️ Short Response — Use Evidence

Explain how water moved through one Earth system model from the unit. Use words like evaporation, condensation, precipitation, runoff, or groundwater in your answer.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Use the quiz and short response together: recognition plus explanation gives a stronger picture of understanding.
  • Good evidence sounds specific: look for vocabulary, observations, and a clear reasoned explanation.
  • Fast grading tip: scan for whether the student uses unit vocabulary correctly and connects evidence to their claim.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Parent note: Student wording may vary if the science reasoning is correct.

Multiple-Choice Answer Key

  1. Students will describe where most of Earth’s water is found and compare salt water with fresh water reservoirs.
  2. Students will model how evaporation and condensation move water between Earth’s surface and the atmosphere.
  3. Students will explain how condensed water can return to Earth as precipitation and collect in different reservoirs.
  4. Students will compare runoff and infiltration and explain how water can move across land or into the ground.
  5. Students will identify how Earth’s spheres interact through water movement.
  6. Students will build and defend a model of a local or imagined water system using Earth-system interactions.
  7. Using evidence from observations, tests, or models to support explanations
  8. To make system relationships easier to see and explain

Short Response — What Success Looks Like

  • Student describes more than one part of the water system and connects those parts in a sensible sequence or cause/effect chain.
  • Student uses water-system vocabulary correctly instead of listing isolated terms without explanation.
  • Student explains the role of the Sun or gravity in moving water through the model.

Short Response — Sample Acceptable Answer

“Water evaporated because the Sun warmed it, then it condensed into droplets and later moved back down as precipitation. Some water ran off over land and some soaked into the ground.”
