Waves Carry Energy

Physical Science — Waves, Light & Information | Unit 2, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Waves, Light & Information
A bigger wave motion can carry more energy than a smaller wave motion through the same rope or spring.
📋 NGSS Standard:
4-PS4-1 — Develop a model of waves to describe patterns in terms of amplitude and wavelength and that waves can cause objects to move.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will model waves with a rope, string, or slinky, compare small and large wave motions, and explain with evidence how waves can transfer energy and cause objects to move.

💡 The Big Idea

Waves are patterns of motion that can carry energy from one place to another. Bigger wave motions can transfer more energy, and that energy can make another object move.

🔬 The Science

Science Snapshot
This model shows the key idea visually: a hand makes the wave, the pattern travels, and a larger wave can create a bigger effect at the far end.

What is happening?

When a hand shakes a rope or spring, the material moves up and down or back and forth, but the pattern travels across it. That traveling pattern is the wave.

Why it works that way

The wave transfers energy from the place where it started to another place. Bigger motions usually transfer more energy than smaller motions through the same setup.

What evidence should students collect?

Students should compare what happens when they make a small wave and a larger wave, then look for a change at the far end such as a paper flag, tape marker, or foam piece moving more.

Ask while you read: What part of the system moves back and forth, and what part of the system shows that energy traveled?

📦 Materials

🏠 Household Items to Gather

  • One jump rope, long string, ribbon, or slinky-style spring
  • Masking tape
  • One paper flag, sticky note, or small foam piece for the far-end target
  • Ruler or measuring tape
  • Worksheet and pencil
  • Optional: shallow pan of water for a quick comparison demo

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Build the wave line
Stretch the rope, string, or spring across a table or floor space with a partner holding the far end. Tape a small paper flag near the far end so it is easy to see movement.
Step 2 — Make one small wave
Move one hand up and down gently one time. Watch the pattern travel and observe whether the far-end flag moves.
Step 3 — Make one larger wave
Use the same rope and same distance, but move the hand with a bigger motion. Compare what happens to the far-end flag or marker.
Step 4 — Repeat fairly
Run both wave sizes at least three times. Keep the same rope, same distance, same partner, and same target. Only change the size of the hand motion.
Step 5 — Measure and describe patterns
Students sketch the small wave and the larger wave, then describe which wave had the greater amplitude and which one caused a bigger effect at the far end.
Step 6 — Show that waves can move objects
Place the paper flag, foam bit, or a taped loop where the wave energy reaches the end. Ask which wave made the object move more and what that suggests about energy transfer.
Step 7 — Optional water comparison
Tap water gently in a shallow pan, then tap it harder. Compare the smaller ripples and larger ripples. Ask how the water example is similar to the rope example.
Step 8 — Explain with evidence
Students write a model-based explanation using the words wave, amplitude, energy, and move.

💬 Discussion Questions

What part traveled across the rope?The wave pattern traveled across the rope, even though the rope itself only moved back and forth locally.
What is amplitude in this lesson?It is how big the wave motion is — the larger up-and-down motion makes a larger wave.
What evidence showed that the wave carried energy?The far-end flag or object moved when the wave reached it.
Why did we repeat the small wave and large wave several times?Repeating helps students see a pattern and makes the comparison more trustworthy.
Why is it important to change only one thing?If the rope, distance, and target stay the same, it is easier to connect the different result to the different wave size.

🚀 Extensions

  • Wave messenger: Build a cup-and-string phone later in the unit and compare how another kind of wave carries information.
  • Sound connection: Tap a ruler hanging off a table a little and then a lot. Compare the vibration size and what you hear.
  • Light connection: Shine a flashlight on a mirror and trace how light also travels and can carry information, even though it does not need a rope or water.

👩🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep pointing students back to the evidence target at the far end. That makes the idea of energy transfer visible instead of abstract.

Misconception to catch: Students may think the whole rope traveled across the room. Clarify that the rope material mostly moved in place while the pattern moved across it.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample pattern

Answers vary, but the larger wave should usually make the far-end target move more than the smaller wave when the same rope and distance are used.

What students should name

  • Stayed the same: the same rope, partner, distance, and target
  • Changed: the size of the hand motion, which changed the wave amplitude

Sample explanation

The larger wave had a greater amplitude, so it transferred more energy through the rope. My evidence is that the far-end flag moved more when we made the larger wave. The rope and distance stayed the same, so the wave size was the important change.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student develops and uses a simple wave model.
  • Student connects a larger amplitude with a bigger effect.
  • Student gives evidence that the wave caused another object to move.
