🌩️ Atmosphere + Hydrosphere Weather Clues

Grade 5 Earth systems investigation with real data, variable testing, and evidence-based explanation

📚 Grade: 5th Grade 🔬 Domain: Earth & Space Science ⏱️ Time: 60–75 minutes 🧠 Challenge level: Upper elementary stretch

This is not a cute jar demo. Students act like meteorologists: they build a model, change one variable, collect timed evidence, and defend a claim about how the atmosphere and hydrosphere work together to create weather.

Printable reference card Companion quiz
Mission: Build a tabletop weather system and figure out which condition creates the strongest evidence of evaporation and condensation. Your job is to prove your answer with observations, not vibes.
📋 NGSS Alignment:
5-ESS2-1 — Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interact.

Why this lesson fits: Students use a physical model, manipulate a variable, and explain how water moves between the hydrosphere and atmosphere. They must support their thinking with recorded observations and a labeled systems model.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will investigate how temperature and airflow change the interaction between the hydrosphere and atmosphere, then use evidence from their model to explain real weather phenomena such as clouds, dew, fog, and precipitation.

💡 The Big Idea

What strong Grade 5 science sounds like

Water is constantly moving between Earth systems. When surface water gains energy, some of it evaporates into the air. When that water vapor cools, it condenses into droplets. In real life, that exchange helps produce clouds, dew, fog, and precipitation.

  • Hydrosphere: the warm liquid water source
  • Atmosphere: the air space holding water vapor
  • Energy transfer: warming speeds evaporation; cooling triggers condensation
  • Weather clue: visible droplets are evidence that invisible vapor changed state
Success target: By the end, students should be able to say which setup produced the strongest weather clues, what evidence supports that claim, and how the model is useful and limited.

📦 Materials

Household materials

  • 2 clear jars or cups
  • Warm tap water
  • 2 covers: plates, plastic wrap, or zip-top bags
  • Ice cubes
  • Dark paper or towel under each container
  • Timer or phone stopwatch
  • Small fan, folded paper, or cardboard for airflow test
  • Pencil and printed worksheet

Recommended setup

  • Jar A: warm water + room-temperature lid
  • Jar B: warm water + ice-cooled lid
  • Keep the amount of water the same in both jars
  • Observe both jars at the same time intervals
  • Optional second round: test airflow after the first comparison
Safety note: Use warm tap water only, never boiling water. An adult should pour the water and handle glass. Clean up spills right away so the experiment stays science-y, not slapstick.

🌦️ Phenomenon Launch

Start with a real question

Ask: Why do some mornings have fog on the window, dew on the grass, or clouds building overhead while other days do not?

Tell students they are going to test a model that helps explain those weather clues by tracking what happens when water and air interact under different conditions.

🧪 Investigation: Build a Storm Lab

Step 1 — Predict like a scientist (5 min)Students examine the two setups and make a claim about which jar will show stronger condensation evidence first. They must explain why before testing.
Step 2 — Build two controlled models (8 min)Put the same amount of warm water in Jar A and Jar B. Cover both. Add ice only to Jar B’s lid. Label water as hydrosphere and the air above as atmosphere. Emphasize that the cooled lid is the only intended variable.
Step 3 — Record timed observations (10–12 min)Observe both jars at 1, 3, 5, and 8 minutes. Students note where droplets appear first, how many are visible, whether fogginess forms, and which setup changes fastest.
Step 4 — Add airflow as a second factor (5–8 min)Gently fan one jar for 20–30 seconds or place a fan on low nearby. Students record whether moving air changes visibility, cooling, or droplet patterns. Discuss how wind moves heat and moisture in real weather systems.
Step 5 — Model the system with arrows (10 min)Students draw both jars and use arrows to show water moving from liquid to vapor to droplets. They must label evaporation, condensation, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and the variable they changed.
Step 6 — Defend the explanation (12–15 min)Students write a CER response: Which condition produced the strongest weather clues, what evidence supports that claim, and how does the model explain a real weather event?

🧠 Push the Thinking Higher

Evidence challenge

  • Which observation was the earliest sign that water changed state?
  • What counts as stronger evidence: one giant droplet or many small droplets? Defend your answer.
  • How do you know the droplets were not leaking through the lid?

Systems-thinking challenge

  • What part of the model stands in for the atmosphere?
  • What part of real weather is missing from this model?
  • How might the geosphere or biosphere affect the same water cycle in the real world?

💬 Discussion Questions

Why did the ice-cooled lid usually create stronger droplets?Students should connect cooling to condensation, not just say “because it was colder.”
What evidence showed water moved from the hydrosphere into the atmosphere before becoming visible again?Look for sequence reasoning: warm water, invisible vapor in the air, then visible droplets.
Why is this model useful even though it is tiny and simplified?A good answer names both usefulness and limitations.
How could this investigation help explain dew, clouds, fog, or rain outside?Students should transfer the mechanism to a real weather clue.

🚀 Extensions

Forecast connection

Check the local forecast for humidity, temperature, and chance of precipitation. Ask students which pieces of data suggest stronger atmosphere-hydrosphere interaction that day.

Design a better model

Challenge students to sketch an improved weather model that includes a sun/heat source, land surface, or moving air. What would it explain better than the jar system?

Outside evidence hunt

Have students photograph or sketch one real weather clue—dew, puddles drying, clouds building, foggy glass—and label where the atmosphere and hydrosphere interacted.

Data twist

Repeat the experiment with cold water instead of warm water and compare how quickly condensation appears. Which variable mattered more: the water temperature or the lid temperature?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Raise the bar: Don’t accept vocabulary-only answers. Ask for the specific observation that supports each claim.
  • Common misconception: Condensation is not the same thing as “steam.” Students should know droplets formed when water vapor cooled.
  • Strong NGSS move: Students should compare setups, identify the variable, and explain why one model produced clearer evidence.
  • If your child races through it: Ask them to explain one limitation of the model and propose an improvement. That usually separates “finished” from “actually understood.”

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Sample high-quality explanation

Jar B usually showed stronger weather clues because the colder lid caused water vapor in the atmosphere part of the model to cool faster and condense into visible droplets. The warm water in both jars represented the hydrosphere and supplied water vapor through evaporation, but the ice-cooled lid created clearer evidence of condensation. This helps explain real weather because clouds, fog, dew, and precipitation all depend on water moving into the air and then cooling back into liquid droplets.

✅ What success looks like

  • Student correctly identifies the changed variable and keeps the rest of the setup controlled.
  • Student uses actual observations, not just definitions, as evidence.
  • Student explains that condensation formed from cooled water vapor, not from water “leaking” through the lid.
  • Student names at least one limitation of the model, such as missing clouds at scale, landforms, sunlight, or full wind systems.
