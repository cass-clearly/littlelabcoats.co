🌦️ Atmosphere + Hydrosphere Weather Clues

Grade 5 Free Weather Investigation — Earth Systems Interactions

📚 Grade: 5th Grade🔬 Domain: Earth & Space Science⏱️ Time: 45–60 minutes
Use it this week: Students build a tiny weather system in a clear container, observe condensation and moving air clues, then model how the atmosphere and hydrosphere interact to make observable weather.

Make weather visible with a warm-water jar, a cool lid, and a simple observation chart.
Weather is not random magic: it is evidence of Earth systems interacting — especially water at Earth’s surface and gases in the air above it.
📋 NGSS Alignment:
5-ESS2-1 — Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interact.

Why this lesson fits: Students use a physical model and a labeled drawing to explain how water from the hydrosphere can enter the atmosphere as vapor, cool into droplets, and create weather clues such as clouds, fog, dew, or precipitation.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will build and use a model to explain how the hydrosphere and atmosphere interact, then support their weather explanation with observed evidence.

💡 The Big Idea

The hydrosphere and atmosphere constantly exchange water and energy. When warm surface water evaporates and cooler air or surfaces cause condensation, we can observe weather clues such as droplets, clouds, fog, dew, and precipitation.

Science snapshot

This model turns invisible water vapor into visible droplets, making a Grade 5 Earth-systems interaction concrete enough to observe and explain.

  • Hydrosphere: warm liquid water at Earth’s surface
  • Atmosphere: air space holding water vapor
  • Weather clues: condensation, cloud/fog, wind, and precipitation patterns

📦 Materials

🏠 Household Items to Gather

  • Clear glass jar, clear cup, or clear plastic container
  • Warm tap water — warm, not boiling
  • Small plate, clear plastic wrap, or a zip-top bag to cover the opening
  • Ice cubes
  • Dark paper or towel under the container so droplets are easier to see
  • Small fan, paper fan, or a piece of cardboard for gentle moving-air observations
  • Timer, pencil, and printed worksheet
  • Optional: flashlight to make droplets easier to spot

Safety note: Use warm tap water only. Do not use boiling water. An adult should pour the water and handle glass. If the container feels hot, wait until it is comfortable to touch before students work with it.

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Set up the question (5 min)
Ask: How can water at Earth’s surface help create weather clues in the air? Students predict where they might see evidence of water moving into the air or changing back into droplets.
Step 2 — Build the tiny weather system (5–8 min)
Pour 2–4 cm of warm water into the clear container. Cover the top with a plate, plastic wrap, or a sealed bag. Label the warm water hydrosphere and the air space above it atmosphere.
Step 3 — Add a cold surface (10 min)
Place ice on the cover. Watch the inside top and sides closely. Students record the first clue they see, where droplets appear, and whether the number of droplets changes over time.
Step 4 — Make moving-air clues (5 min)
Gently fan near the outside of the container or use a fan on low nearby. Notice whether droplets, fogginess, or temperature feel changes. Explain that real weather also depends on moving air, not just water.
Step 5 — Draw a labeled model (10–12 min)
Students sketch the container and add arrows: liquid water → water vapor → droplets. They must label hydrosphere, atmosphere, evaporation, and condensation.
Step 6 — Connect model to real weather (10–15 min)
Compare the model to a real weather example: dew on grass, fog on a window, clouds forming, or rain. Students write a claim-evidence-reasoning explanation.
Student claim frame: The hydrosphere and atmosphere interact when __________. My evidence from the model was __________. This helps explain weather because __________.

💬 Discussion Questions

What evidence showed that water moved from the hydrosphere into the atmosphere?Look for vapor/fog/droplet observations and the sequence from warm water to air space.
Why did droplets form on the cool lid or cover?Water vapor cooled and condensed into liquid droplets on the colder surface.
How is this model like real weather, and how is it different?It shows evaporation and condensation, but it is smaller, simpler, and does not include every part of the atmosphere.
Why is a labeled model stronger than a drawing without labels?Labels and arrows explain the system interaction instead of only showing what the setup looked like.

🚀 Extensions

  • Cold vs. room-temperature cover: Repeat with no ice and compare droplet formation.
  • Local weather bridge: Look at today’s weather forecast and identify water clues: humidity, cloud cover, dew point, fog, or precipitation.
  • Weather detective walk: Find one outside clue that water and air interacted recently, such as dew, puddles drying, frost, clouds, or damp soil.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Best teaching move: Keep asking, “What is your evidence?” Grade 5 students should explain from observations, not just repeat vocabulary.
  • Common misconception: Droplets on the lid did not leak through from above. They formed when water vapor in the air cooled and condensed.
  • NGSS move: The finished product is the labeled model plus the explanation of hydrosphere-atmosphere interaction.
  • Pacing: If time is short, do steps 1–3 live, then complete the model and writing later.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Sample explanation

The warm water represented the hydrosphere. Some liquid water evaporated into water vapor in the air space, which represented the atmosphere. When the water vapor touched the cool lid, it condensed into droplets. This model shows one way the hydrosphere and atmosphere interact to create weather clues such as clouds, fog, dew, or precipitation.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student labels both Earth systems: hydrosphere and atmosphere.
  • Student uses arrows or sequence language to show water moving/changing state.
  • Student identifies condensation droplets as evidence.
  • Student connects the small model to a real weather clue without claiming the model shows every part of weather.
