# Calvin Audit — Grade 3 LS2 Unit 4 (`gr3-ls2-unit4-*`)

Last updated: 2026-04-14  
Reviewer: Calvin  
Scope: continuity, novelty, repetition, knowledge progression, experiment differentiation, and cross-grade value

> Serena referred to this as the next Grade 3 Life Science unit that also needs overhaul. In the repo, the current package is `gr3-ls2-unit4-*` and is titled **Group Behavior & Survival**.

## Verdict

Serena is right again: this unit is not a clean 6-step progression. It reads like six versions of the same claim — **group behavior helps survival** — with small example swaps instead of real knowledge lift. Lessons 2–5 all reuse the same pattern: look at one group behavior, compare group vs alone, fill in a three-column chart, then restate the survival benefit. That makes the middle of the unit flat, and Lesson 5 is especially weak because it overlaps heavily with Grade 1 parent/offspring protection work instead of feeling like a stronger Grade 3 behavior lesson. Calvin judgment: **complete overhaul recommended**.

## Lesson-by-lesson findings

### Lesson 1 — `gr3-ls2-unit4-lesson1-why-animals-sometimes-stick-together.html`
**Disposition:** keep concept, tighten hard

What works:
- correct intro role
- establishes that behavior can have a function
- gives the capstone a usable anchor claim

What is weak:
- very broad and generic
- already uses the same compare-alone-vs-group move that later lessons keep repeating
- does not set up a strong evidence ladder for the rest of the unit

### Lesson 2 — `gr3-ls2-unit4-lesson2-lookout-calls-and-warning-signals.html`
**Disposition:** keep topic, rebuild completely

What works:
- warning behavior is a real survival mechanism
- signal spreading could support a strong evidence lesson

What is weak:
- the current simulation is still just another "group helps survival" restatement
- no stronger measurement demand than Lesson 1
- worksheet and reasoning load are nearly identical to surrounding lessons

### Lesson 3 — `gr3-ls2-unit4-lesson3-schooling-flocking-and-moving-together.html`
**Disposition:** keep topic only if rebuilt; otherwise merge/replace

What works:
- schooling/flocking is a distinct phenomenon worth teaching
- predator-confusion is a real mechanistic idea

What is weak:
- currently feels too similar to Lesson 2 structurally
- same compare frame, same evidence shape, same written output
- does not force students to reason about *why this behavior works differently* from warning signals

### Lesson 4 — `gr3-ls2-unit4-lesson4-working-together-to-find-food.html`
**Disposition:** keep concept, rebuild around measurable output

What works:
- finally changes the survival problem from danger to food
- cooperation for food is a useful lane for the unit

What is weak:
- still uses the same explanation template as Lessons 1–3
- the activity is too generic to feel like a real step up
- should require stronger data collection such as time, amount collected, or success rate

### Lesson 5 — `gr3-ls2-unit4-lesson5-parents-babies-and-group-protection.html`
**Disposition:** retire from sequencing and replace

Why it fails:
- overlaps too much with Grade 1 `gr1-ls3-unit2-*` parent/offspring protection content
- feels like a sideways revisit rather than a Grade 3 lift
- reuses the exact same reasoning move as the rest of the unit
- weakens the unit's identity by drifting from group-behavior mechanisms into familiar parent-care territory

Bottom line:
- this slot should be replaced with a lesson that creates a new reasoning demand, not a softer replay of earlier-grade offspring protection content

### Lesson 6 — `gr3-ls2-unit4-lesson6-model-how-group-behavior-helps-survival.html`
**Disposition:** keep capstone role, rebuild after the middle lessons are fixed

What works:
- capstone role is correct
- modeling several behavior types belongs at the end

What is weak:
- the capstone is doing too much rescue work because Lessons 2–5 do not truly escalate
- asks for multi-example synthesis without enough differentiated evidence work earlier in the unit

## Repetition / weak-value patterns

1. **Lessons 1–4 all make the same claim at the same depth**  
   The organism examples change, but the unit keeps asking students to say the same sentence in slightly different words.

2. **Activity progression is weak**  
   The middle lessons all use nearly the same workflow: observe example -> compare group vs alone -> record benefit -> explain survival.

3. **Experiment differentiation is weak**  
   The lessons do not meaningfully escalate from noticing to measuring to modeling to arguing.

4. **Lesson 5 is a cross-grade duplication risk**  
   Grade 1 already covers parent/offspring protection. Grade 3 should not spend a prime unit slot retelling that idea without a much sharper mechanism or evidence demand.

## Cross-grade progression note

### Compared with Grade 1
Reference points:
- `lesson-plans/gr1-ls3-unit2-lesson3-parents-protect-offspring.html`
- `lesson-plans/gr1-ls3-unit2-lesson4-parents-teach-offspring.html`

Current problem:
- Grade 3 Lesson 5 does not feel sufficiently more advanced than Grade 1's parent-care work
- the unit as a whole does not create a clear jump from "parents help babies" to "students can analyze different group-behavior mechanisms with evidence"

### Bridge to later grades
A strong Grade 3 behavior unit should help students get ready for later ecosystem/population reasoning by making them track:
- what problem a behavior solves
- what evidence supports that claim
- how different behaviors solve different challenges

Current unit mostly stops at: "group = safer/helpful."
That is too shallow for the slot.

## Sharp overhaul plan

### Recommended target arc

**Lesson 1 — What Counts as Group Behavior?**
- keep as intro
- job: sort behaviors by purpose and establish the unit question
- key gain: clean starting point instead of a generic repeated claim

**Lesson 2 — How Fast Can a Warning Spread?**
- replace current light simulation with a timed signal-spread investigation
- job: introduce measurable evidence
- key gain: students compare response times and use data, not just opinion

**Lesson 3 — Why Is a Group Harder to Target?**
- rebuild schooling/flocking around predator-confusion or target-selection evidence
- job: explain a mechanism
- key gain: distinct reasoning from Lesson 2

**Lesson 4 — When Does Cooperation Improve Food Success?**
- rebuild around measurable success: speed, amount gathered, or task difficulty
- job: apply comparison to a new survival problem
- key gain: shifts from danger to resource gathering with real output data

**Replacement Lesson 5 — Match the Behavior to the Survival Challenge**
- retire the current parent/babies page from sequencing
- new job: compare multiple behavior types and defend which one best fits a given challenge
- possible organizer: challenge -> likely behavior -> evidence -> why it fits better than another behavior
- key gain: gives the unit a real pre-capstone synthesis lesson instead of a Grade 1 replay

**Lesson 6 — Model and Defend How Group Behavior Helps Survival**
- keep capstone role
- job: synthesize and defend with evidence from the rebuilt unit

## Calvin judgment summary

- Lesson 1: salvageable intro
- Lesson 2: worthy topic, current page too repetitive
- Lesson 3: worthy topic, current page too repetitive
- Lesson 4: salvageable only if rebuilt around measurable cooperation evidence
- Lesson 5: retire and replace
- Lesson 6: keep as capstone after rebuild

## Priority order

1. treat the whole unit as **overhaul lane**, not minor polish lane
2. replace Lesson 5 outright
3. rebuild Lessons 2–4 so each one uses a different evidence move
4. only then re-check Lesson 6, because the capstone depends on that middle-unit repair
