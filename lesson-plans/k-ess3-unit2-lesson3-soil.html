🪱 Soil — What's Under Our Feet? 🪱

Earth Science — Earth & Human Activity | Unit 2, Lesson 3

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🌍 Subject: Earth Science — Earth & Human Activity 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🌱 🪱 🪨 🍂 💧
Healthy soil is alive — it's full of rocks, minerals, organic matter, water, air, and living creatures!
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-ESS3-1 — Use a model to represent the relationship between the needs of different plants or animals (including humans) and the places they live.

🎯 The Big Idea

Soil isn't just dirt — it's one of Earth's most important natural resources! Healthy soil is full of tiny rock particles, organic matter (dead leaves and animals that have broken down), water, air, and living things like worms, bacteria, and fungi. Without good soil, plants can't grow — and without plants, animals (including us!) can't eat. Soil is the foundation of life on land.

🔬 The Science

Soil has four main ingredients:

  • 🪨 Minerals — tiny bits of broken-down rock. These give soil its texture (clay, silt, sand).
  • 🍂 Organic matter (humus) — decomposed plants and animals. This gives soil its dark color and most of its nutrients.
  • 💧 Water — fills the spaces between particles. Plants absorb water through their roots from soil.
  • 💨 Air — also fills spaces between particles. Roots and soil organisms need oxygen to survive.

Healthy soil absorbs and holds water well, provides nutrients plants need, and supports a diverse community of organisms. Sandy soil has large particles with big spaces — water drains through it fast. Healthy loamy soil has a mix of particle sizes and lots of organic matter — it holds water and nutrients much better.

💡 Key vocabulary: soil, organic matter, mineral, humus, sand, clay, decompose, nutrients, absorb, earthworm

📦 Materials

🏠 For the Soil Investigation

  • Outdoor soil or potting soil (1–2 cups)
  • Sand from a playground, sandbox, or garden store
  • Magnifying glass
  • Two clear cups or containers
  • 1 cup of water
  • White paper or paper plate (to examine soil on)

🧰 Optional

  • Spoon or stick for digging and stirring
  • Ruler (to measure water drainage)
  • Tray or newspaper (to keep things tidy!)

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Look Closely at Soil (5–8 min)
Spread a small amount of outdoor or potting soil on a white piece of paper. Use a magnifying glass to look at it closely. Ask your students: "What do you see?"
  • Dark bits? → Organic matter (old plants and animals that broke down)
  • Tiny rocks or pebbles? → Mineral particles
  • Worms or tiny bugs? → Living organisms!
  • Threads or stringy bits? → Fungal threads called hyphae
Pick up a pinch of soil. Does it feel gritty? Smooth? Sticky when wet?
Step 2 — Compare Potting Soil and Sand (5 min)
Put some potting soil in one cup and sand in another. Compare them side by side:
  • Which is darker? (Soil — because of organic matter)
  • Which feels richer and clumpier? (Soil)
  • Which smells more earthy? (Healthy soil has a distinctive smell from bacteria!)
  • Which would you rather grow a plant in? Why?
Step 3 — Water Absorption Test (8–10 min)
Set up the experiment:
  1. Put a cup of potting soil in one container.
  2. Put a cup of sand in another container.
  3. Slowly pour half a cup of water over each.
  4. Watch! Which absorbs the water better? Which lets it drain through right away?
Result: Healthy soil absorbs and holds water much better than sand. This is why sandy soil isn't great for most plants — the water (and nutrients!) drains away before roots can absorb it.
Step 4 — Student Worksheet (10 min)
Students draw what they see in the soil (observation illustration) and answer questions about the absorption test. They also draw or label the 4 ingredients of soil.
Step 5 — Discussion (5 min)
Use the discussion questions below. Connect to why healthy soil matters for animals and people.

🌱 Healthy Potting Soil

  • Dark brown color
  • Earthy smell
  • Holds water well
  • Has nutrients
  • May have worms!
  • Plants grow well in this

🏖️ Sandy Soil

  • Light tan/beige color
  • Minimal smell
  • Water drains right through
  • Low nutrients
  • Few organisms
  • Hard for most plants to grow

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • What is soil made of? Can you name the 4 things?
  • Why do plants need good soil? What happens if soil is poor or gone?
  • Which absorbed water better — soil or sand? Why does that matter for plants?
  • If there were no soil, what would happen to the animals that eat plants?
  • How do worms help soil? (They break down dead things and mix the soil!)
💡 Suggested answer — why healthy soil matters: Plants need soil to anchor their roots, absorb water, and get nutrients. Without healthy soil, plants can't grow. Without plants, animals that eat plants (like rabbits, deer, and insects) can't survive. Without those animals, predators that eat them can't survive either. Everything connects back to soil! That's why soil erosion (washing away of soil) and soil pollution are serious environmental problems.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🪱 Worm observation: Find an earthworm after rain. Watch how it moves. Worms eat dead plant material, pass it through their bodies, and their castings (poop!) are rich nutrients for soil.
  • 🌱 Grow experiment: Plant the same seed in potting soil and in sand. Water both equally. Which grows better over 2 weeks?
  • 🌿 Compost connection: Start a simple compost jar with vegetable scraps. Over weeks, watch the scraps break down into rich organic matter — just like nature makes soil!
  • 📚 Read aloud: "Dirt: The Scoop on Soil" by Natalie Rosinsky or "Jump Into Science: Dirt" by Steve Tomecek

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Safety: Wash hands thoroughly after handling outdoor soil. Some soils may contain fertilizers or other chemicals — potting soil from a bag is safest for indoor work.
  • If you can't get outdoor soil: Potting soil from any garden center works perfectly. Compare it with play sand (also at garden centers).
  • The absorption test: If using cups, make sure both cups have the same amount of soil/sand and you pour the same amount of water. The difference will be obvious — soil soaks it up, sand lets it pool and drain.
  • Connection to K-ESS3-1: Soil is where plants live and grow. Animals depend on plants for food and habitat. Healthy soil = healthy ecosystems = healthy animals (including humans). This is the "relationship between needs and places they live."

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Soil's 4 Ingredients (Part 3 Answers)

  • 🪨 Minerals — tiny broken-down rock particles (gives soil its texture: gritty, smooth, or clay-like)
  • 🍂 Organic matter (humus) — decomposed plants and animals (the dark bits; full of nutrients)
  • 💧 Water — stored in pores between particles (plants absorb this through roots)
  • 💨 Air — fills remaining pore spaces (roots and soil organisms need oxygen)

Water Absorption Test (Part 2 Answers)

  • Which absorbed water better? → SOIL. Organic matter acts like a sponge, holding water in the spaces between particles.
  • Which is better for growing plants? → SOIL. Holds water + nutrients that plants need.
  • Why? → Sandy soil has large, uniform particles with big gaps — water drains right through before roots can absorb it. Healthy soil has a mix of particle sizes + organic matter that holds water.
