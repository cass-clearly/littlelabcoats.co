The Big Idea

A plant does not finish the reproduction job when a flower is pollinated. Seeds must be protected, and many plants benefit when seeds spread away from the parent plant.

Seeds and fruits are strong examples of structures that support reproduction and survival at the same time. They protect offspring and increase the chance that new plants can grow.

The Science

Seeds contain the beginning of a new plant and a protective outer covering. Fruits often surround seeds and can help with dispersal when animals eat them, carry them, or when the fruit helps the seed travel another way. A seed’s structure and a fruit’s structure both support the success of the next generation of plants.

Key vocabulary: seed, fruit, offspring, protection, dispersal, germination, coat, reproduction

Materials

Gather Before You Start

  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • seeds, fruit, or seed photos
  • paper and pencil
  • optional: apple, bean, berry, or dandelion examples

Let's Get Started

Step 1: Show a few seed and fruit examples and ask what clues suggest these structures are helping the next generation.
Step 2: Explain that seeds protect the young plant, while fruits often help move seeds to new places.
Step 3: Compare examples that use animals, wind, or other methods to spread offspring.
Step 4: Have students identify one protective feature and one dispersal-supporting feature.
Step 5: Finish with a short explanation of how seeds and fruits support reproduction and survival.

Discussion Questions

Q: Why is seed protection important?A young plant needs protection before it can grow.
Q: Why might a plant benefit if seeds spread away from the parent plant?Less competition for space, light, water, and nutrients.
Q: How can a fruit help reproduction?By protecting seeds or helping them travel.

Extensions

Cut open a fruit and inspect where the seeds are stored. Then compare that example to a dry seed or a wind-dispersed seed picture and discuss how each structure helps the next generation.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: This lesson should connect clearly back to the Unit 2 reproduction thread. Keep the focus on structures helping offspring success, not on a broad ecosystem survey.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: Student wording may vary if the structure-function science is correct.

  • What Success Looks Like: Student explains that seeds protect new plants and fruits can help with protection or dispersal.
  • Acceptable examples: A seed coat protects the young plant; fruit can attract animals that move seeds; moving seeds away from the parent can reduce competition.
  • Writing support: Strong responses mention offspring, the next generation, or reduced competition instead of only saying seeds “go somewhere else.”
  • Coach for clarity: Encourage students to mention offspring or the next generation, not just “plants like fruit.”
