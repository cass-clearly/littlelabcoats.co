🎯 Learning Objective

Students will support an argument that plants get the materials they need for growth chiefly from air and water using multiple pieces of evidence.

💡 The Big Idea

A strong scientific argument states the claim, uses evidence, and explains why the evidence supports it.

🔬 The Science

The standard asks students to support an argument. That means making a clear claim and backing it up with investigations and models.

The best arguments in this unit combine evidence of water movement, carbon dioxide input from air, soil’s limited role, and the plant matter model.

🧪 Culminating Task

Step 1 — Gather evidence
Review the unit’s strongest evidence pieces.
Step 2 — State the claim
Write the argument in one clear sentence.
Step 3 — Add evidence
Use at least two pieces of evidence from the unit.
Step 4 — Add reasoning
Explain how the evidence supports the claim.
Step 5 — Revise for precision
Check for the word chiefly and accurate roles for soil and sunlight.

💬 Discussion Questions

What made your evidence strong?It came from an investigation, model, or clear comparison.
Why must the argument mention chiefly?Because the standard is about the main sources, not the only sources.
What reasoning connects the evidence?Water and air enter plants as matter inputs; soil does not chiefly supply the plant body.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample final argument

Plants get the materials they need for growth chiefly from air and water. Our colored-water test showed that water enters and moves through the plant. Our model showed that leaves take in carbon dioxide from the air. Soil helps support the plant and provides minerals, but it is not the chief source of the plant’s growth matter.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student states the claim accurately.
  • Student uses multiple relevant pieces of evidence.
  • Student reasons clearly about the roles of air, water, sunlight, and soil.
