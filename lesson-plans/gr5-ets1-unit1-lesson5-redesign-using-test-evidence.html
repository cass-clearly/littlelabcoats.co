Redesign Using Test Evidence

Engineering — Clean Water Design Lab | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 50–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Clean Water Design Lab
🔁📈💡
Redesign should be purposeful and evidence-driven.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem that reflects a need or a want, includes specified criteria for success and constraints on materials, time, or cost; generate and compare multiple possible solutions; and plan and carry out fair tests with variables controlled.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will redesign a filter prototype by choosing one or two specific changes supported by test evidence from version 1.

💡 The Big Idea

Strong redesign is not random. Engineers use evidence from a fair test to decide what to change and why.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

Redesign is most powerful when students connect each change to a specific problem revealed during testing. If version 1 was clear but too slow, students might reduce one dense layer. If version 1 flowed quickly but stayed muddy, students might add a finer layer.

Students should avoid changing everything at once. Strategic redesign allows engineers to learn which changes actually improved performance.

Grade 5 students can begin to justify redesign choices in terms of trade-offs and evidence quality.

📋 Printables

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review version 1 evidence: Highlight the weakest criterion and the evidence that revealed it.
Step 2 — Choose targeted changes: Students decide on one or two changes that directly respond to the weak point.
Step 3 — Predict the effect: Students explain how each change should improve the design and what trade-off might appear.
Step 4 — Update the plan: Label the redesign as version 2 and clearly mark what changed.
Step 5 — Prepare for retesting: Students make sure the next test will still be fair and comparable.

Discussion Questions

Why is changing everything at once a weak redesign strategy?You cannot tell which change caused the improvement.
What evidence tells you the design should be changed?Look back at weak performance data or observations.
How can a redesign create a new trade-off?Improving one criterion may reduce another.
Why should version 2 still be tested fairly?Without a fair comparison, you cannot trust the result.

Extensions

Extension: Ask students to rank proposed redesigns from strongest evidence-based choice to weakest.

Extension: Have students write a short claim-evidence-reasoning paragraph about one redesign decision.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Some students want to rebuild from scratch. Redirect them to purposeful iteration tied to evidence.

Upward-rigor move: Ask students to predict not only how version 2 will improve but what new weakness might appear.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Biggest Weak Point — Sample Use

Accept responses tied to evidence such as muddy output, slow flow, leaks, or structural instability.

2️⃣ Version 2 Change Plan — Sample Ideas

Students may add a finer layer, reorder layers, widen the opening, or strengthen support if they explain the expected effect.

3️⃣ How Will You Keep the Next Test Fair? — Strong Response

Students should name the same dirty-water mixture, same amount, same pour method, and same scoring system.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student ties redesign decisions to actual version 1 evidence.
  • Student proposes purposeful changes instead of random ones.
  • Student protects fairness for the next comparison test.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If students propose many unrelated changes, ask which one is most directly connected to the weak point.
  • If redesign just says “make it better,” prompt for the exact part and exact reason.
