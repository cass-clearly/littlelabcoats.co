✏️ Draw Your Idea

Engineering Design — Build It! | Unit 1, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🔧 Subject: Engineering Design — Build It! 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
✏️ 📐 🌉
Real engineers draw their ideas before they build. A good sketch saves time, materials, and mistakes!
📋 NGSS Standard: K-2-ETS1-2 — Develop a simple sketch, drawing, or physical model to illustrate how the shape of an object helps it function as needed to solve a given problem.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Create a labeled sketch of a solution design
  • Label each part of their design and explain what it does
  • Explain their design to a parent/teacher before building

🔬 The Big Idea

Real engineers don't just start building and hope for the best. They draw their design first — this is called a blueprint or engineering drawing. By drawing first, engineers can:

  • See if their idea makes sense before wasting materials
  • Explain their idea to others and get feedback
  • Plan exactly what they need to build
  • Notice problems before they become expensive mistakes
🏛️ Real Engineering Fact: Before the Golden Gate Bridge was built, engineers spent years drawing and planning. The design went through dozens of revisions on paper before a single piece of steel was put in place. Drawing first saved millions of dollars and thousands of hours of work!
💡 Key vocabulary: sketch, blueprint, label, design, materials, structure, function

📦 Materials

🖊️ For Drawing (Required)

  • Paper (plain or the worksheet in this lesson)
  • Pencil
  • Crayons or colored markers

🌉 For Building (Optional — if time allows)

  • 3–5 sheets of plain copy paper
  • 12 inches of tape (masking or clear)
  • 8–10 toothpicks (adult supervision required — tips are sharp)
  • Small toy car OR a marble
  • A tray, container lid, or baking sheet filled with 1 inch of water (the "puddle")

🏆 The Challenge

🌉 Design a bridge over a puddle that a toy car can drive across!

Materials: Paper, tape, and toothpicks only. Bridge must span a small tray of water.

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review the Challenge (5 min)
Re-read the challenge. Ask the Lesson 2 questions first:
  • How wide is the puddle? (Measure the tray/container)
  • How heavy is the car/marble?
  • What materials do we have?
  • What does the bridge HAVE to do? (Not touch the water, hold the car, let the car cross)
Write down the answers — these are your criteria (what the bridge must do) and constraints (what you're limited to).
Step 2 — What Makes a Good Bridge? (5 min)
Look at (or think about) real bridges. What shapes do they use? Key observations:
  • Bridges have a flat surface for things to cross on
  • Bridges have supports underneath or on the sides to keep them up
  • Bridges have to span the gap without touching the middle
Ask: "If you were making a bridge from paper, what would you do to make it strong?"
💡 Paper Engineering Hint: Flat paper is weak. But folded paper is surprisingly strong! Try folding paper into a long tube, an accordion fold, or a box shape. The shape changes how strong it is.
Step 3 — DRAW First! (10–12 min)
No touching the materials yet! Students draw their bridge design on the worksheet.

Their drawing should include:
  • A top view OR a side view of the bridge (or both!)
  • Labels for each part: "this is the road surface," "these are the supports," "this is where the car enters"
  • Notes on materials: "this part is paper," "this part is tape"
It doesn't have to be perfect — just clear enough that someone else could understand their plan.
Step 4 — Explain Your Design (5 min)
Each student shows their drawing to a parent/teacher/sibling and explains:
  1. What is this part? (Point to each labeled section)
  2. What does it do? (How does it help the bridge work?)
  3. Why did you choose this design?
This is what real engineers do in "design reviews" — you explain your plan to others to catch problems before you build!
Step 5 — Build! (Optional — if time allows)
If there's time, build the bridge using only the allowed materials. Save the original drawing — you'll need it for Lesson 4 to compare your design vs. your actual bridge!

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • What did drawing help you figure out that you wouldn't have noticed just by thinking?
  • Did your drawing change your plan at all? (It often does!)
  • When you explained your design, did the other person spot any problems you missed?
  • Can you think of things that need to be designed before they're built? (A house, a car, a toy…)
  • Why do labels matter on an engineering drawing?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • The drawing does NOT have to be good art: Engineering drawings are about communication, not beauty. A rough sketch with clear labels is better than a beautiful drawing with no labels.
  • Enforce the rule: draw before touching materials. If students rush to build, gently stop them: "Real engineers always draw first. Let's see your plan."
  • The "design review" in Step 4 is powerful: When students explain to an adult, they often notice gaps in their plan themselves. Let them talk it through without fixing it for them.
  • Keep the drawing! Students will compare it to their actual bridge in Lesson 4. Having both documents side-by-side is a great learning moment.
  • Assessment: Does the student have a drawing with at least two labeled parts? Can they explain what each part does? That's success for Lesson 3.

📋 Challenge Notes — Expected Answers

  • What must the bridge do: Span the puddle without touching water / support the toy car / let the car roll across smoothly
  • What must it NOT do: Touch the water / collapse under the car's weight / tip to one side

🎨 Design Drawing Rubric

No single correct design! Evaluate the drawing based on:

  • ✅ Drawing shows a recognizable bridge shape (something that spans a gap)
  • ✅ At least two parts are labeled with what they do
  • ✅ Materials are noted somewhere on the drawing or parts list
  • ✅ Student can explain each part verbally during Step 4

💡 Common Strong Design Elements to Look For

  • Folded paper for the deck (makes it stiffer than flat paper)
  • Supports along the sides or underneath
  • Toothpicks used as support beams or railings
  • Tape used at joints to strengthen connections

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Drawing was completed BEFORE touching building materials
  • At least two parts are labeled with their function
  • Student can explain their design to an adult
