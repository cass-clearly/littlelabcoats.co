🏛️ Who Runs Your City? 🏛️

All About Mayors — The Leaders of Our Cities & Towns!

📚 Grade: 4th ⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes 🏛️ Subject: Social Studies — Civics & Government
Meet the mayor — the person in charge of making your city a great place to live!
📋 Standards Alignment:
C3 Framework D2.Civ.1.3-5 — Distinguish the responsibilities and powers of government officials at various levels
C3 Framework D2.Civ.2.3-5 — Explain how a democracy relies on people's responsible participation
C3 Framework D2.Civ.6.3-5 — Describe ways people can act to influence their local government

🎯 The Big Idea

Every city and town in the United States has a leader — and that leader is called the mayor! The mayor is kind of like the principal of a city. Just like your principal makes decisions about your school, the mayor makes decisions about your whole city. And here's the coolest part: regular people get to vote to choose who the mayor is! 🗳️

💡 Key vocabulary: mayor, election, candidate, campaign, ballot, vote, city council, government, citizen, community

🤔 What IS a Mayor?

Think of it this way: If your city were a school...
🏫 The principal = the mayor
👩‍🏫 The teachers = the city council (they work with the mayor)
📋 The school rules = the city laws (called ordinances)
🧒 The students = the citizens (that's YOU and your family!)

A mayor is the person elected to lead a city or town government. They are the top decision-maker for the community. Most mayors serve for 4 years at a time (called a "term"), and then there's another election!

🌟 Fun Fact: The youngest mayor in US history was Bobby Tufts — he became mayor of Dorset, Minnesota at just 3 years old in 2013! (It was a small town with a fun tradition of picking a name from a hat.) 🎩

📋 What Does a Mayor DO All Day?

Being a mayor is a BIG job! Here are some of the things mayors do:

🚒 Keep people safe: The mayor works with the police department and fire department to make sure your city is a safe place to live.
🛣️ Fix roads & bridges: Potholes? Broken sidewalks? The mayor helps decide which roads get repaired and when.
🏞️ Build parks & playgrounds: Want a new splash pad or skate park? The mayor helps decide where parks get built!
💰 Manage the city's money: Every city has a budget (like an allowance, but MUCH bigger!). The mayor helps decide how to spend it — on schools, roads, libraries, and more.
🤝 Listen to people: Citizens come to the mayor with problems and ideas. A good mayor LISTENS and tries to help!
📢 Be the city's spokesperson: When something big happens — a storm, a celebration, or a new business coming to town — the mayor speaks for the whole community.
💡 Think about it: If YOU were mayor, what's the FIRST thing you'd want to fix or build in your city? 🤔

🗳️ How Does Someone BECOME Mayor?

Voting is how we choose our leaders — every vote counts!

Mayors aren't born into the job — they're elected! That means the people of the city get to CHOOSE who they want to lead them. Here's how it works:

Step 1 — Decide to Run! 🏃
Someone in the community says, "I want to make my city better — I'm going to run for mayor!" This person is now called a candidate.
Step 2 — Campaign! 📣
The candidate tells everyone their ideas. They make posters, give speeches, knock on doors, and explain WHY people should vote for them. This is called a campaign.
Step 3 — Election Day! 🗳️
On a special day, adult citizens go to a polling place and cast their vote — they pick which candidate they think would be the best mayor.
Step 4 — Count the Votes! 📊
After everyone votes, the votes are counted. The candidate with the most votes wins! That's democracy in action!
Step 5 — Inauguration! 🎉
The new mayor takes an oath (a special promise) to do their best for the city. Then they get to work!

🏛️ Mayors, Governors & Presidents — What's the Difference?

The United States has three levels of government, like layers of a cake! Each level has its own leader:

Government works in layers — from your city all the way up to the whole country!
🏘️City / TownLed by the MAYOR
🗺️StateLed by the GOVERNOR
🇺🇸Country (Federal)Led by the PRESIDENT
🏘️ Mayor → Takes care of ONE city or town (roads, parks, police, fire dept.)
🗺️ Governor → Takes care of a whole STATE (state highways, state parks, state laws)
🇺🇸 President → Takes care of the entire COUNTRY (national defense, dealing with other countries)
💡 Easy way to remember: Mayor = My city. Governor = my state (Got the whole state!). President = the whole Place (the whole country!). Each one is in charge of a BIGGER area! 🎯

🎨 Activity: Run for Mayor!

🗳️ Design Your Campaign — Head-to-Head!

You're BOTH running for mayor of your town — against each other! Each of you will create a campaign, then hold a debate and election. May the best candidate win! 🏆

Part 1 — Campaign Poster 🖼️
On a separate piece of paper (or the back of your worksheet), design a campaign poster! Include:
  • Your name and a catchy slogan (example: "Vote for Maya — She'll Make Our Parks Amazing!")
  • A drawing of yourself looking like a leader
  • At least 3 promises of things you'd do as mayor
  • Bright colors and bold letters so people notice it!
Part 2 — Campaign Speech 🎤
Write a short speech (3–5 sentences) telling your family why they should vote for you. Use the space on the activity page! Remember to include:
  • What problem in your town you want to fix
  • Your plan to fix it
  • Why YOU are the best person for the job
Part 3 — The Great Debate! 🎙️
Now face off! Take turns answering these questions (1 minute each):
  • "What's the #1 problem in our town and how would YOU fix it?"
  • "Why should people vote for YOU instead of your opponent?"
  • "What's one thing you'd build or create for the community?"
After the debate, have a parent (or even a pet 🐕) cast the deciding vote!

💬 Discussion Questions

🔵 4th Grade Discussion
  • Why is it important for citizens to vote in elections?
  • What qualities make a GOOD mayor? (Think about what makes a good leader!)
  • If you could ask your city's mayor ONE question, what would it be?
  • Why do you think mayors only serve for a few years before there's a new election?
  • What's one thing in your neighborhood or town that you wish the mayor would change?
  • How is being a mayor similar to being a class president? How is it different?
  • Why do we need DIFFERENT leaders for cities, states, and the whole country? Why not just one person in charge of everything?

🚀 Extensions

  • 🔎 Research Your Mayor: Find out who the mayor of YOUR city is! What's their name? How long have they been mayor? What are they working on?
  • 📰 Mayor in the News: Find a news article about a mayor making a decision. What did they decide? Do you agree with it?
  • 🎭 Mock Election: Hold a family election! Both students give speeches, then a parent (or siblings, stuffed animals, or pets!) act as voters. Use secret ballots — fold your votes so nobody peeks! Count them together.
  • ✉️ Write to Your Mayor: Write a real letter to your city's mayor about something you'd like to see changed. Most mayors LOVE hearing from kids!
  • 🗺️ Government Scavenger Hunt: Find the names of your mayor, your governor, AND the president. Bonus: find the vice president and your city council members!

👩‍🏫 Parent / Teacher Notes

  • Struggling readers: Read sections aloud together — take turns! The "school analogy" (mayor = principal) is a powerful anchor — keep returning to it.
  • For the campaign activity: Give both kids 15–20 minutes. Play upbeat music! Have them present their speeches to each other and to you — it builds confidence.
  • Real-world connection: Look up your city's mayor's office website together. Show kids it's a REAL person with a REAL job.
  • ⚠️ Common misconception: Kids often think the president is in charge of everything. Emphasize that mayors handle LOCAL issues — the stuff that affects their daily life most directly!
  • Differentiation: For students who need more challenge, ask them to compare mayor-council vs. council-manager forms of city government.
  • Keep it nonpartisan: Focus on the ROLE and PROCESS of being mayor, not specific political parties or current political debates.
  • Assessment: The matching exercise and fill-in-the-blank on the activity page make great formative assessments.

🤔 Short Answer — Example Responses

These are sample answers — accept any reasonable response that shows understanding!

1. Why is it important for a mayor to listen to the people in their city?
Because the mayor works FOR the citizens. If they don't listen, they won't know what problems need fixing. Citizens know their neighborhoods best — a good mayor uses that knowledge to make better decisions for everyone.
2. What's the difference between what a mayor does and what a governor does?
A mayor takes care of one city — like fixing local roads, managing the city's police and fire departments, and building parks. A governor takes care of a whole state — like state highways, state parks, and state laws that apply to every city in the state.
3. A mayor wants to build a new park but there isn't enough money. What should they do?
They could: raise money through fundraisers, apply for a grant, ask the city council to adjust the budget, ask citizens to volunteer to help build it, or wait and save up money over time. They should also ask citizens if a park is what they want most!
4. Why do we have elections instead of just letting someone be mayor forever?
Elections make sure the leader is doing a good job — if people aren't happy, they can vote for someone new. It also gives other people a chance to lead and bring new ideas. Without elections, a mayor might stop listening to what people want.
