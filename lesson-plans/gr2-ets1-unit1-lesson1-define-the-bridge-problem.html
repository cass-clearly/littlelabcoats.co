Define the Bridge Problem

Engineering — Bridge Builders | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade⏱️ Duration: 40–50 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Bridge Builders
🌉🤔🧱
Engineers start by naming the gap, the user, and what a successful bridge must do.
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-2-ETS1-1 — Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will define a simple bridge-design problem by naming who needs the bridge, what the bridge must cross, and what success should look like.

💡 The Big Idea

A strong bridge design begins with a clear problem. Engineers need to know who the bridge is for, what gap it must cross, and what the bridge must do before they start building.

🔬 The Science

Bridges help people, animals, or vehicles cross over something safely. A bridge might cross water, a road, or a deep space between two places.

In this unit, students will design a small model bridge for a toy animal to cross between two tables or stacks of books. The bridge must stay up, reach across the gap, and hold a small weight.

Lesson 1 is about the problem, not the building. When students clearly name the user, the gap, and the success rules, they make smarter choices later.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Launch the Challenge: Show two books with a small space between them. Explain that a toy animal needs a bridge to get safely across.
Step 2 — Name the User and Need: Ask who needs help and what the bridge must help the toy do. Keep the problem concrete and visible.
Step 3 — Set Success Rules: Generate simple criteria such as reaches across the gap, stays up, and holds the toy animal without falling.
Step 4 — Notice Constraints: Discuss limits: only simple household materials, bridge must fit the gap, and building time is short.
Step 5 — Write the Problem Statement: Students complete a sentence frame naming the user, the need, and what success means.

Discussion Questions

Why do engineers name the problem before they build?Look for: it helps them know what the design must do.
Who is the user in our bridge challenge?Look for: the toy animal or traveler trying to cross.
What does the bridge have to do to be successful?Look for: reach the gap, stay up, and hold the toy.
Why are limits like materials and time important?Look for: real designs must work within limits.

Extensions

Extension: Look around your neighborhood or books for different real bridges and ask what each one is helping people cross.

Extension: Use blocks or cups to create a wider and narrower gap, then predict which one would be harder for a bridge.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Students often jump straight to “make it tall” or “make it pretty.” Redirect them to what the bridge must do first.

Teaching note: Keep the challenge small and concrete so the criteria feel reachable for Grade 2 students.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Define the Challenge — Sample Use

Accept answers that clearly name the toy animal/user and the gap the bridge must cross.

2️⃣ What Must the Bridge Do? — Sample Ideas

Strong rules include reaching the gap, staying balanced, and holding the toy or a few pennies.

3️⃣ Write a Problem Statement — Model Response

“I need to design a bridge that helps a toy fox cross a gap between two books so it can get to the other side safely.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student names a clear user and gap.
  • Student lists at least two useful success rules.
  • Student writes a bridge problem statement that makes sense.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student focuses on colors or decorations, ask what the bridge must do first.
  • If the problem statement is vague, bring the toy and gap back into view and restate the need out loud.
