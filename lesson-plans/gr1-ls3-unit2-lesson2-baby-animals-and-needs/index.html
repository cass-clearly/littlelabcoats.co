🐣🐾🦆 Baby Animals and Their Needs

Parents and Offspring Unit, Lesson 2

📚 Grade: 1st Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science 📋 NGSS Standard: 1-LS3-1
🐣🐾🦆
Baby animals need the same things adult animals need. But they often need a lot more help getting them!
📋 NGSS Standard:
1-LS3-1 — Make observations to construct an evidence-based account that young plants and animals are like, but not exactly like, their parents.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Identify that baby animals have the same basic needs as adults: food, water, shelter, and safety.
  • Recognize that parents help meet their offspring's needs, especially when babies are young and vulnerable.
  • Observe that some baby animals look very different from their parents (metamorphosis), yet still share the same needs.

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need (Household Only)

  • Paper and crayons
  • Optional: a nature documentary description from the parent (no screen required)

✅ All household items. Nothing to purchase.

📖 Vocabulary

🍎 Needs
Things a living thing must have to survive (food, water, shelter, safety).
🐣 Offspring
A baby animal (the young of a parent).
🐻 Parent
The adult animal that cares for the offspring.
🏠 Shelter
A safe place to live and stay protected.

📚 Background (Read Aloud to Student)

Every living animal needs four basic things to survive: food, water, shelter, and safety. This is true for adult animals, and it is just as true for baby animals. In fact, baby animals need the same things, but they usually cannot get those things by themselves. That is where parents come in!

Most baby animals are born or hatched without the ability to find their own food, stay warm, or protect themselves from predators. A newborn kitten cannot hunt for mice. A newly hatched duckling cannot fly away from danger. These babies depend on their parents to meet their needs until they are old enough to care for themselves.

Here is something interesting: some baby animals look almost nothing like their parents. A tadpole does not look like a frog. A caterpillar does not look like a butterfly. These animals go through an amazing process called metamorphosis, where their body completely changes shape as they grow. But even though they look so different, they still have the same basic needs. A tadpole still needs water, food, and safety to survive, just like the frog it will become.

🦋 Fun fact! The word "metamorphosis" comes from the Greek words for "change" and "form." A caterpillar changes its form to become a butterfly. This is one of the most dramatic changes in the animal kingdom!

🔬 Procedure

Step 1 — The Big Four
Before starting the drawing activity, discuss the 4 basic needs together: food, water, shelter, and safety. Ask: "Do you need these things? Does a cat? Does a tadpole?" Establish that all animals share these needs.
Step 2 — Baby Animal Match 🎨
Fold a piece of paper into 5 sections (fold it like a book and then cut or fold again). In each section, draw the baby animal AND its adult form as a pair:
  • 🐱 Kitten and Cat
  • 🐶 Puppy and Dog
  • 🐥 Duckling and Duck
  • 🐸 Tadpole and Frog
  • 🦋 Caterpillar and Butterfly
Step 3 — Label the Needs
For each animal pair, write or dictate the answer to two questions: "What does the baby need?" and "How does the parent help meet that need?" Keep answers simple. One or two words per need is fine.
Step 4 — Circle the Shape-Changers
Look at all five pairs. Circle the ones where the baby looks VERY different from the adult. (Tadpole/frog and caterpillar/butterfly should be circled.)
Step 5 — The Big Question
Discuss together: "Even though a tadpole looks nothing like a frog, does it still have the same needs?" Work toward the answer: yes! The body may look different, but the needs stay the same.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: Which baby animals look the most different from their parents?

Tadpoles look nothing like frogs, and caterpillars look nothing like butterflies. These animals go through a big change called metamorphosis. The puppy, kitten, and duckling look much more like their parents right away.

🗣️ Question 2: Why do tadpoles and caterpillars look so different from their parents?

These animals go through metamorphosis, a process where their body completely changes shape as they grow. A tadpole is built for swimming in water, while the frog it becomes is built for both land and water. The body changes to match a new way of living.

🗣️ Question 3: Do baby animals that look different from their parents still have the same needs?

Yes! Even though a tadpole looks different from a frog, it still needs water, food, and safety to survive. The shape of the body changes, but the basic needs of any living animal stay the same.

🗣️ Question 4: How do parent animals help their babies survive?

Parents provide food, warmth, protection from predators, and shelter. Without parental help, many baby animals would not survive long enough to grow up. A duckling stays close to its mother for warmth and protection for weeks before it can fend for itself.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Nature documentary clips: If you have access to nature content (YouTube, library DVDs, or a streaming service), clips of a mother duck with ducklings or a cat nursing kittens make this lesson come alive. However, the lesson works just as well with parent narration and imagination.

Explaining metamorphosis simply: You can say: "Some animals have two completely different bodies during their life. The caterpillar body is for eating and growing. Then it wraps itself in a cocoon and its body completely rebuilds itself into a butterfly body that is built for flying and finding mates. It is like the animal has a first life and a second life."

Extension activity: Help your student draw a lifecycle diagram for the frog (egg, tadpole, froglet, frog) and the butterfly (egg, caterpillar, chrysalis, butterfly). These are excellent visual summaries of metamorphosis and make a great addition to the student's science notebook.

The difference between tadpoles and frogs: Tadpoles breathe through gills (like fish) and live entirely in water. As they develop into froglets and then frogs, they grow legs, lose their tail, and develop lungs. This is complete metamorphosis. Point out that while the BODY changes, the NEED for food, water, and safety never changes.
