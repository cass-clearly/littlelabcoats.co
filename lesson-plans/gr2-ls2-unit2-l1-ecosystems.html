🌿 What Is an Ecosystem?

Grade 2 Life Science, Unit 2 — Lesson 1

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Ecosystems 🌱 Unit: 2-LS2 Ecosystems
🌳🐛☀️💧🌱
Living and nonliving things working together — that is an ecosystem!
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-LS2-1 — Make observations of plants and animals to compare the diversity of life in different habitats.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify the living and nonliving parts of an ecosystem
  • Understand that living things depend on their environment to survive
  • Recognize that all parts of an ecosystem are connected

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

  • Paper and pencil (for the Ecosystem Map)
  • Green and blue crayons or colored pencils
  • Window or outdoor access (a few minutes of looking outside)
  • Magnifying glass — optional but fun!

✅ No specialty purchases needed — paper and crayons only!

📖 Vocabulary

Ecosystem All the living and nonliving things in one place, working together
Living Something that grows, breathes, and needs food and water
Nonliving Something that was never alive (rocks, water, sunlight)
Habitat The place where an animal or plant lives and finds what it needs
Environment Everything that surrounds a living thing: air, water, soil, other living things
Depend To need something else in order to survive

🌍 Background

An ecosystem is made up of all the living and nonliving things in one place. Trees, insects, birds, and soil bacteria are living parts. Rocks, sunlight, water, and air are nonliving parts. Everything in an ecosystem is connected — change one part and the whole ecosystem feels it.

Living things in an ecosystem depend on the nonliving parts to survive. Plants need sunlight and water to grow. Animals need clean air to breathe and fresh water to drink. When one part of an ecosystem changes (for example, if a drought stops the rain), all the living things are affected.

Ecosystems come in all sizes. A puddle can be a tiny ecosystem for bacteria and water insects. A rainforest is a giant ecosystem with millions of species. Even a backyard, a park, or a window box is an ecosystem you can explore today. No matter how small, every ecosystem has the same ingredients: living things, nonliving things, and the connections between them.

🔬 Activity: Ecosystem Map

In this activity, you will look carefully at the outdoor world around you and make a map of the ecosystem you can see.

Step 1: Look out a window or step outside for 5 minutes. Bring paper and pencil.
Step 2: Draw a simple map of what you can see — trees, sky, soil, grass, sidewalk, birds, insects, rocks, puddles, and sunlight. It does not need to be perfect — just get everything you notice onto the paper.
Step 3: Go back through your drawing and circle every living thing in green. Circle every nonliving thing in blue.
Step 4: Count how many living things you found and how many nonliving things. Write the numbers at the bottom of your map.
Step 5: Look at your map again. Draw a dotted arrow from one living thing to a nonliving thing it depends on. For example: draw an arrow from a tree to the sunlight, or from a bird to a tree.
Step 6: Share your map and discuss: "What surprised you about how many connections you found?"

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: If the rain stopped falling for an entire year, which living things would be most affected first?

Suggested answer: Plants would be affected first because they need water to grow. Without plants, plant-eating animals would lose their food. Then meat-eating animals would lose their food too. The whole ecosystem would change, starting with the plants — they are the foundation of most ecosystems.

🗣️ Question 2: Is a rock a living or nonliving thing? How do you know?

Suggested answer: A rock is nonliving because it does not grow, breathe, eat, or reproduce. It was never alive. Soil is interesting though — it contains tiny living things called bacteria and fungi. The dirt itself is nonliving, but the organisms living inside it are alive. This shows that living and nonliving things are always mixed together.

🗣️ Question 3: Can a living thing survive without any nonliving things nearby?

Suggested answer: No! Every living thing needs nonliving things. Plants need sunlight (nonliving), water (nonliving), and minerals in soil (nonliving). Animals need air (nonliving) and water (nonliving). Life and nonlife are always working together in every ecosystem on Earth.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • No materials to buy: Paper and crayons only. A magnifying glass adds excitement if you have one — even a cheap plastic one transforms the experience.
  • If outdoor access is limited: Look out a window, or describe the ecosystem of a houseplant (soil, sunlight from window, the plant itself, and any bugs you might find). Even a kitchen windowsill is an ecosystem.
  • Connection to prior learning: Students may have learned about animal needs (food, water, shelter, air). This lesson zooms out to show HOW animals get those needs met — from their ecosystem.
  • Differentiation: Younger learners can sort picture cards into "living" and "nonliving" piles. Advanced learners can research a specific ecosystem (desert, ocean, rainforest) and compare it to their backyard.
  • This lesson is the foundation for the whole unit. Spend extra time on the idea that everything is connected. The Ecosystem Map activity makes that connection visible and personal.
  • Common misconception: Students sometimes think soil is nonliving. Soil contains billions of living microorganisms! The mineral particles are nonliving, but a teaspoon of healthy soil contains more living things than there are people on Earth.
