Construct an Argument: How Animal Structures Help Them Live

Grade 4 · Unit 1
A strong science argument does more than list facts. It makes a claim and supports that claim with evidence from observed structures and their functions.
Key vocabulary
argumentclaimevidencereasoning
What to notice
  • Review the unit categories: finding food, movement, protection, and sensing the world.
  • Model a short argument using one animal: “A duck’s webbed feet help it swim, so that structure supports survival by helping it move through water.”
  • Ask students to gather evidence from at least three animals before writing.
Materials snapshot
  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • notes or examples from Lessons 1–5
  • pencil or colored pencils
  • optional animal image set for evidence support
Talk-about prompts
  • What is the difference between a claim and evidence?
  • Why is one example usually weaker than several examples?
  • How can a structure support behavior or reproduction, not just survival?
Why it matters

The performance expectation for this domain asks students to construct an argument from evidence. In elementary science, that means making a clear claim, using accurate examples, and explaining how the examples support the claim. Students are not expected to write formal essays, but they should connect evidence to reasoning.

Try this next: Turn the final argument into a mini poster with one central claim and three evidence boxes. This makes revision visible and helps families see what a science argument looks like at home.
