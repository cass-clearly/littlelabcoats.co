Reducing Weather-Related Hazards

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 6 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
🛠️🌧️🏡
A strong claim explains why one solution has more merit for reducing a weather hazard.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS3-1 — Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will make and support a claim about which design solution has the most merit for reducing a weather-related hazard.

💡 The Big Idea

A strong science-and-engineering claim does more than name a favorite design. It explains why one solution better reduces the hazard and what evidence supports that choice.

🔬 The Science

Merit means a solution is worth choosing because it meets the problem well. To show merit, students should connect the hazard, the design, and the evidence that the design reduces the impact.

This final lesson brings the unit together: weather patterns help us understand local conditions, hazards show why a problem matters, and design comparison helps us defend which solution is best.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review the hazard and solutions (5 min)
Remind students what problem they are trying to reduce and which design ideas they compared.
Step 2 — Choose the strongest solution (10 min)
Students decide which design has the most merit for the chosen hazard.
Step 3 — List evidence and reasons (10 min)
Students write what the solution does and why that matters.
Step 4 — Turn the reasons into a claim (10 min)
Students write a complete explanation using evidence and merit language.
Step 5 — Share and revise (10 min)
Students listen for missing evidence and improve their claim before finishing.

Discussion Questions

What makes one design solution have more merit than another?It reduces the hazard better and fits the criteria more closely.
Why is evidence important when defending a solution?Evidence turns the claim into something justified instead of only opinion.
How does this lesson connect to weather and hazard learning earlier in the unit?Students use what they learned about patterns, hazards, and impacts to defend a design.
Why should students revise a claim after sharing?Revision helps them notice missing evidence or weak reasoning.

Extensions

Extension: Add one improvement to your chosen design and explain whether it would increase the solution’s merit.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep the claim tied to the specific hazard and local conditions instead of letting students write a generic “this is good” answer.

Important misconception to catch: Students may think merit means “most expensive” or “biggest.” Keep the focus on reducing the actual hazard well.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Name the Problem — Sample Acceptable Answers

Students should identify a real weather-related hazard and the impact they want to reduce, such as flooding near a building or heat affecting people outdoors.

2️⃣ Choose the Best Solution — Sample Idea

A strong answer explains how the design reduces the specific impact, not just that it “looks helpful.”

3️⃣ Make a Claim — Sample Response

“This design solution has merit because it moves floodwater away from the building and reduces damage during heavy rain.”

4️⃣ Quick Revision Check — Sample Use

Students should use the checklist to notice whether the claim names the hazard, explains the impact reduction, and includes evidence.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student makes a clear merit claim.
  • Student connects the solution to a specific hazard and impact.
  • Student supports the claim with evidence and reasoning.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student writes “It is the best” with no reason, ask “Best for what problem?”
  • If the claim sounds generic, ask the student to add how the design reduces the impact of the specific hazard.
