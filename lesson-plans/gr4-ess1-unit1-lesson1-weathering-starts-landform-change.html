🪨 Weathering Starts Landform Change

Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

Grade: 4Duration: 45–55 minutesSubject: Earth Science, WeatheringMaterials: Sugar cubes or chalk, droppers, tray
NGSS Standard: 4-ESS2-1 — Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define weathering as the breaking of rock into smaller pieces.
  • Compare how rubbing, dripping water, and shaking can change a model rock.
  • Use observations as evidence to explain how weathering starts landform change.

📦 Materials

  • 3 sugar cubes, chalk pieces, or very dry crackers
  • Tray or plate to catch crumbs
  • Water and eyedropper or spoon
  • Small jar or cup with lid
  • Pencil and worksheet

📖 Vocabulary

landformA natural shape on Earth’s surface, such as a hill, canyon, or plain
weatheringThe process that breaks rock into smaller pieces
mechanical weatheringRock breaking apart without changing into a new substance
sedimentLoose pieces of broken rock, sand, or soil
evidenceObservations or measurements that support an explanation

🔬 Background

Landforms may look still, but Earth’s surface is always changing. One of the first steps in that change is weathering. Weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces. Over time those pieces can become gravel, sand, or soil.

Weathering can happen in many ways. Water can seep into cracks and slowly widen them. Wind-blown particles can scrape rock surfaces. Plant roots can push into cracks. Ice can expand in cracks and pry them open. Even though each event may be small, the pattern repeats over and over.

In Grade 4, we care about the evidence for change. If a rock becomes crumbly, cracks deepen, or more sediment appears after a test, those observations support the explanation that weathering is happening.

🧪 Activity

Step 1: Put three sugar cubes, chalk pieces, or crackers on a tray. Label them A, B, and C on your worksheet.
Step 2: Test A for rubbing weathering. Rub it gently between your fingers for 15 seconds over the tray. Record how much sediment you see.
Step 3: Test B for water weathering. Place it on the tray and drip 10 drops of water onto the same spot. Watch for softening, cracking, or collapse.
Step 4: Test C for collision weathering. Put it in a small jar and shake for 10 seconds. Pour it back onto the tray and observe how many pieces formed.
Step 5: Compare the three model rocks. Which test produced the most new sediment? Which produced the clearest cracks or crumbling?
Step 6: Write a short claim using evidence from your observations: “Weathering changes rock because …”

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why does weathering matter even before erosion begins?

Hint: Weathering makes the loose pieces that erosion can later move.

Q2: Why can a very slow process still create huge changes to a landform?

Hint: Small changes repeated for years add up to major change.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Easy substitution: Chalk works especially well if sugar cubes are not available.
  • Upward-rigor move: Push students to cite the exact observation that counts as evidence, not just say “it changed.”
  • Link forward: Remind students that weathering breaks rock, but the next lesson focuses on what happens when water or wind moves that broken material.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Observation table: Answers vary. Look for accurate notes such as crumbs formed, edges softened, or pieces broke off.
  2. Most change: Accept any answer supported by evidence from the student’s own test.
  3. CER statement: Strong responses explain that weathering breaks rock into smaller pieces that can later form soil or sediment.
  4. Before/after drawing: The “after” drawing should show cracking, crumbling, or smaller pieces.
