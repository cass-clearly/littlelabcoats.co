Atmosphere + Hydrosphere Weather Clues

Grade 5
Weather clues often come from interactions between water at Earth’s surface and gases in the air above it.
NGSS anchor

5-ESS2-1: Develop a model using an example to describe ways Earth systems interact. In this lesson, the example is hydrosphere water evaporating into the atmosphere and condensing into visible droplets.

What to notice
  • Droplets appear on the cool cover, not because water leaked through.
  • Warm water supplies water vapor to the air space.
  • Cooling changes invisible vapor back into liquid water.
  • Moving air can change where warmth, moisture, or droplets are noticed.
Materials snapshot
  • Clear jar or cup
  • Warm tap water
  • Plate, plastic wrap, or bag cover
  • Ice cubes
  • Dark paper/towel
  • Timer, pencil, worksheet
Talk-about prompts
  • Which observation was your strongest evidence?
  • Where was the hydrosphere in the model?
  • Where was the atmosphere?
  • How does this help explain clouds, dew, fog, or rain?
Use it to explain

A strong Grade 5 explanation says: Water from the hydrosphere evaporated into the atmosphere. When that water vapor cooled, it condensed into droplets. This is one way Earth systems interact to make weather clues.

Family bridge: After the lesson, look for one real-life clue — dew, foggy windows, clouds, puddles drying, or rain — and ask, “Where is the hydrosphere-atmosphere interaction?”
