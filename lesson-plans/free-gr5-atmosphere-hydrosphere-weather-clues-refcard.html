Atmosphere + Hydrosphere Weather Clues

Grade 5
Students compare two weather models, track evidence over time, and explain why cooling turns invisible water vapor into visible droplets.
NGSS anchor

5-ESS2-1: Develop a model to describe ways Earth systems interact. In this lesson, students model water moving from the hydrosphere into the atmosphere and condensing into visible droplets.

Key vocabulary
hydrosphere atmosphere evaporation condensation variable evidence
Model sequence
💧Warm water
hydrosphere
🌫️Water vapor in air
atmosphere
🌦️Cooled droplets form
condensation clue
What to notice
  • The ice-cooled lid usually makes droplets appear faster.
  • Warm water supplies vapor to the air above it.
  • Droplets are evidence that vapor cooled and condensed.
  • Airflow can change how heat and moisture move through the model.
Scientific moves
  • Identify the changed variable.
  • Compare Jar A and Jar B at the same times.
  • Use observations as evidence, not just vocabulary.
  • Name one limitation of the model.
Strong discussion prompts
  • How do you know droplets did not leak through the lid?
  • Why does cooling matter so much?
  • What real weather clue works the same way?
  • What can this model not explain?
Sample explanation

Water from the hydrosphere evaporated into the atmosphere part of the model. The colder lid cooled that water vapor, so it condensed into droplets. That interaction helps explain weather clues like dew, fog, clouds, and precipitation.

Family bridge: After the lesson, go outside or look through a window and find one weather clue—dew, clouds, fog, rain, or drying puddles. Ask: Where is the atmosphere-hydrosphere interaction happening here?
