🗺️ Maps and Directions

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 4 of 6

Grade: 2 Duration: 45 minutes Subject: Earth Science, Geography Skills Materials: Paper, pencil, crayons, ruler
NGSS Standard: 2-ESS2-2 — Develop a model to represent the shapes and kinds of land and bodies of water in an area.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Understand that maps show Earth's features from a bird's eye (top-down) view
  • Create a simple map with a compass rose, legend/key, and map symbols
  • Use a map to navigate through a space

📦 Materials

  • Paper (one large sheet per student, or use notebook paper)
  • Pencil
  • Crayons or markers
  • Ruler (optional)

📖 Vocabulary

map A drawing that shows an area from above, like a bird looking down
compass rose A symbol on a map that shows North, South, East, and West
legend A key that explains what the symbols on a map mean
symbol A small drawing or shape that represents something real on a map
bird's eye view Looking at something from directly above, as if you were flying

🔬 Background

A map is a drawing that shows what an area looks like from directly above, as if you were a bird flying overhead. Maps can show any size area, from your bedroom to the whole planet. Scientists who make maps are called cartographers. Maps have been used for thousands of years to help people navigate, find resources, and understand the world.

All maps have some basic features. A compass rose shows the four directions: North, South, East, and West. A legend (also called a key) explains what the symbols on the map mean. Symbols are small drawings or shapes that represent real things, like a blue line for a river, a green square for a park, or a small house shape for buildings. Using symbols makes maps easier to read and understand than drawing detailed pictures of everything.

Maps are very useful for studying Earth's features. Topographic maps show hills and mountains using curved lines (contour lines). Political maps show country and state borders. Physical maps show landforms and bodies of water with colors (brown for mountains, blue for water, green for flat land). Reading maps is a scientific skill that geographers, explorers, engineers, and scientists all use every day.

🧪 Activity: Room Map

Step 1: Walk around the room (or yard) you will map. Note the main features: doors, windows, furniture or large objects, any special areas.
Step 2: Start with a large sheet of paper turned sideways (landscape). Decide which side of your paper will be "North" and lightly write N at the top.
Step 3: Draw the outline of the room as a simple rectangle or shape. Include the walls.
Step 4: Add a compass rose in one corner (N, S, E, W pointing in the right directions).
Step 5: Draw the main features: at least one door, one window, and 3 pieces of furniture or major objects. Use simple shapes and symbols.
Step 6: Create a legend/key box in a corner: list each symbol and what it means (example: rectangle = table, X = door, square with circle = chair).
Step 7: Add a title to your map ("My Bedroom," "Our Backyard," etc.).
Step 8: Trade maps with a family member. Can they follow your map to find a specific object you point to? Can you follow theirs?

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: How is a map different from a photograph of the same room?

Hint: A photo shows what things look like from the side: you can see colors, textures, and heights. A map shows where things are in relation to each other from directly above. Maps leave out detail but make it easier to understand the layout and navigate. Pilots, ship captains, and hikers all need maps more than photos because maps show position.

Q2: Why do mapmakers use symbols instead of drawing everything in detail?

Hint: If you drew every tree as a real tree picture, the map would be too crowded and hard to read. Symbols are simple and quick to understand once you know the legend. They also save space. All maps use the same standard symbols by agreement (blue = water, for example) so people everywhere can read maps from any place.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Scale is not required: The map does not need to be perfectly to scale. The goal is understanding the concept of bird's eye view and the use of symbols, not precise measurement.
  • Estimation is fine: If measuring is difficult, encourage estimation: "The table is about halfway across the room." That is more than enough for 2nd grade.
  • Make it interactive: When trading maps with a family member, one person holds the map while the other walks around following it. This makes the activity memorable and fun.
  • Cross-curricular connection: This lesson directly supports understanding of physical geography maps, which appear in many social studies curricula at the same grade level.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. My Map: Accept any top-down drawing of a room or yard that includes walls/boundaries, some features inside, a compass rose, a legend, and a title. The map does not need to be to scale.
  2. Map Checklist: All five items should be checked. If any are missing, have the student add them to their map.
  3. Fill in the blank: symbol
  4. Quick quiz: water (accept also: oceans, rivers, lakes)
