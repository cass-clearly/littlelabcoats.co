💧 Erosion Moves Sediment

Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

Grade: 4Duration: 50 minutesSubject: Earth Science, ErosionMaterials: Pan, soil or sand, water, ruler
NGSS Standard: 4-ESS2-1 — Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define erosion as the movement of sediment from one place to another.
  • Measure how far sediment moves in two model streambank tests.
  • Explain why surface cover such as “roots” or plants can change erosion rate.

📦 Materials

  • Baking pan or tray
  • 2–3 cups sand or soil
  • Water in a cup or squeeze bottle
  • Ruler
  • Toothpicks, grass clippings, or short paper strips to model roots
  • Pencil

📖 Vocabulary

erosionThe movement of sediment by water, wind, ice, or gravity
rateHow fast something happens
runoffWater that flows across the land surface
vegetationPlants growing in an area
measurementA number-based observation used as evidence

🔬 Background

After weathering produces sediment, that sediment can be moved. This movement is called erosion. Water is one of the strongest erosion agents because it can pick up loose material and carry it downhill.

The amount of erosion depends on patterns such as water speed, slope, and surface cover. Bare soil usually erodes faster than soil held together by roots. That is why grasses, shrubs, and trees often help keep streambanks and hillsides stable.

Scientists do not just say “more erosion happened.” They look for evidence such as deeper channels, more exposed soil, or measurements showing that sediment traveled farther in one test than another.

🧪 Activity

Step 1: Build two small, equal hills or streambanks in the same tray. Label one Bare Soil and the other Rooted Surface.
Step 2: Press toothpicks, grass, or paper strips gently into the second hill to model plant roots holding soil together.
Step 3: Predict which hill will lose more sediment and explain why.
Step 4: Pour the same amount of water from the same height onto each hill.
Step 5: Measure how far the sediment traveled from each hill. Record the distance and what the surface looked like afterward.
Step 6: Compare the two results. Use your measurements and observations to explain which surface had the faster erosion rate.

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why is a measurement more useful than saying “that one looked worse”?

Hint: Measurements make comparisons clearer and easier to support with evidence.

Q2: How can vegetation protect land even during heavy rain?

Hint: Roots hold soil, and stems or leaves slow down moving water.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Fair-test reminder: Keep the water amount and pouring height the same.
  • If the surfaces collapse: That still counts as evidence; students can explain why loose material moved so quickly.
  • Link forward: Ask where the moved sediment might end up. That leads naturally into deposition.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Prediction: Most students should predict the bare soil will erode faster, but any prediction is acceptable if reasoning is included.
  2. Data table: Distances vary. Strong evidence includes longer sediment travel, deeper channels, or more collapsed edges on the bare surface.
  3. Compare the rate: Students should use both a measurement and a visual observation.
  4. Short explanation: Strong responses explain that vegetation or “roots” slow the movement of sediment and reduce erosion rate.
