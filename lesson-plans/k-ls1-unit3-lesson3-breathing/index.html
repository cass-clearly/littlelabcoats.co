🫁 Breathing: Lungs, Gills, and More 🐟

Life Science — Animal Needs  |  Unit 3, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Animal Needs
🧍💨 🐟🌊
All animals need oxygen to survive — but not all animals breathe the same way!
📋 NGSS Standard: K-LS1-1 — Use observations to describe patterns of what plants and animals (including humans) need to survive.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will IDENTIFY that all animals need oxygen and DESCRIBE the difference between lungs and gills with at least one example of each.

🔬 The Big Idea

Every animal alive needs oxygen — it's one of the most important things on the Animal Survival Checklist! But here's the cool part: animals get oxygen in different ways depending on where they live.

🫁 Lungs
Breathe air
Humans, dogs, birds, whales
🐟 Gills
Extract oxygen from water
Fish, tadpoles, crabs
🐸 Both!
Gills as babies, lungs as adults
Frogs, salamanders
💡 Key Vocabulary: lungs, gills, oxygen, breathe, inhale, exhale

📦 Materials

🏠 All From the Kitchen — No Purchases Needed!

  • Clear glass or jar
  • Water
  • Food coloring (any color)
  • Turkey baster (best tool!) or a straw for gentle bubbling
  • Timer or clock
  • Paper and crayons

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Feel Your Lungs! (5 min)
Have your child place one hand flat on their chest. Take a big, slow, deep breath in.
"Do you feel that? That's your lungs expanding — filling up with air!"
Now exhale slowly. Feel the chest fall.
Use a timer: count how many breaths they take in one minute. Write the number down!
(Typical kindergartner: about 20–30 breaths per minute at rest.)
Step 2 — How Lungs Work (3 min)
Explain: "When you breathe in, air rushes into your lungs. Your lungs grab the oxygen out of the air and send it into your blood. Then you breathe out the parts your body doesn't need."
Practice the vocab together: inhale (breathe in) and exhale (breathe out).
Step 3 — Set Up the Fish Breathing Model (5 min)
Fill the clear glass with water. Add one drop of food coloring and stir gently — the color helps you see the water moving.
"Fish can't breathe air like we do. They breathe water! Their gills pull oxygen right out of the water as it flows through."
Hand your child the turkey baster. Show them how to:
  1. Squeeze the bulb and dip it in the water
  2. Release slowly — the water is pulled IN (like a fish's mouth opening)
  3. Squeeze again — the water is pushed OUT (like water flowing over gills)
Watch the colored water move! "That flowing water is how a fish gets its oxygen."
💡 Straw alternative: If no turkey baster, use a straw to gently stir and circulate the colored water — this shows how water flows over gills. (Avoid blowing out through the straw, which models exhalation rather than gill function. The goal is to show water moving over the gill surface to extract oxygen.)
Step 4 — Compare Side by Side (5 min)
"Let's think about this together."
  • Our lungs pull oxygen out of air → we breathe air in and out
  • Fish gills pull oxygen out of water → water flows in through the mouth and out over the gills
They do the same job — just with different stuff!
Ask: "Have you ever seen a fish's mouth moving in a fish tank or at the store? That's the gills working!"
Step 5 — Bonus: Spiracles! (2 min)
"Here's a wild bonus fact. Insects — like ants, grasshoppers, and beetles — don't have lungs OR gills! They breathe through tiny little holes in the sides of their bodies called spiracles. Isn't that amazing?"
Let them search for a bug outside (or look at a picture) and imagine where those tiny holes might be.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: "Could a fish survive out of water? Why or why not?" Give them a moment to think before sharing the hint below.

Suggested Answer: A fish can't survive out of water because its gills only work in water — they need water flowing over them to get oxygen. It's like if we tried to breathe underwater without any equipment — our lungs need air, not water!

🗣️ Question 2: "Can you think of an animal that can breathe BOTH in water and on land?" Hint: Think about an animal that starts life in a pond and grows up to hop on land…

Suggested Answer: Frogs! Tadpoles breathe with gills, but adult frogs breathe with lungs AND can absorb some oxygen right through their damp skin. Turtles and crocodiles also breathe air but spend lots of time in water.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🐠 Fish Tank Watch: If you have a fish tank at home, observe the fish's mouth and gills moving — that's real breathing!
  • 🐸 Frog Life Cycle: Draw or look up the frog life cycle. When does it switch from gills to lungs?
  • 🔍 Bug Hunt: Go outside and find an insect. Explain to it (yes, really!) why its spiracles are cooler than lungs and gills.
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "What Do Animals Need?" or any book about ocean animals and how they breathe.
  • 🎨 Animal Sort: Draw or find pictures of animals and sort them: lungs, gills, or both!

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • All materials from the kitchen — no purchases needed. The turkey baster is the best tool because pulling and pushing the water is very tactile, but a straw works fine (gently blow into the colored water).
  • Fun connection: If they've ever seen a fish's mouth moving in a tank or at the store, point out that those are the gills at work — the mouth opens to let water flow in, and it exits over the gills.
  • Don't skip the spiracles! Kids love the "no lungs, no gills, just holes" fact about insects. It broadens the concept beyond the binary.
  • Frog is the star of the bonus: If your child is fascinated by frogs, this is a great segue into metamorphosis — coming up later in the unit!
  • Vocab to reinforce: lungs, gills, oxygen, breathe, inhale, exhale, spiracles
  • If they have a fish tank at home: this is a perfect time to pause and observe real gills in action before or after the activity.
  • Assessment check: Can the child explain that all animals need oxygen? Can they name at least two ways animals breathe? That's success!

💬 Discussion Answer Guide

Q1: Could a fish survive out of water?
No — gills only work when water is flowing over them. Out of water, gills can't grab oxygen and the fish can't breathe. Compare: it's like a person trying to breathe underwater — our lungs are built for air, not water.

Q2: An animal that breathes BOTH in water and on land?
Best answer: Frogs. Tadpoles use gills in water → adult frogs use lungs on land and can absorb oxygen through moist skin. Accept any reasonable answer (turtles, crocodiles, salamanders).

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Child can state that all animals need oxygen (air).
  • Child can name lungs and gills as two ways animals breathe.
  • Child can correctly fill in: lungs breathe air, gills breathe water.
  • Child shows arrows on drawings pointing in the correct directions.
  • Bonus: Child names a frog (or similar) as an animal that can do both.

🔢 Breath Count Reference

Typical resting breath rate for kindergartners: 20–30 breaths per minute. Any answer in this range is normal and healthy. If they count more after jumping around — great observation to discuss!
