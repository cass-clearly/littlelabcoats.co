📞 Design a Sound Sender 📞

Physical Science — Sound & Light  |  Unit 1, Lesson 6 of 6

📚 Grade: 1st Grade⏱️ Duration: 35–45 minutes🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Sound & Light
📞🧵👂
A sound sender works when vibrations can travel clearly from one place to another.
📋 NGSS Standards:
1-PS4-1 — Plan and conduct investigations to provide evidence that vibrating materials can make sound and that sound can make materials vibrate.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will DESIGN, TEST, and IMPROVE a simple sound-sending device and explain how vibrations helped the message travel.

💡 The Big Idea

Engineers can use what they know about vibrations to solve problems. If we want a quiet message to travel farther, we can design something that helps the vibration move clearly from one place to another. This lesson combines the unit ideas: sound starts with vibration, and sound can travel through matter.

💡 Key vocabulary: design, test, improve, vibration, sound sender, message, string, engineer

📦 Materials

🏠 Gather Before You Start

  • 2 paper or plastic cups
  • Long string or yarn (6–12 feet)
  • Tape
  • Scissors or a hole punch (adult use)
  • Optional: different strings, extra cups, paper clips

✏️ For the Worksheet

  • Printed Student Worksheet
  • Pencil or crayons

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Name the Problem (3 min)
Tell your child: "We want to send a quiet message to someone farther away without shouting. How could we do that?" Explain that today they will be engineers building a sound sender.
Step 2 — Build the First Model (10 min)
Help your child poke a small hole in the bottom of each cup. Thread the string through both cups and tie it inside. Pull the string tight. One person talks softly into one cup while the other listens at the other cup.
💡 Important test rule: The string should be pulled tight and should not touch the floor or table while testing.
Step 3 — Test What Works Best (10 min)
Try the sound sender with the string tight, then loose. If you have different materials, compare them. Ask: "When did the message travel best?"
Engineer Thinking:
What helps the vibration move better?
  • A tighter string?
  • A different string?
  • Standing farther or closer apart?
Step 4 — Improve the Design (8 min)
Change one thing and test again. Maybe use a different string, hold the cups differently, or make sure the string is straighter. Compare the new result to the first test. Ask: "Was the message clearer, louder, or easier to hear this time?"
Step 5 — Share Your Best Design (5 min)
Have your child explain what they changed and why they think it helped. Encourage them to use the word vibration in their explanation.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: "What part of the sound sender vibrated?"

💬 The cup and string vibrated. The voice made vibrations, and those vibrations moved through the string to the other cup.

🗣️ Question 2: "Why did the sound work better when the string was tight?"

💬 A tight string helped the vibration travel more clearly from one cup to the other.

🗣️ Question 3: "What change made your design better?"

💬 Answers will vary, but students should explain one improvement and how it helped the message travel.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🧵 Try different string lengths and compare.
  • 🏠 Test inside and outside.
  • 🧪 Compare yarn, string, ribbon, or fishing line if you have them.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • This is the engineering-style capstone for the Grade 1 sound unit, even though the formal engineering unit is separate. Students use science knowledge to solve a simple communication problem.
  • Ideal explanation: "The message traveled because my voice made vibrations and the string carried them."
  • Success bar: students do not need a perfect phone — they need a design, a test, and a simple improvement.

🔑 PARENT/TEACHER ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Part 1

Any recognizable cup-and-string sound sender drawing is acceptable.

Part 2

Accept simple changes such as tightening the string, standing closer, using a different string, or keeping the string off the floor. A strong response includes both the change and its effect, for example: "I pulled the string tighter, and the message sounded clearer the second time."

Part 3

  • My message traveled through the string.
  • I made my design better by pulling the string tight, keeping it straight, or another tested change.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Child can describe one test and one improvement.
  • Child can explain how the second version worked better or more clearly than the first.
  • Child can connect the design to vibration and sound travel.
