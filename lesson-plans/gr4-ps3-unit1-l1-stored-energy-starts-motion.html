Stored Energy Starts Motion

Physical Science — Energy & Motion | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Energy & Motion
A higher starting position can give a toy car more energy and help it move faster when released.
📋 NGSS Standard:
4-PS3-1 — Use evidence to construct an explanation relating the speed of an object to the energy of that object.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare a low ramp start and a high ramp start, then explain with evidence how a higher start can give the same object more energy and change its speed.

💡 The Big Idea

When the same object starts with more stored energy, it can move faster and travel farther. Scientists use fair comparisons to connect the object’s speed to the energy it had at the start.

🔬 The Science

Energy is the ability to make something happen. In this lesson, the toy car has stored energy when it begins higher up on the ramp. When the car is released, that stored energy helps start the car’s motion.

Grade 4 students do not need complicated math here. They need a strong cause-and-effect explanation: when the same car starts higher, it usually moves faster because it began with more energy. Evidence comes from what students see and measure in a fair comparison.

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Build one ramp, two start heights (5–10 min)
Set up one ramp with a low release point and a higher release point. Use tape to mark the two starting spots so the object can be released from the same places each time.
Step 2 — Test the low start first (10 min)
Release the car without pushing it. Watch how quickly it moves and where it stops or how far it rolls after leaving the ramp.
Step 3 — Test the higher start (10 min)
Keep the same car and ramp, but release the car from the higher mark. Compare the speed and distance with the low-start test.
Step 4 — Repeat and record evidence (10 min)
Repeat both starts at least twice. Students should record what stayed the same, what changed, and which start led to faster motion.
Step 5 — Explain the pattern (10 min)
Use evidence from the fair test to explain how the higher start gave the car more energy and changed its motion.

💬 Discussion Questions

What stayed the same in this fair test?Look for: the same car, the same ramp, and the same surface. Only the starting height changed.
What evidence suggests the higher start gave the car more energy?Look for: the car moved faster, rolled farther, or reached the end sooner when it started higher.
Why should you release the car instead of pushing it?A push would add another change and make it harder to tell whether the starting height caused the difference.
How does this lesson connect to motion?Energy at the start can change how fast an object moves and how much motion it has later.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Add a paper-cup target at the bottom of the ramp and compare which starting height makes a bigger motion change when the car reaches the target. This sets up the next lesson on collisions.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep the language concrete: higher start, faster motion, farther roll, same car, same ramp.

Misconception to catch: Students may say the higher start worked because you "pushed it more." Remind them that the release was the same; the stored energy came from the higher starting position, not from an extra hand push.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample observation pattern

Answers vary, but the same car should usually move faster or travel farther from the higher starting point than from the lower starting point.

What students should name in Part 2

  • Stayed the same: the same car, ramp, surface, and release method
  • Changed: the starting height on the ramp

Sample explanation

The high start gave the car more stored energy. My evidence is that the same car rolled faster and farther when it started higher on the same ramp. The car and ramp stayed the same, so the starting height was the important change. That evidence supports the idea that more starting energy changed the car’s speed.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student compares low and high starts using the same car and ramp.
  • Student names both an observation and a cause-and-effect explanation.
  • Student explains that the higher start, not an extra push, caused the speed difference.
