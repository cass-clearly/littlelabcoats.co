What Is an Ecosystem?

Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs
🌿🌎
An ecosystem is a system of living and nonliving parts working together.
📋 NGSS Standard:
5-LS2-1 — Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will describe an ecosystem as living and nonliving parts interacting together.

💡 The Big Idea

An ecosystem is not just a place with living things. It is a system where organisms and their environment affect one another.

🔬 The Science

An ecosystem includes organisms like plants, animals, and fungi, plus nonliving parts like water, soil, air, sunlight, and temperature.

Scientists study ecosystems by looking at interactions — who depends on what, where energy comes from, and how matter moves through the system.

🖼️ Ecosystem Study Cards

Use these four ecosystem cards as the main observation source for this lesson. They give students real scenes to study instead of asking them to guess from a manicured backyard.

Forest ecosystem card
Look for trees, fungi, animals, rocks, soil, shade, and sunlight.
Pond ecosystem card
Look for plants, animals, water, mud, sunlight, and shelter at the pond edge.
Desert ecosystem card
Look for cacti, animals, sand, rocks, sunlight, and places to hide from heat.
Tide pool ecosystem card
Look for seaweed, animals, salt water, rocks, shallow pools, and changing light.

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Sort specific ecosystem components from one ecosystem card (10 min)
Choose one ecosystem study card and sort what you actually notice into three buckets: living parts, nonliving parts, and interactions. For example, on the pond card a frog and cattails are living parts, water and mud are nonliving parts, and a frog hiding among plants is an interaction.
Step 2 — Use the card to find interactions (10 min)
Stay with the same ecosystem card and list at least three interactions you can point to on the card. Example: a squirrel uses a tree for shelter, pond plants grow in water and sunlight, or a tide pool crab depends on wet rocks and seawater. Reference the card as you talk instead of drawing a new picture here.

✏️ Model-planning help before students draw

Ecosystems they could draw:
forest, pond, desert, or tide pool
Helpful labels to include:
sunlight, water, soil, rocks, plants, animal names, shelter, and arrows showing one interaction
Step 3 — Build the model (10 min)
Students sketch one ecosystem model with labels. Give concrete examples of labels such as sunlight, water, rocks, soil, oak tree, frog, cactus, or seaweed. They may draw any of these ecosystems: forest, pond, desert, or tide pool.
Step 4 — Explain one interaction (10 min)
Choose one organism from the model and describe how it depends on the environment.
Step 5 — Share the system idea (10 min)
Students explain why the ecosystem works as a system, not as separate pieces.

💬 Discussion Questions

Why is sunlight part of an ecosystem even though it is not alive?Nonliving parts still affect living things.
How do living and nonliving parts interact?Plants, animals, and fungi depend on water, air, soil, and light.
Why is an ecosystem called a system?The parts affect one another.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Compare two ecosystems, such as pond and desert, and list what is the same and different.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Push students beyond naming objects. Ask how the parts are connected.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample Thinking

Plants need sunlight, water, and soil. Animals need food, water, and shelter. These interactions make the ecosystem a connected system.

Sample ecosystem choices and labels

Accept a forest, pond, desert, or tide pool model. Strong labels may include sunlight, water, soil, sand, rocks, seaweed, cactus, frog, fish, bird, shelter, or arrows showing one interaction.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student includes both living and nonliving parts.
  • Student uses one ecosystem card as evidence and can name what was actually visible there.
  • Student shows at least one real interaction.
  • Student includes concrete labels on the model instead of leaving the drawing unlabeled.
  • Student uses the word system accurately.
