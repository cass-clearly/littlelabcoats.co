⚖️ Balanced and Unbalanced Forces ⚖️

Physical Science — Forces & Motion | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes 🧲 Subject: Physical Science
↔️📦
When forces match, motion stays the same. When one force wins, motion changes.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-PS2-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence of the effects of balanced and unbalanced forces on the motion of an object.

💡 The Big Idea

Objects do not just move for no reason. A push or pull changes motion. If forces are balanced, they cancel out and the object does not change motion. If forces are unbalanced, the object's motion changes.

🔬 The Science

A force is a push or a pull. Forces can start motion, stop motion, speed something up, slow it down, or change its direction. Scientists compare what happens when forces are equal and when they are unequal.

When two forces are the same size but act in opposite directions, they are balanced. Balanced forces do not mean there are no forces. They mean the forces match. When one force is stronger, the forces are unbalanced, and the object's motion changes.

Balanced

Equal pushes or pulls cancel out. The object stays still or keeps moving the same way it already was.

Unbalanced

One force is stronger. The object starts moving, stops, speeds up, slows down, or changes direction.

💡 Key vocabulary:
force push pull balanced unbalanced motion evidence

📦 Materials

  • 1 small toy car, block, or smooth object that can slide
  • 1 flat table or floor surface
  • 2 index cards or stiff paper pieces
  • Masking tape
  • Pencil
  • Printed Student Worksheet

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Set Up a Fair Test (5 min)
Place the object on the same starting spot each time. Explain that good investigations keep most things the same so we can notice what changed.
Step 2 — Try a Balanced Push (8 min)
Use two index cards to push gently from opposite sides at the same time. Ask: "Did the object change motion? What do you notice?"
Step 3 — Try an Unbalanced Push (8 min)
Now make one push stronger than the other. Watch what happens. Ask: "Which force won? How did the motion change?"
Step 4 — Repeat and Record (10 min)
Do at least three trials: one balanced and two unbalanced. Record what happened each time on the Student Worksheet.
Step 5 — Explain with Evidence (10 min)
Use your observations to explain why the object stayed still in one test and moved in another. Push students to use the words balanced, unbalanced, and evidence.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: What happened when the pushes matched?Look for: The forces were balanced, so the object did not change motion.
🗣️ Question 2: What happened when one push was stronger?Look for: The forces were unbalanced, so the object moved or changed motion.
🗣️ Question 3: Why is it important to repeat the test?Look for: Repeating gives stronger evidence and helps us see whether the same pattern happens again.
🗣️ Question 4: Does balanced mean there were no forces?Look for: No. Balanced means the forces were equal and canceled each other out.

✨ Extensions

  • 🏒 Sports Spotting: Find a sports example of balanced forces and one of unbalanced forces.
  • 🧪 Change the Object: Repeat the test with an object that is heavier or rougher. How does that affect your results?
  • 📝 Evidence Sentence: Write one complete sentence using the words balanced, motion, and evidence.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Keep it visible: The goal is not precision physics. The goal is helping students notice whether motion changed or stayed the same.
  • Push observation language: Encourage words like stayed still, moved, changed direction, started moving, and slowed down.
  • Correct a common misconception: Students often think balanced means “nothing is happening.” Re-emphasize that balanced means the forces match.
  • Assessment check: A successful response explains both what happened and why the evidence supports balanced or unbalanced forces.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: This page is for parent/teacher reference. Student wording may vary if the science idea is correct.

Part 1

Answers vary. Balanced trials should show little or no change in motion. Unbalanced trials should show the object moving or changing motion.

Part 2

  • Balanced answer: the object stayed still / did not change motion.
  • Unbalanced answer: the object moved / changed motion.
  • Strong evidence statement: the student compares at least two trials and explains what stayed the same or changed.
  • Look for: observations from the chart, not just the vocabulary words by themselves.

What Success Looks Like

  • Student can distinguish balanced from unbalanced situations.
  • Student uses evidence from the tests rather than only repeating vocabulary.
  • Student can explain that balanced does not mean “no forces.”

Troubleshooting / Misconceptions

  • If the student says balanced means “nothing happened at all,” remind them that equal forces were still acting.
  • If a test result looks messy, focus on the pattern across repeated trials rather than one imperfect push.
