🏔️ Landforms All Around

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 1 of 6

Grade: 2 Duration: 45 minutes Subject: Earth Science, Earth Features Materials: Playdough, blue paper, toothpicks, crayons
NGSS Standard: 2-ESS2-2 — Develop a model to represent the shapes and kinds of land and bodies of water in an area.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Name and describe at least 4 common landforms (mountain, valley, plain, island)
  • Use playdough to build models that show the shape of different landforms
  • Compare where on Earth different landforms are found

📦 Materials

  • Playdough (store-bought, OR homemade: 2 cups flour, 1/2 cup salt, 3/4 cup water, 1 tbsp oil... mix until smooth)
  • Blue construction paper or a bowl of water (to represent the ocean/lake)
  • Small paper scraps and toothpicks or pencil tips for "flag" labels
  • Crayons or markers

📖 Vocabulary

landform A natural shape on Earth's surface, like a mountain or valley
mountain A very tall, steep rise of land with a peak at the top
valley A low area of land between hills or mountains
plain A wide, flat area of land
island A piece of land completely surrounded by water
peninsula A piece of land that sticks out into water, connected on one side

🔬 Background

Earth is not flat! The surface has many different shapes called landforms. A mountain is a tall, steep rise of land with a peak at the top. A valley is a low area of land between hills or mountains, often with a river running through it. A plain is a wide, flat area of land. An island is a piece of land completely surrounded by water.

Landforms are created by powerful forces inside and outside the Earth. Volcanoes push hot rock up to form new mountains. Moving plates of rock under Earth crack and fold the surface into ridges and valleys. Wind and water slowly wear away rock over millions of years, carving out canyons and valleys.

Maps show us where landforms are found. Scientists who study Earth's surface are called geographers. They make maps that show mountains with triangles, valleys with V shapes, and water with blue color. When you look at a globe, you can see the rough, bumpy shape of continents, islands, and ocean floors.

🧪 Activity: Landform Builder

Step 1: Look at photos of Earth (a globe, atlas, or the internet) and find one example of each: a mountain, valley, plain, and island. If a globe is not available, discuss what each looks like from memory.
Step 2: Set up your workspace with playdough and blue paper (or a bowl of water for the "ocean").
Step 3: Build a mountain: roll playdough into a cone shape, tall and peaked at the top.
Step 4: Build a valley: make two small hills side by side, then press a low curved channel between them.
Step 5: Build a plain: flatten a piece of playdough into a wide, flat shape.
Step 6: Build an island: shape playdough into a mound and place it in the "ocean" (blue bowl or on blue paper surrounded by blue space).
Step 7: Make small paper flags: cut tiny rectangles, fold them over a toothpick or pencil tip, and label each landform with its name. If that feels too fiddly, just write the labels on scrap paper and place them beside each model.
Step 8: Look at all 4 landforms together. Discuss: Which is tallest? Which is lowest? Which is surrounded by water?

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Where would you build a city, on a plain or in a valley? Why?

Hint: Plains make the easiest places to build cities because they are flat and wide with lots of room. Many of the world's biggest cities (like Chicago, Denver) are built on plains. Valleys can be good too because rivers often run through them, providing water, but they can flood!

Q2: What do you think would happen if you poured water at the top of your mountain model?

Hint: The water would run down the sides of the mountain and collect in the low valley below. This is exactly how rivers and streams form in real life: rain falls on mountains and flows downhill into valleys.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Homemade playdough recipe: 2 cups flour, 1/2 cup salt, 3/4 cup water, 1 tbsp oil. Add food coloring if desired. It lasts about a week in an airtight bag.
  • No playdough? Use rolled-up paper or pillows to model landforms conceptually.
  • Mastery check: This lesson is more about vocabulary and mental models than memorization. If a child can describe why a mountain is different from a plain in their own words, that is full mastery.
  • Preview: Connect to L3 (Water Shapes the Land) by asking: what happens when water hits your mountain? Preview erosion.

  3. Circle the right answer: (a) In a valley
  4. Draw a simple map: Accept any drawing that includes a triangular or peaked mountain shape, a low area for a valley, and a blue-colored area for the ocean. Labels are a bonus.
