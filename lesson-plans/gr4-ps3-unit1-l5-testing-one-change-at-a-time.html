Testing One Change at a Time

Physical Science — Energy & Motion | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Energy & Motion
📋🎯
Scientists learn more from motion tests when they change one variable and keep the rest controlled.
📋 NGSS Standard:
4-PS3-1 — Use evidence to construct an explanation relating the speed of an object to the energy of that object. / 4-PS3-3 — Ask questions and predict outcomes about the changes in energy that occur when objects collide.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will plan and run a fair Energy & Motion test that changes one variable and uses evidence to compare results.

💡 The Big Idea

A fair test changes one important variable at a time so the evidence is easier to trust and explain.

🔬 The Science

Scientists compare results by controlling as many parts of a test as possible. In this unit, students have already seen that starting speed, collisions, and surfaces can all matter.

Now they decide on one variable to test — such as ramp height, surface type, or collision target — while keeping the object and release method the same.

Grade 4 rigor means naming both the changed variable and the controlled parts of the setup.

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Choose one variable (10 min)
Select one thing to change: speed, surface, or collision setup.
Step 2 — Name the controls (10 min)
List what will stay the same in every test.
Step 3 — Run at least two trials (10 min)
Carry out the comparison and record what happened.
Step 4 — Look for a pattern (10 min)
Decide whether the changed variable affected the result.
Step 5 — Defend the fairness (10 min)
Explain why the test gives useful evidence.

💬 Discussion Questions

What variable did you change?Students should name one specific feature such as ramp height or surface type.
What did you keep the same?The same object, same release method, or same target can all be controls.
Why does fairness matter in science?It helps you trust that the changed variable caused the result.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Trade test plans with a partner or family member and check whether the plan really changes only one variable.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: If students want to change too many things, have them circle the one question they most want to answer first.

Misconception to catch: “More complicated” does not mean “more scientific.” Clear comparisons are stronger.
