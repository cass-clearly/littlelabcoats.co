Compare Shelter Materials and Shapes

Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design
📏🧱🌬️
Engineers compare materials and structures before choosing a plan.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem with criteria and constraints, generate and compare solutions, and plan fair tests with variables controlled and failure points considered.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare possible materials and shelter shapes and explain which combinations have the most merit for the challenge.

💡 The Big Idea

Comparing ideas before building helps engineers make stronger choices.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

Grade 4 students should compare more than one idea using evidence, not preferences alone. For a shelter challenge, material stiffness, water resistance, and shape all matter.

Materials

  • Printed lesson pages and worksheet
  • Cardstock, paper, foil, craft sticks, tape samples
  • Cup of water or spray bottle optional
  • Pencil

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Inspect candidate materials
Students bend, press, and lightly mist materials to compare their properties.
Step 2 — Compare shapes
Discuss flat roofs, slanted roofs, triangles, and box-like shapes.
Step 3 — Rank options
Students decide which materials and shapes seem strongest for wind and rain.
Step 4 — Explain trade-offs
A stronger material may be heavier; a lighter one may be less stable.
Step 5 — Record evidence
Students justify their best choices using observations.

Discussion Questions

Why might shape matter as much as material?Structure affects stability and how rain or wind is redirected.
What makes one material have more merit?It fits the criteria and constraints better.
Why compare before building?Comparisons reduce random guessing.
What counts as evidence in this lesson?Observed strength, stiffness, water resistance, and stability.

Extensions

Extension: Test a few household items and rank which would make better shelter walls or roofs.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Encourage students to use observed properties, not brand names or favorite colors.

Misconception to catch: The strongest-looking material is not automatically the best if it fails another criterion.

2️⃣ Which option has the most merit right now?

Name one material or shape. Describe one observed property and connect it to one shelter criterion such as staying upright, blocking rain, or fitting the constraints.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample ideas

Students may choose a slanted roof because water rolled off instead of pooling, or a triangular support shape because it stayed more stable during handling. Accept any option that is justified with a real observed property and a connection to the shelter criteria or constraints.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student compares more than one material or shape.
  • Student names an observed property such as stiffness, water resistance, or stability.
  • Student connects that property to a shelter criterion or constraint instead of choosing by preference alone.
