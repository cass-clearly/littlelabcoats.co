🐾 You Look Like Your Parents 🐾

Life Science — Parents & Offspring | Unit 2, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 1st Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Parents & Offspring 🏠 Materials: Household only
🐕 🐶   🐈 🐱   🐄 🐮
Baby animals look like their parents — but smaller! What traits did they inherit?
📋 NGSS Standard:
1-LS3-1 — Use observations to describe patterns of what plants and animals (including humans) do to survive and how the behavior of parents helps offspring survive.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will observe and describe how offspring resemble parents in structure, and explain what inherited traits are.

🔬 The Big Idea

When animals have babies, their offspring look a lot like them! A puppy looks like a dog. A kitten looks like a cat. A calf looks like a cow. This is because babies inherit traits — features like fur color, body shape, and the number of legs — from their parents. These traits are passed down from parent to offspring, just like a gift from one generation to the next.

💡 Key vocabulary: offspring, parent, inherited trait, observe, describe, structure

📦 Materials

🏠 Household Items to Gather

  • The matching cards printed from the worksheet pages (or draw your own)
  • Scissors ✂️ and a glue stick
  • Crayons or colored pencils
  • A family photo (optional) — of the student's own family to find similarities
  • A mirror (optional) — to look at their own face and identify features

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Warm-Up: Who's the Baby? (5 min)
Look at the emoji pairs below. Ask: "Can you tell which one is the parent and which is the baby? How do you know?"
🐕 and 🐶  |  🐈 and 🐱  |  🐄 and 🐮  |  🐴 and 🐎
Key discussion: "What makes them look the same? What makes them look different?"
Step 2 — Parent-Offspring Matching (15 min)
Cut out the matching cards on the worksheet page. Sort them into pairs: baby animal with its parent.
  • 🐶 Puppy → 🐕 Dog
  • 🐱 Kitten → 🐈 Cat
  • 🐮 Calf → 🐄 Cow
  • 🐑 Lamb → 🐏 Sheep
  • 🐴 Foal → 🐎 Horse
  • 🐥 Duckling → 🦆 Duck
For each pair, observe: "What body parts look the same? Same type of legs? Same fur or feathers? Same face shape?"
Step 3 — Trait Chart (10 min)
Fill in the trait chart on the worksheet. For each animal pair, note which body features the baby inherited from the parent.
Step 4 — Connect to Humans (10 min)
Look at a family photo (or draw one). Ask: "What features do you have that look like your mom? Your dad?"
Examples: eye color, hair color, nose shape, smile, ear shape.
Introduce the vocabulary: "These features that you got from your parents are called inherited traits."
Step 5 — Wrap-Up Drawing (5 min)
On the worksheet, draw a picture of yourself and circle one trait you inherited from a parent.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • Why does a puppy look like a dog but not like a cat?
  • What body parts look the same between a baby bird and an adult bird?
  • Can you think of something a baby animal has that looks different from its parent? (Baby ducks are fluffier! Kittens have blue eyes at birth.)
  • Do you look exactly like one parent, or are you a mix of both?
🌟 Big Question: "You got some traits from your mom and some from your dad. Does that mean you're just a mix of them?"

Sort of, but it's more complex! Each parent passes on half their genetic information. Which half gets passed is partly random — that's why siblings look different even with the same parents. You might get your dad's eye color and your mom's nose, or combine them in a new way. That's why children resemble but don't look identical to either parent.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🔍 Nature Walk: Look for a plant and its seeds or seedlings. Do they look like the parent plant?
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "Like a Windy Day" or any book about animal families.
  • 🎨 Family Portrait: Draw your whole family and circle shared traits between people.
  • 📷 Photo Compare: Find old photos of parents as children. How do they compare to photos of yourself?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Age tip: First graders grasp the visual matching easily — keep the focus on observable features (color, shape, size), not genetics vocabulary.
  • Sensitive families: If a child is adopted or doesn't have photos of biological family, redirect to the animal examples and have them draw a fictional animal family instead.
  • Key concept to emphasize: Baby animals look like their parents, not like other species. A puppy will always grow up to look like a dog — never a cat.
  • Assessment: Can the child match all 6 animal pairs? Can they name at least 2 inherited traits they observe? Can they use the word "inherited"?
