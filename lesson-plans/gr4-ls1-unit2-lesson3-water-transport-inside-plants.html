The Big Idea

Plants need more than roots that absorb water. They also need internal structures that move water upward to stems, leaves, and flowers.

This lesson makes the hidden structure-function idea visible: even when we cannot see the transport pathway directly, we can use evidence from observations to infer what the internal structure is doing.

The Science

Water moves from roots upward through internal transport tissues inside the plant. In classroom observations, colored water in celery or white flowers can help students see that water reaches different plant parts through internal pathways. These internal structures support survival because the plant must move water where it is needed for growth and food-making processes.

Key vocabulary: internal structure, transport, pathway, absorb, stem, water movement, evidence, tissue

Materials

Gather Before You Start

  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • celery stalk or white flower
  • cup, water, food coloring
  • pencil

Let's Get Started

Step 1: Set up or discuss a classic colored-water observation with celery or a flower.
Step 2: Ask students what changed and what that change suggests about water moving inside the plant.
Step 3: Connect the visible result to the hidden structure: internal pathways carry water upward.
Step 4: Have students record an observation, an inference, and the function that inference supports.
Step 5: Finish with a claim about why water-transport structures matter for survival and growth.

Discussion Questions

Q: What evidence suggests water moved inside the plant?Color change or visible movement clues are the main evidence.
Q: Why is water transport important after the roots absorb water?Because the whole plant needs access to water, not just the roots.
Q: How is this lesson about internal structures, not just visible parts?Students infer a hidden pathway from observable evidence.

Extensions

Repeat the celery test with two stalks in different colors and compare how the color spreads. Then discuss what the evidence suggests about internal pathways inside the stem.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: This is the strongest Unit 2 lesson for evidence language. Emphasize observation plus inference, since the internal structure is not directly visible in everyday life.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: Student wording may vary if the structure-function science is correct.

  • What Success Looks Like: Student uses an observation to infer that internal pathways move water through the plant.
  • Acceptable examples: Colored water appeared in the celery veins or petals, showing that water moved upward inside the plant.
  • Sentence-frame support: Students may write, “I observed color in the stem. This suggests water moved inside the plant. This helps the plant because water reaches other parts that need it.”
  • Coach for evidence: Push students to connect “what changed” to “what that reveals about the hidden structure.”
