Reading Weather Data

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
📊🌦️🧭
Charts and graphs help scientists turn weather observations into evidence they can compare.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS2-1 — Obtain and combine information to describe climates in different regions of the world.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will read simple weather charts and graphs and use them to compare conditions and defend a claim with evidence.

💡 The Big Idea

Weather data is easier to understand when it is organized in a chart or graph. Scientists use displays to compare days, notice trends, and support explanations.

🔬 The Science

A weather chart might show temperature, rainfall, or cloud symbols across several days. A graph makes it easier to notice whether values are rising, falling, or staying about the same.

In this lesson, students interpret a data display instead of only looking at isolated observations. This builds the habit of supporting an explanation with evidence from a chart.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Read the included chart title and labels first (5 min)
Use the five-day weather chart above. Show students how the day names, weather symbols, and labels tell what kind of data they are reading.
Step 2 — Find the biggest change (10 min)
Look at the included weather chart and ask which day was warmest, wettest, windiest, or cloudiest.
Step 3 — Compare two days (10 min)
Students use the included chart to say how one day was different from another and which two days seem most similar.
Step 4 — Write an evidence sentence (10 min)
Students explain a trend using numbers, rainfall amounts, wind speeds, or weather symbols from the included chart.
Step 5 — Share a claim and evidence (10 min)
Students practice saying “I know this because the chart shows…” and point to one exact part of the included chart.

Discussion Questions

Why are labels important on a chart or graph?Labels tell what the evidence means.
What trend do you notice in the data?Students may say temperature rose, rainfall increased, or wind decreased.
How can two students use the same chart to make the same claim?They can point to the same evidence in the display.
Why is a chart better than only guessing from memory?A chart organizes evidence clearly.

Extensions

Extension: Turn one row of the worksheet into a simple bar graph and label the trend you notice.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep asking students to point to the exact part of the chart that supports their answer.

Important misconception to catch: Students may think the biggest bar or symbol always means “best” instead of simply meaning “more” or “higher.”

1️⃣ Read the Weather Chart — Sample Acceptable Answers

Warmest day: Thursday, because the included chart shows 74°F.

Most rain: Thursday, because the chart shows 0.8 inches of rainfall.

Most similar days: Tuesday and Friday are a strong answer because both are partly cloudy and both show 7 mph wind.

2️⃣ Describe a Trend — Sample Idea

“The temperature rises from Monday to Thursday and then drops on Friday.”

3️⃣ Claim + Evidence — Sample Response

“I know Thursday had the most rain because the included chart shows 0.8 inches of rainfall on Thursday, which is more than any other day.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student reads the included chart labels correctly.
  • Student compares days using specific evidence from the included five-day weather chart.
  • Student writes a claim and supports it with an exact number, day, or weather symbol from the chart.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student answers correctly but gives no evidence, ask: “Which number, label, or symbol on the included chart proves that?”
  • If a student misreads the display, slow down and re-check the chart title, day labels, and column labels first.
