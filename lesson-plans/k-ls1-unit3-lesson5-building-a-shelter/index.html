🏠 Building a Shelter: Every Animal Needs a Home 🏠

Life Science — Animal Needs  |  Unit 3, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Animal Needs 📋 Unit 3, Lesson 5 of 6
🐦🪹 🐇🕳️ 🐻🏔️ 🐟🪨
Birds weave nests, rabbits dig burrows, bears find dens — every animal builds or finds a home!
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-LS1-1 — Use observations to describe patterns of what plants and animals (including humans) need to survive.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will BUILD a model shelter for a toy animal using household materials, IDENTIFY at least 2 things a shelter must protect an animal from, and EXPLAIN why their shelter design meets those needs.

💡 The Big Idea

Every animal needs shelter to survive. A shelter is a safe place that protects animals from weather (rain, cold, wind), hides them from predators (animals that might eat them), and gives them a cozy place to rest and raise their babies. Without shelter, animals would be cold, wet, and in danger.

💡 Key Vocabulary: shelter, predator, protection, den, burrow, nest, habitat

🔬 The Science

Animals can't just live anywhere — they need a habitat that provides food, water, AND shelter. Different animals build or find very different shelters depending on their size, their predators, and the weather in their home environment. A bird's nest is made of twigs and held high in a tree. A rabbit's burrow is dug deep underground. A bear's den might be a cave or a hollow log. A fish might hide inside an underwater cave or rocky crevice. All of these shelters do the same important jobs — they keep the animal safe, dry, and comfortable.

🏠 Connect to humans: Our houses are our shelters too! A roof keeps us dry. Walls keep out the wind and cold. Locks keep us safe. We're animals too — we just build fancier homes!

📦 Materials

🧸 You'll Need a Toy Animal

  • Any small toy: bird, rabbit, bear, fish, or similar
  • A stuffed animal works great too!

🏠 Household Building Supplies

  • Cardboard boxes (any size)
  • 5–10 small sticks or twigs (pencil-length), collected from outdoors. No yard? Use toothpicks, wooden coffee stirrers, or pipe cleaners instead!
  • Yarn or string
  • Cotton balls
  • Towels and blankets
  • Pillows
  • Tape and scissors
  • A bowl or bin (for fish option)
  • A spray bottle with water (for shelter testing!)

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — What Is a Shelter? (5 min)
Start with a question: "What does a bird need to survive? What about a bear? What about a rabbit?"

After students name food and water, ask: "What about a safe place to sleep? A place to have babies? A place to hide from danger? That's called a SHELTER."

Show pictures or draw simple sketches of: a bird nest, a rabbit burrow, a bear den, a fish cave. Let kids identify them.
Step 2 — Choose Your Animal (3 min)
Each student picks a toy animal and decides what kind of shelter it needs:

🐦
Bird → Nest
Use sticks/twigs from the yard, yarn, and cotton balls in a bowl
🐇
Rabbit → Burrow
Drape towels over a cardboard box with a "tunnel" entrance
🐻
Bear → Den
Stack blankets and pillows into a cozy cave shape
🐟
Fish → Underwater Hiding Spot
Fish find shelter in underwater plants and rocks! Create a safe hiding spot for a toy fish using a cup and crumpled paper (rocks) with a few sprigs of fake or real plant tucked around it. Note: this is a dry, tabletop model — no water needed! We're representing what an underwater shelter would look like.
Step 3 — Build the Shelter! (10–15 min)
Using ONLY household materials, build the shelter for the chosen animal. Encourage kids to:
  • Think about the SIZE — can the animal fit inside?
  • Think about COVERING — is there a roof or walls?
  • Think about HIDING — can the animal be hidden from outside?
  • Add a space for "babies" — make it big enough for a family!
"Real animals are incredible engineers. You are too!"
Step 4 — Test the Shelter! (5–10 min)
Now it's time to see if the shelter really works. Run each test:

🧪 Shelter Test Checklist

🌧️ "Rain" test: Lightly sprinkle water near the shelter with a spray bottle. Does the animal stay dry?
🐣 "Baby" test: Could another small toy animal fit inside? Is there room for a family?
👁️ "Predator" test: Step back and look — can you see the animal from outside? Is it hidden?
If a test fails, IMPROVE the shelter! Real animals do this too — they adjust and rebuild.
Step 5 — Share & Discuss (5 min)
Each child presents their shelter. Ask: "What did you build? What does it protect your animal from? What would you change to make it even better?"

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: "What would happen if a bird didn't have a nest?"

Without a nest, a bird's eggs would fall to the ground and break, and baby birds would be exposed to rain, cold, and predators. The nest keeps the family safe, warm, and together — it's like a nursery and a fortress!

🗣️ Question 2: "Humans build houses for shelter. What's one thing a human house does that an animal's shelter also needs to do?"

Both need to keep out rain, cold, and wind — provide safety from danger — and have space for the family! Animals are clever builders: beavers build dams, birds weave nests, some fish dig holes in sand. We're not so different from animals after all!

🚀 Extensions

  • 🦫 Beaver Dam Challenge: Can you build a dam with sticks across a shallow tray of water? Will it hold? Beavers do this to make a safe pond for their lodge!
  • 🕸️ Spider Web Hunt: Go outside and find a spider web. What materials did the spider use? How is it like a shelter?
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "A House Is a House for Me" by Mary Ann Hoberman — a classic exploration of all kinds of animal homes!
  • 🗺️ Animal Home Map: Draw a neighborhood — but for animals! Where does the bird live? The rabbit? The bear? Label each home.
  • 🏘️ Real vs. Model: Look up pictures of real bird nests, beaver dams, and termite mounds. How close did your model get?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • All household materials — nothing to buy. Sticks and twigs from the yard are perfect for bird nests. Raid the blanket closet for bear dens!
  • This is an engineering activity as well as science. When students test their shelters and find they fail, encourage them to iterate and improve — that's real science and engineering thinking. Failure is part of the process!
  • Connect back to humans: Our houses are our "shelters" too — what does our house protect us from? Rain, wind, cold, danger? Help kids see that humans and animals have the same survival needs.
  • Discuss different real animal shelters: birds' nests, beaver dams, rabbit burrows, spider webs, termite mounds, prairie dog towns. Each one is perfectly designed for that animal's needs.
  • Vocab to reinforce: shelter, predator, protection, den, burrow, nest, habitat
  • Assessment tip: Can the child name at least two things a shelter protects an animal from? Can they explain why their shelter works (or what it needs to work better)? That's the learning goal.

✂️ PARENT/TEACHER ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

💬 Discussion Question 1

Q: "What would happen if a bird didn't have a nest?"

A: Without a nest, a bird's eggs would fall to the ground and break, and baby birds would be exposed to rain, cold, and predators. The nest keeps the family safe, warm, and together — it's like a nursery and a fortress!

💬 Discussion Question 2

Q: "Humans build houses for shelter. What's one thing a human house does that an animal's shelter also needs to do?"

A: Both need to keep out rain/cold/wind, provide safety from danger, and have space for the family. Animals are incredibly clever builders — beavers build dams, birds weave nests, some fish dig holes in sand. Humans and animals share the same core survival needs!

✏️ Worksheet Fill-In

"My shelter protects my animal from ___."
Accept any of the following (great answers include multiple): rain, cold, wind, predators, danger, getting wet, the cold ground, being found by enemies, bad weather. A child who names 2 or more is demonstrating excellent understanding.

🧪 Shelter Test Expectations

  • 🌧️ Rain test: The shelter should provide cover. If it doesn't, guide the student to add a "roof" layer (a box lid, extra towel, or leaves).
  • 🐣 Baby test: If the shelter is too small, encourage expanding it. Real animals often enlarge their nests/burrows as their family grows!
  • 👁️ Predator test: If the animal is visible, encourage adding walls or a "door." Rabbits' burrow entrances are often disguised with leaves and sticks.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student built a shelter that covers/hides the animal in some way
  • Student can name at least 2 things a shelter protects an animal from
  • Student can explain the function of their shelter (not just describe what it looks like)
  • Bonus: Student made at least one improvement after a failed test

🐾 Real-World Animal Shelter Reference

  • 🐦 Bird nest: Woven from twigs, grass, mud — keeps eggs warm and off the ground
  • 🐇 Rabbit burrow: Tunnels underground — warm, hidden from foxes and hawks
  • 🐻 Bear den: Cave, hollow log, or dug-out space — for hibernating through winter
  • 🐟 Fish hiding spot: Underwater plants and rocks — hides from larger fish predators. Model: cup + crumpled paper (rocks) + plant sprigs
  • 🦫 Beaver lodge: Built from sticks and mud in the middle of a pond — entrance is underwater for protection!
  • 🕷️ Spider web: A trap AND a home — some spiders hide in curled leaves attached to their web
  • 🐜 Termite mound: Can be taller than a human — temperature-regulated inside!
