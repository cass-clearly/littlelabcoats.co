🧱 Explain and Protect a Changing Streambank

Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 6 of 6

Grade: 4Duration: 60 minutesSubject: Earth Science, Evidence & SolutionsMaterials: Tray, water, rocks, sticks, paper strips
NGSS Standard: 4-ESS2-1 and 4-ESS3-2 — Use evidence about weathering and erosion, and generate or compare solutions that reduce the impacts of natural Earth processes on people.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Test two different ways to reduce streambank erosion in a model.
  • Measure and compare which solution protects the surface better.
  • Write an evidence-based explanation for which design worked best and why.

📦 Materials

  • Baking pan or tray with sand or soil
  • Water cup or squeeze bottle
  • Small rocks or pebbles
  • Craft sticks or cardboard strips
  • Paper strips, grass clippings, or moss to model vegetation
  • Ruler and pencil

📖 Vocabulary

streambankThe side edge of a stream or river channel
solutionA design or action meant to solve a problem
criteriaWhat success should look like
compareTo look for similarities and differences using evidence
protectTo reduce damage or loss

🔬 Background

People often need to protect roads, trails, farms, or homes from erosion. Scientists and engineers study patterns of land change and then compare possible solutions. Some solutions use rocks. Some use planted vegetation. Some use walls or barriers. Good decisions rely on evidence.

In this final unit lesson, students use what they learned about weathering, erosion, deposition, maps, and patterns to explain a real surface problem. Then they compare solutions by testing them in a simple model.

The goal is not to find one perfect answer forever. The goal is to use observations and measurements to defend which solution worked better in this test.

🧪 Activity

Step 1: Build one sloped streambank in a tray. Run a control test with no protection and measure how far the sediment moves.
Step 2: Rebuild the streambank. Choose Solution A, such as a line of rocks or a stick wall. Test it with the same amount of water and measure the movement.
Step 3: Rebuild again. Choose Solution B, such as vegetation strips or a combination design. Test it the same way.
Step 4: Compare all three trials: control, Solution A, and Solution B. Which one slowed erosion the most?
Step 5: Sketch the strongest design and write an explanation supported by evidence from your measurements and observations.
Step 6: Connect your result to the real world by naming a place where protecting land from erosion matters.

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why must all three trials use the same amount of water?

Hint: A fair comparison needs the same test conditions.

Q2: Why might a combination of rocks and vegetation work better than only one material?

Hint: Different materials solve different parts of the problem.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Fair-test reminder: Keep slope, water amount, and starting point the same.
  • Good enough is okay: Measurements can be approximate as long as they are used consistently.
  • Culminating connection: This lesson ties together breaking, moving, dropping, mapping, and explaining surface change.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Data table: Distances vary. The strongest work uses measurement plus observations such as “the rocks stopped the bank from collapsing.”
  2. Best solution: Accept any solution supported by evidence from the student’s own test.
  3. Sketch: The design should show thoughtful placement of materials.
  4. Final explanation: Strong responses compare trials and explain why the chosen solution reduced erosion best.
