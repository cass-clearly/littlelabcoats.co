✍️ Short Response — Use Evidence

Prompt: Explain how your salt dough model helped you understand what fossils tell scientists.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Use the quiz and short response together: The multiple-choice section checks recognition, while the short response checks whether the student can explain an idea with evidence.
  • Good evidence sounds specific: Look for references to the actual materials, observations, comparison tests, or redesign notes from the lesson.
  • Freebie parity note: This quiz is intentionally structured like a real LLC companion artifact rather than a bare answer sheet.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: This page is for parent/teacher reference. Student wording may vary if the science idea is correct.

Multiple-Choice Answer Key

  1. 1. A clue from a long-ago living thing
  2. 2. An imprint
  3. 3. It lets us model how an imprint can be preserved
  4. 4. Paleontologists
  5. 5. What some ancient living things looked like or did
  6. 6. They formed quickly and are models, not ancient preserved evidence
  7. 7. A clue about shape and structure
  8. 8. Compare the patterns and describe what they notice

What Success Looks Like

  • Fossils are clues from long ago
  • An imprint is a pressed shape left in material
  • Our dough model is a fast model, not a real fossil
  • Important distinction: A student should recognize that the salt dough version is practice evidence — it shows how a fossil imprint can preserve clues, but it did not form over a long time in nature.

Short Response Guidance

  • Student states a correct science idea from the lesson.
  • Student uses one real observation, comparison, or test result as evidence.
  • Student connects the evidence to the idea instead of only restating a vocabulary word.
  • Strong responses explain that the dough model helped the student notice shape clues, while a real fossil is ancient preserved evidence from the past.
