Rocks and Soils

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 2

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 50 minutes 🔬 Subject: Earth Science: Earth's Materials 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🪨🌱💧🔬
Rocks are made of minerals, and soil is full of life! Let's examine Earth's building blocks.
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-ESS2-2: Map the shapes and kinds of land and bodies of water in an area and where water on Earth is found.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Compare rocks using observable properties (color, texture, size, hardness).
  • Explain that soil has layers and is made of weathered rock plus organic matter.
  • Observe soil settling in water to see different layers.

💡 Background

Rocks are made of minerals. Different minerals give rocks different colors, textures, and hardness. There are three main types of rocks: igneous (formed from cooled lava), sedimentary (formed from layers of sand and shells pressed together), and metamorphic (changed by heat and pressure). Soil is made from tiny pieces of weathered rock mixed with decayed plants and animals. Soil has layers: topsoil (dark, rich in nutrients), subsoil (lighter, less nutrients), and bedrock (solid rock at the bottom).

💡 Key vocabulary: mineral, texture, soil, layer, sediment

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

  • 3 to 4 rocks from the yard, driveway, or a park (different types work best)
  • A clear jar or bottle with a lid
  • About 1 cup of soil (from the yard or a pot)
  • Water
  • A ruler (optional)
  • Paper and pencil

✅ All household items, nothing to buy!

📖 Vocabulary

minerala natural, nonliving solid that makes up rocks
texturehow the surface of a rock feels: rough, smooth, grainy, glassy
soila mixture of tiny rock particles, water, air, and organic matter
layera distinct band of material, such as the layers in soil
sedimentsmall particles of rock, sand, or soil that settle at the bottom of water

🔬 Procedure

Step 1 (10 min): Rock Observation: Examine each rock carefully. Record color, texture (rough or smooth?), and hardness (can you scratch it with a coin?). Are there layers or crystals visible?
Step 2 (10 min): Soil Layer Experiment: Put 1 cup of soil in the jar. Add water to about 3/4 full. Secure the lid and shake vigorously for 30 seconds. Set on a level surface and watch without disturbing.
Step 3 (15 min): Observe Settling: Over the next 10 to 15 minutes, watch as particles settle: heavy gravel and sand settle first (bottom), then silt (middle), then clay (top). Tiny organic bits may float.
Step 4 (10 min): Discussion and Wrap-Up: Ask the discussion questions and complete the worksheet.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Q1: How do you think rocks turn into soil over millions of years?

Suggested answer: Rocks are slowly broken down by water, wind, ice, and plants. Water freezes in rock cracks and splits them. Roots grow into cracks and widen them. Wind blows particles away. This process is called weathering. Over millions of years, big rocks become tiny particles that mix with dead plants to make soil.

🗣️ Q2: Why do plants need soil? Could they grow on bare rock?

Suggested answer: Plants need soil for nutrients (from decayed organic matter), water (soil holds moisture), and anchorage (roots grip soil). Most plants cannot grow on bare rock without soil, though some hardy plants like moss and lichen can slowly start breaking down rock surfaces.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🪨 Rock ID: Look up images of igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks. Can you match your yard rocks to a type?
  • 🌱 Seed in Sand vs. Soil: Plant the same seed in a cup of sand and a cup of soil. Which grows better and why?
  • 📚 Book Connection: 'If You Find a Rock' by Peggy Christian

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Settling times: Gravel settles in seconds, sand in minutes, silt in hours, clay can take days. The experiment shows the beginning of settling.
  • Rock type shortcuts: Shiny crystals = probably igneous; visible layers = probably sedimentary; swirly patterns = probably metamorphic.
  • Soil life: Healthy soil contains billions of bacteria, fungi, worms, and insects per handful. Soil is very much alive!
