🌿 Plant Parts on the Job

Garden Detectives Unit, Lesson 3

🌼 Ages: mixed-age family learners⏱️ Time: 45–60 minutes🪴 Unit: Garden Detectives📖 Lesson: 3 of 6
Hands-on gardening science
Roots, stems, leaves, flowers, and seeds become a tiny garden workforce. Kids run a visible water-transport demo and explain each part’s job.

Big idea: plant parts work together; a healthy garden depends on each structure doing its job.

🎯 Learning Goal

Students will use observations and evidence to explain how plant parts work together.

📦 Materials

  • Celery stalks with leaves or white carnations
  • Clear cup or jar
  • Water
  • Food coloring
  • Knife for adult use only
  • Paper towels
  • Printed plant-part cards or the large job cards on this page

🧪 Lesson Flow

Step 1 — Meet the garden work crew
Use the big job cards to introduce five workers: roots anchor and drink, stems hold and move water, leaves make food, flowers help seeds form, and seeds start new plants.
Step 2 — Start the color-change demo
Place celery or a white flower in colored water. Predict which part will show color first and what a gardener might notice later.
Step 3 — Garden rescue match-up
Instead of acting it out, use plant problem cards or spoken scenarios: droopy leaves, floppy stem, dried roots, no flowers, pale leaves. Match each garden problem to the plant part that needs help and say what the gardener should do.
Step 4 — Evidence check
After 20–40 minutes, inspect colored lines in celery or petals. Adult may slice celery crosswise to reveal tiny tubes. For older learners, explain that xylem carries water upward through the plant, while phloem carries sugar made in the leaves to other parts.
Step 5 — Garden care connection
Use the evidence from the demo to decide how a gardener helps each worker: deep watering helps roots, spacing/stakes help stems, sunlight and air help leaves, and pollinators help flowers make seeds.

🌈 Mixed-age roles

Age 4 — Plant Worker

  • Point to the large root/stem/leaf/flower/seed job cards
  • Match one garden problem to one plant part
  • Color the plant-part job badge

Age 9 — Job Explainer

  • Record what changed in the dye demo
  • Match plant problems to plant part jobs
  • Explain how roots and stems work together in a real garden

Age 12 — Systems Thinker

  • Use evidence from colored tubes to explain transport
  • Define xylem and phloem in simple scientific language
  • Connect plant-part jobs to garden care decisions and failure points
Garden problem matching cards:

🥬 Droopy leaves

The leaves look limp by afternoon. Is the biggest issue roots, stems, or leaves? What should the gardener check first?

🌱 Floppy stem

The plant falls sideways after a windy day. Which plant part needs support? What could a gardener do?

🪴 Crowded roots

A plant in a tiny pot dries out fast and stops growing. What is happening underground?

🌼 No flowers yet

The plant has leaves but is not making flowers. What might it still need from its environment?

💬 Discussion Questions

Which plant part had the most visible evidence in the demo?
Why can’t a plant solve every problem with only leaves?
If a seedling falls over, which structure might need support?

🚀 Extensions

  • Compare celery with leaves vs celery without leaves to ask whether leaves affect water movement.
  • Dissect a soaked bean seed and look for seed coat, stored food, and tiny embryo.
  • Make a garden-care chart: water helps roots, stakes help stems, sunlight helps leaves.

👩‍🏫 Parent / Teacher Notes

  • Adult handles cutting celery; children can observe the cross-section safely.
  • Food coloring timing varies. If color is slow, inspect again later and treat it as long-term observation.
  • Keep tying each part back to real gardening: watering roots, spacing/staking stems, giving leaves light and air, and protecting flowers for pollination.

✅ Parent Answer Key / Coaching Guide

  • Root: anchors and absorbs water/nutrients. Stem: supports and transports water. Leaf: uses sunlight, water, and air to make food. Flower/seed: helps make new plants.
  • Dye evidence should mention colored water moving through the stem into leaves or petals.
  • For older learners: xylem is the tissue that carries water upward; phloem carries sugar made in leaves to the rest of the plant.
  • Problem-card reasoning matters more than perfect vocabulary; the strongest answers connect the plant part to a practical gardening move.
