🌦️ Weather Patterns

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 5 of 6

Grade: 2 Duration: 5 min/day for 5 days Subject: Earth Science, Weather Materials: Paper, pencil, window/outdoor access
NGSS Standard: 2-ESS1-1 — Use information from several sources to provide evidence that Earth events can occur quickly or slowly.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Observe and record weather data daily for 5 consecutive days
  • Identify patterns in weather observations over time
  • Distinguish between weather (short-term) and climate (long-term patterns)

📦 Materials

  • Paper and pencil
  • Access to a window or outdoor space (5 minutes per day for 5 days)
  • Crayons (optional, for weather symbols)
  • This worksheet (fill in each day)

📖 Vocabulary

weather What the atmosphere is doing right now (temperature, clouds, rain, wind)
climate The typical weather patterns of a place over a long period of time
temperature How hot or cold the air is
precipitation Water that falls from the sky (rain, snow, sleet, hail)
pattern Something that repeats or happens in a regular, predictable way
meteorologist A scientist who studies and predicts weather

🔬 Background

Weather is what is happening in the atmosphere right now: today's temperature, whether it is sunny or cloudy, if it is raining or snowing, how windy it is. Weather changes from day to day and even hour to hour. Scientists who study and predict weather are called meteorologists. They use thermometers, rain gauges, barometers, and satellites to gather data about weather all around the world.

Climate is different from weather. Climate describes the typical weather patterns of a place over a long period of time, usually 30 years or more. For example, the Sahara Desert has a hot, dry climate. Colorado has a semi-arid climate with cold, snowy winters. When we track weather data day after day, year after year, we start to see patterns. These patterns are what make up climate. Tracking weather for just 5 days gives us a tiny glimpse of this process.

Weather affects almost everything people do. Farmers need to know when to plant. Pilots check weather before flying. Communities prepare for storms, blizzards, and droughts. Understanding weather patterns helps scientists predict what is coming and helps people stay safe. Even ancient peoples observed weather patterns to plan their farming and travel.

🧪 Activity: 5-Day Weather Journal

Step 1: Each day at the same time (morning is best), go to a window or step outside.
Step 2: Observe and record on your weather journal: Temperature feel (warm/cool/cold), Sky (clear/partly cloudy/cloudy/overcast), Precipitation (none/light rain/heavy rain/snow/sleet), Wind (calm/light breeze/strong wind).
Step 3: Optional: Draw a quick weather symbol in the "sketch" box for that day.
Step 4: On Day 5, look back at all 5 days together. Answer the reflection questions.
Step 5: Were most days similar? Did any day surprise you? Did you see a pattern?

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: How is weather different from climate? Can you give an example of each?

Hint: Weather is what is happening right now: "It is cloudy and 45 degrees today." Climate is the overall pattern over a long time: "Colorado Springs usually gets about 16 inches of rain per year and has 300 sunny days." Weather changes daily; climate changes over decades.

Q2: Why do scientists track weather data for years and years instead of just looking outside?

Hint: One week of data, or even one year, does not show the full pattern. Scientists need decades of data to understand climate, detect trends (like warming), and make reliable predictions. The more data you have, the more confident your predictions. This is why weather stations have been collecting data continuously for more than 100 years.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Scheduling: This activity spans 5 days. Plan it on a school week. It only takes 5 minutes per day.
  • Alternative data source: If weather is hard to observe (all cloudy week, indoor situation), you can supplement with a weather website or app for actual data. The goal is observing patterns, not just looking outside.
  • Temperature feel: "Warm/cool/cold" is appropriate for 2nd grade. If your child is ready, you can add actual thermometer readings.
  • Scientific thinking: This is a great opportunity to discuss how scientists use data. Emphasize: good scientists write down their observations every time. They do not trust their memory!
  • Climate connection: This connects to the concept of climate change. When global temperatures are tracked for decades, scientists can see the long-term warming trend. This is age-appropriate to introduce as: "When scientists look at 100 years of weather records, they noticed the Earth has been getting warmer."

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. 5-Day Weather Journal: Answers will vary based on actual weather observed. Check that the child filled in all 5 days and used the correct categories (temperature feel, sky, precipitation, wind).
  2. Reflection questions: Answers will vary. Accept any reasonable observation based on the data collected. The goal is noticing patterns, not a specific "right" answer.
  3. Circle one: Weather is short-term. Climate is long-term.
  4. Bonus: Accept any thoughtful answer. Good responses mention seasonal changes (cold in winter, warm in summer), more rain in certain months, snow in winter, or longer days in summer.
