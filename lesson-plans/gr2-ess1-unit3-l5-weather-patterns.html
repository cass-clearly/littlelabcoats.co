Weather Patterns

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 5

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 5 days (15 min/day) + 30 min analysis 🔬 Subject: Earth Science: Weather 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
☀️🌧️❄️🌤️📊
Scientists observe the sky every day to find weather patterns. You can too!
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-ESS1-1: Use information from several sources to provide evidence that Earth events can occur quickly or slowly.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Record daily weather observations over five consecutive days.
  • Identify patterns in weather data (temperature trends, repeating conditions).
  • Distinguish between weather (daily conditions) and climate (typical long-term patterns).

💡 Background

Weather is the condition of the atmosphere at a specific place and time: sunny, rainy, windy, hot, cold. Climate is the typical weather pattern for a region over many years. Meteorologists (weather scientists) observe and record weather data daily to find patterns and make predictions. Weather changes quickly from day to day. Climate changes very slowly. Scientists use weather data over many years to understand climate patterns and track long-term changes.

💡 Key vocabulary: weather, climate, pattern, data, temperature

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

  • Paper folded into a 5-day weather journal (fold 5 sheets and staple)
  • Pencil and crayons
  • A window to observe the sky
  • Optional: an outdoor thermometer

✅ All household items, nothing to buy!

📖 Vocabulary

weatherthe condition of the atmosphere on a specific day: temperature, clouds, wind, rain
climatethe typical weather patterns of a region over many years

temperaturea measure of how hot or cold something is

🔬 Procedure

Day 1 Setup (10 min): Make the weather journal. Label pages Day 1 through Day 5. Each day's page should have space for: date, sky conditions (clear/cloudy/partly cloudy), precipitation (none/rain/snow), estimated temperature (hot/warm/cool/cold), wind (none/gentle/strong), and a drawing of the sky.
Days 1 to 5 (15 min each day): At the same time each day, observe the weather and record it. Look outside, feel the air (briefly if safe), note any rain, snow, or clouds.
Day 5 Analysis (20 min): Look back at all 5 days. Create a simple summary chart. Did temperature go up or down? Were some days sunny and some cloudy? Were there patterns?
Wrap-Up (10 min): Ask the discussion questions and complete the summary worksheet.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Q1: If it was sunny and warm for 4 days, then rainy on Day 5, does that mean the climate is 'rainy'?

Suggested answer: No! Climate is based on patterns over many years, not just a few days. Weather changes quickly from day to day. To know the climate of a place, scientists look at decades of weather data and find what is typical. Five days is too small a sample.

🗣️ Q2: Why do you think weather forecasters sometimes get predictions wrong?

Suggested answer: Weather systems are very complex with millions of interacting factors: air pressure, temperature, moisture, wind, landforms. Even with supercomputers and satellites, small changes can lead to different outcomes. Long-range forecasts are harder because small errors grow over time.

🚀 Extensions

  • 📈 Monthly Weather Chart: Continue the weather journal for a full month and graph the data.
  • 🌡️ Thermometer Reading: If you have a thermometer, record the actual temperature each day at the same time.
  • 📚 Book Connection: 'Weather Words and What They Mean' by Gail Gibbons

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Timing tip: Observe at the same time each day (such as 9 AM or right after breakfast) for the most consistent data.
  • Weather vs. climate: An easy analogy: weather is what you wear today; climate is what kind of clothes you keep in your closet.
  • Extensions across the unit: This lesson pairs well with keeping records across multiple units. Weather observation can continue all year.
