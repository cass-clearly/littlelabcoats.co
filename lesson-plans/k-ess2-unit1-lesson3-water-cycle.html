🎯 Learning Objective

Students will DESCRIBE the water cycle and DEMONSTRATE evaporation and condensation using simple household materials.

🔬 The Big Idea: The Water Cycle

Water on Earth is always moving in a big loop called the water cycle. It evaporates from oceans and lakes into the air, forms clouds, falls back down as rain or snow, and flows back to the ocean — then the cycle starts all over again. The same water molecules have been cycling around Earth for billions of years!

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

  • A piece of dark-colored paper (black or dark blue works best)
  • Water
  • Ice cubes
  • A glass or cup
  • A warm, sunny room (or a sunny window)

🔬 Two Mini-Experiments

💨 Demo 1: Evaporation — The Disappearing Handprint

  1. Wet your hand with water
  2. Press it firmly onto a piece of dark paper, making a handprint
  3. Place the paper near a sunny window or in a warm spot
  4. Watch and wait — where does the handprint go?

What's happening? The water turns into invisible water vapor and rises into the air. That's evaporation! This is exactly how water moves from oceans and puddles into the sky.

🧊 Demo 2: Condensation — Water Appears From Nowhere!

  1. Fill a glass with cold water and ice cubes
  2. Set it on the table in a warm room and wait 5 minutes
  3. Look at the outside of the glass — do you see water droplets forming?
  4. Run your finger along the outside of the glass

What's happening? That water did NOT come from inside the glass — it came from the warm air around it! When warm air (carrying invisible water vapor) touches the cold glass, the vapor cools down and turns back into liquid water. That's condensation! This is exactly how clouds form in the sky.

Step 3 — Connect the Dots (5 min)
After both demos, discuss:
  • "Where did the handprint water go?" → Into the air as vapor (evaporation)
  • "Where did the water on the outside of the glass come from?" → From the air (condensation)
  • "How is this like clouds and rain?" → Water evaporates into the air, rises, cools into clouds (condensation), and falls back down as rain!
Step 4 — Draw the Water Cycle (5–10 min)
Use the worksheet on the next page to draw your own water cycle diagram. Label evaporation, condensation, and precipitation!

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • Have you ever seen a puddle dry up after rain? Where did the water go?
  • Why does a cold glass "sweat" on a warm day? Where does that water come from?
  • What's the difference between rain and snow? (They're both precipitation — rain is liquid, snow is frozen!)
  • Big Question: Where does rain come from, and what happens to it after it lands?

💡 Suggested answer: Water evaporates from oceans, lakes, and puddles into the air. It forms clouds when it cools at altitude. When clouds have too much water, it falls as rain or snow. It soaks into the ground or flows into rivers and eventually back to the ocean — then the cycle starts again! This cycle has been running for billions of years.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🌱 Bag Water Cycle: Draw a sun, clouds, and an ocean on a plastic zip-lock bag. Add a little water, seal it, and tape it to a sunny window. Watch the water cycle in action over a few days!
  • 🌊 Puddle Watch: After rain, trace a puddle with chalk. Come back every hour. How long until it evaporates?
  • 🏔️ Where Does Snow Come From? Look up how snowflakes form — each one starts as a tiny ice crystal around a speck of dust!
  • 📚 Read Aloud: A Drop of Water by Walter Wick or The Magic School Bus Wet All Over by Patricia Relf

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Best conditions for Demo 2: The condensation effect works best on a warm, humid day. If it's cold and dry, the effect may be less dramatic — try a hot summer day or near a warm stove.
  • Demo 1 timing: Dark paper dries faster in direct sunlight. On a cloudy day, it still works but takes longer. This is a great opportunity to talk about why!
  • Common misconception: Kids often think the water on the outside of a cold glass "leaked through." Emphasize: touch the ice water — it's cold! The water on the outside came from the warm air around it.
  • Rain vs. snow: Both are precipitation. Snow forms when water vapor freezes directly into ice crystals high in the atmosphere. Rain forms when water droplets in clouds get heavy enough to fall.
  • Assessment: Can the child describe evaporation and condensation in simple terms? Can they trace a water droplet through the cycle: ocean → sky → cloud → rain → river → ocean?

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Water Cycle Steps

  1. Evaporation — liquid water is heated by the sun and turns into invisible water vapor that rises into the air. (Demo 1: the handprint dries up)
  2. Condensation — water vapor rises and cools, turning back into tiny water droplets that form clouds. (Demo 2: droplets form on the cold glass)
  3. Precipitation — when clouds hold too much water, it falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
  4. Collection/Runoff — water flows into rivers and oceans, and soaks into the ground — then evaporation starts the cycle again

Experiment Explanations

  • Wet Handprint: Water evaporated into the air as water vapor. This is like water evaporating from oceans and lakes. Expected observation: the handprint fades and disappears.
  • Ice Water Glass: Water condensed on the outside from the warm air. The water on the glass came from humidity in the air that cooled when it touched the cold surface. Expected observation: water droplets appear on the outside of the glass.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Child can name the 3 main stages: evaporation, condensation, precipitation
  • Child can explain where rain comes from (water evaporates, forms clouds, falls as rain)
  • Child's diagram shows a cycle (circular path with arrows)
