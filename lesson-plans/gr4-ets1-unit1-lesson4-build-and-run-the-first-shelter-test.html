Build and Run the First Shelter Test

Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design | Unit 1, Lesson 4 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design
🛠️💨🌧️
First tests are for learning, not for perfection.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem with criteria and constraints, generate and compare solutions, and plan fair tests with variables controlled and failure points considered.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will build a first prototype and carry out a fair wind-and-rain test while recording evidence.

💡 The Big Idea

Testing reveals strengths, weaknesses, and failure points that planning alone cannot show.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

By Grade 4, students should begin thinking about fair tests. That means keeping important variables the same, such as shelter location, fan distance, or spray amount, so results can be compared.

💡 Key Vocabulary
fair testvariableprototypefailure pointevidenceobservation

Materials

  • Printed lesson pages and worksheet
  • Planned materials
  • Fan or hand-made wind source optional
  • Spray bottle or dripping cup optional
  • Paper figure or small toy

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Build version 1
Students assemble the first shelter using their plan.
Step 2 — Set fair-test rules
Keep the same paper figure, fan distance, and water amount for all trials.
Step 3 — Run the first trial
Observe whether the shelter stays upright and blocks wind and rain.
Step 4 — Record evidence
Write what worked, what failed, and where the weakest point was.
Step 5 — Prepare for redesign
Choose one or two changes to try next.

Discussion Questions

Why should the test stay fair?Fair tests help engineers compare versions honestly.
What is a failure point?The weakest part that did not work well.
Why is version 1 useful even if it fails?Failure gives evidence for redesign.
What kind of observations matter most?Specific evidence about movement, leaks, collapse, or stability.

Extensions

Extension: Repeat the same test after one small change and compare results.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Treat failure as information, not as the end of the process.

Misconception to catch: Students may want to change many things at once; remind them that smaller controlled changes create clearer evidence.

Sample ideas

Useful evidence may include roof bending, water reaching the figure, the whole structure sliding, or one wall collapsing in wind.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student records specific observations from a fair test.
  • Student identifies at least one failure point.
  • Student proposes a change connected to the evidence.
