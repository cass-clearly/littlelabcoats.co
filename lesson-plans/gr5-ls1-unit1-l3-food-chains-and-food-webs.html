Food Chains and Food Webs

Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs | Unit 1, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs
🕸️🦊
Food webs show the many pathways energy can take.
📋 NGSS Standard:
5-LS2-1 — Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare a food chain and a food web and explain why a web shows more realistic ecosystem relationships.

💡 The Big Idea

A food chain shows one path for energy flow, but a food web shows that most organisms connect to many others.

🔬 The Science

Simple diagrams are useful, but real ecosystems are messy and connected. A single organism may eat several things and may be eaten by several predators.

Food webs help scientists predict how a change in one population can affect many others.

💡 Key Vocabulary
food chainfood webconsumerpredatorpreyenergy flow

📦 Materials

🏠 Household Items to Gather

  • Organism cards
  • String or yarn
  • Worksheet and pencil

📋 Printables

  • Student worksheet page from this lesson

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Build a simple chain (10 min)
Create a producer → herbivore → predator example.
Step 2 — Add more connections (10 min)
Use more organism cards and yarn to connect feeding relationships.
Step 3 — Compare the models (10 min)
Students identify what a web shows that a chain misses.
Step 4 — Spot shared links (10 min)
Find one organism connected to multiple others.
Step 5 — Explain the advantage (10 min)
Write why a food web gives a better ecosystem picture.

💬 Discussion Questions

How is a food web different from a food chain?A web shows many pathways instead of one path.
Why is a food web more realistic?Most organisms have more than one relationship.
What can happen if one shared organism changes?Many linked organisms can be affected.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Build a food web from a local habitat using animals and plants you know.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep the arrows consistent. Point them from food source to the organism that gets the energy.
