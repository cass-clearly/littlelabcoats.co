🌬️ Patterns of Water, Wind, Ice, and Vegetation

Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

Grade: 4Duration: 50–60 minutesSubject: Earth Science, Pattern-Based ExplanationMaterials: Sand tray, straw, ice cube, water
NGSS Standard: 4-ESS2-1 — Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Compare the patterns left by water, wind, ice, and vegetation in simple models.
  • Match visible clues to the Earth process most likely responsible.
  • Use pattern evidence to explain how Earth’s surface changes in different ways.

📦 Materials

  • Tray with sand or soil
  • Straw or small fan
  • Ice cube
  • Cup of water or dropper
  • Small paper strips, grass, or craft moss to model vegetation
  • Pencil

📖 Vocabulary

agent of changeSomething that causes Earth’s surface to change, such as water, wind, or ice
glacierA large moving mass of ice
surface patternA visible clue left on the land
vegetation coverPlants or plant material covering the ground
explanationA claim supported by evidence and reasoning

🔬 Background

Science Snapshot
Processes leave recognizable patterns on the land, and vegetation can change the pattern too.

What is happening?

Different agents shape land in different patterns. Flowing water often carves channels, wind can move fine particles into dunes, ice can scrape and carry large material, and vegetation can slow erosion by holding soil in place.

Why it works that way

Scientists look for repeated patterns because patterns are clues to the process that caused them. A bare slope eroding after rain tells a different story than a stable, plant-covered slope where roots trap soil and slow runoff.

Look for this in the lesson

Students should compare places and ask which Earth process is most likely responsible. The science grows when they defend their choice with pattern evidence instead of guessing.

Ask while you read: Which visible pattern best reveals whether water, wind, ice, or plants shaped the area?

🧪 Activity

Step 1: Smooth four small sections in the tray and label them Water, Wind, Ice, and Vegetation + Water.
Step 2: In the Water section, drip or pour a small amount of water so it runs downhill. Observe channels or piles.
Step 3: In the Wind section, blow gently through a straw or use a fan on low. Observe whether the loose particles shift into drifts or ripples.
Step 4: In the Ice section, drag an ice cube slowly across the surface. Observe grooves, pushed sediment, or smoothing.
Step 5: In the Vegetation + Water section, place paper strips or grass on the surface before adding water. Compare how much sediment moved there versus the bare water section.
Step 6: Record the pattern each agent left and explain which evidence helped you identify it.

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why is vegetation listed with the agents if plants are living things?

Hint: Vegetation changes the rate of erosion by protecting and holding the surface.

Q2: Why do scientists care about patterns rather than one tiny observation alone?

Hint: Patterns make explanations stronger because they show repeated evidence.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Mess control: Use very small amounts of water and a towel under the tray.
  • Reasoning prompt: Ask “What pattern makes you think that?” after every answer.
  • Link forward: The final lesson asks students to use this kind of evidence to explain and protect a changing streambank.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Pattern chart: Answers vary, but water should usually create channels, wind should move loose particles, and ice may push or smooth sediment.
  2. Match the clue: Narrow channel + pile = water; scratches or pushed sediment = ice; less movement under strips = vegetation slows erosion.
  3. Short explanation: Strong responses match a process to a visible pattern and cite evidence.
