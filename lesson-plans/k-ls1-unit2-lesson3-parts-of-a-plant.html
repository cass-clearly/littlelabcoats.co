🌱 Parts of a Plant: Root to Flower 🌸

Life Science — Unit 2, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🌿 Subject: Life Science — Plants & Seeds 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🌻 🔍 🌱
Every plant has four main parts — and each part has its own very important job!
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-LS1-1 — Use observations to describe patterns of what plants and animals (including humans) need to survive.

🎯 The Big Idea

Plants aren't just pretty — they're amazing living machines! Every part of a plant has a job that helps the plant grow, drink water, make food, and create new seeds. In this lesson, your child will dig up a real plant, observe all four parts up close, and discover what each part does. Then they'll set up a colorful experiment to actually watch the stem carry water!

🔬 The Science

Plants have four main parts: roots, a stem, leaves, and a flower. Each part has a specific function that helps the plant survive:

  • 🌾 Roots anchor the plant in soil and absorb water and nutrients.
  • 🪴 Stem acts like a pipe or straw — it carries water and nutrients from the roots up to the rest of the plant.
  • 🍃 Leaves catch sunlight and use it (along with air and water) to make food for the plant — a process called photosynthesis.
  • 🌸 Flower attracts bees and butterflies that help the plant make seeds so new plants can grow.

Today's experiments let kids see two of these jobs in action: they'll observe the roots of a real plant, and watch a celery stem actually transporting colored water right before their eyes!

💡 Key vocabulary: roots, stem, leaves, flower, nutrients, absorb, transport, photosynthesis

📦 Materials

🌿 For the Plant Dig Activity

  • 1 small weed or plant from the yard (dandelion is perfect — deep taproot, all 4 parts visible)
  • A spoon or small garden trowel for digging
  • Sheet of white paper (to lay the plant on and observe)
  • Crayons or colored pencils (to sketch the plant)

🥬 For the Celery Stem Experiment

  • 1 celery stalk (with leaves still on, if possible)
  • 1 clear cup or glass
  • Water
  • Red or blue food coloring (10–15 drops — more is better!)

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Set Up the Celery Experiment FIRST! (3 min)
Before anything else, get your celery experiment going — it needs time to work while you do the rest of the lesson!
  • Fill the cup about halfway with water
  • Add 10–15 drops of food coloring and stir — make it nice and bright
  • Place the celery stalk in the colored water (cut the bottom of the stalk at an angle first for better absorption)
  • Set it in a sunny spot and say: "Let's check on this later and see what happens!"
Step 2 — Warm-Up: What Do Plants Need? (5 min)
While you head outside, talk about what the kids already know:
  • "What do YOU need to survive? Water, food, air, sunlight…"
  • "Do plants need the same things? How do you think they get them?"
  • "Does a plant have a mouth? A stomach? How does it drink water without those things?"
Let kids share their ideas — there are no wrong answers yet!
Step 3 — Dig It Up! Plant Excavation (10–12 min)
Head outside and find a small weed or dandelion. Together:
  • Gently dig around the base, going several inches down to get the full root
  • Carefully pull the whole plant out and shake off excess soil
  • Lay it on the white paper and look at it together
  • Ask: "Can you find all four parts? Where are the roots? The stem? The leaves? Any flowers?"
  • Touch each part — roots feel different from stem, leaves feel different from roots
  • Optional: sketch the plant with crayons, labeling each part
💡 Dandelion tip: Dandelions are ideal because their long taproot makes the root system super obvious. You can see all four parts clearly, and the fluffy seed head is a bonus conversation starter about how seeds spread!
Step 4 — Introduce the Four Parts (5–8 min)
Back inside (or outside on a blanket), go through each plant part:
  • 🌾 Roots: Point to the roots. "These are like the plant's straws AND its anchor. What do you think they do?" (See Discussion Question below)
  • 🪴 Stem: Run a finger up the stem. "This is the highway — water travels all the way from the roots to the leaves through here!"
  • 🍃 Leaves: Hold a leaf up to the light. "Leaves catch sunlight like little solar panels to make the plant's food."
  • 🌸 Flower: "Flowers make seeds so new plants can grow — and bees love them!"
Use the uprooted plant as a visual the whole time.
Step 5 — Check the Celery! (5 min)
Return to your celery experiment. After 30–60 minutes, you should start to see color in the leaves. (After a few hours it'll be very vivid — check again at lunch or dinner!)
  • Look at the leaves — any color yet? What about the stalk?
  • Optional: carefully cut the celery stalk crosswise and look at the tiny colored dots — those are the tubes that carry water!
  • "See how the colored water traveled UP the stem? That's exactly what a real stem does every single day!"
Step 6 — Worksheet & Wrap-Up (5–8 min)
Complete the Student Worksheet (page 2). Kids label the four plant parts on the diagram and complete the job-matching activity. Finish with the discussion question below!

💬 Discussion Question

🗣️ Ask this together:
  • "What job do you think roots do for the plant?"
    ➤ Roots are like straws — they drink up water and nutrients from the soil and send them up through the stem to the rest of the plant! They're also like anchors: they hold the plant in the ground so it doesn't blow away in the wind or wash away in the rain.
  • "Why do you think leaves are green and flat? What's the advantage of that shape?"
    ➤ Flat and wide = more surface area to catch sunlight! Green comes from chlorophyll, the special material inside leaves that actually captures the sun's energy to make food.
  • "If a plant had no roots, what would happen?"
    ➤ It couldn't drink water, so it would dry out and die. It also couldn't stay in the ground — the wind would knock it over! Roots do two important jobs at once.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🌹 White Carnation Upgrade: Try the same food-coloring experiment with a white carnation — the result is even more dramatic and beautiful!
  • ✂️ Split-Color Celery: Carefully split the celery stalk partway up and put each half in a different color of water. What happens?
  • 🗺️ Neighborhood Plant Hunt: Walk around the block and find 5 different plants. Do they all have the same four parts? Can you find roots, stem, leaves, and flowers on each?
  • 🎨 Nature Journal: Press a small leaf between two sheets of paper and rub over it with a crayon (leaf rubbing) — try several different leaf shapes and compare!
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "From Seed to Plant" by Gail Gibbons, or "The Tiny Seed" by Eric Carle
  • 🫘 Bean Check: How are the bean plants from Lesson 2 doing? Any sprouts? Can you identify the stem and leaves on the seedling?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • ⏱️ Start the celery FIRST: The food-coloring experiment needs time to work — set it up at the very beginning so the color has 30–60 minutes to travel up while you do the outdoor plant dig. Check it mid-lesson and again after. Overnight is even more impressive!
  • 🌹 Carnations are even better: If you can grab white carnations from a grocery store, use those instead of celery. The color change is vivid, dramatic, and kids go absolutely wild for it. Still works great with celery if that's what you have.
  • 🌼 Dandelions are the ideal dig plant: Their deep taproot makes the root system super obvious — kids can actually see the whole root structure, and it's hard to miss any of the four parts. Dandelions are everywhere and free!
  • 🫘 Check in on Lesson 2 bean plants: While you're doing this lesson, peek at any beans you planted in Lesson 2. Any sprouts? If a seedling has emerged, use it as a live example — this is a real plant with real parts right in front of them!
  • 📖 Vocabulary to reinforce: roots, stem, leaves, flower, nutrients. Use these words naturally throughout the lesson, not just as definitions.
  • 🔬 The celery cross-section is optional but cool: If your kids are curious, cut the celery crosswise with a sharp knife (adult only) and look at the tiny colored dots in the stalk. Those are the vascular tubes that transport water — a great visual for "pipes inside the stem!"
  • ✅ Assessment: Can the child name all four plant parts? Can they point to each one on a real plant or diagram? Can they tell you one job for each part? That's success for Kindergarten.

✂️ PARENT / TEACHER ANSWER KEY — Remove before giving worksheet to student

🏷️ Part 1: Label the Plant — Answers

  • ① Flower — the yellow bloom at the top
  • ② Leaves — the flat green parts on the sides
  • ③ Stem — the vertical green stalk in the middle
  • ④ Roots — the brown branching parts underground in the soil

🔗 Part 2: Match the Job — Answers

  • 🌾 ROOTS → 💧 Drinks up water from soil & holds the plant in the ground
  • 🪴 STEM → 🚿 Carries water from roots up to the leaves
  • 🍃 LEAVES → ☀️ Catches sunlight to make food for the plant
  • 🌸 FLOWER → 🌸 Makes seeds and attracts bees & butterflies

💡 Deeper Notes for Parents

  • Flower → seeds: Some kids initially think flowers are "just pretty" — reinforce that the flower's real job is reproduction. Bees carry pollen from flower to flower, which lets the plant make seeds. That's how new plants grow!
  • Roots → two jobs: Roots drink AND anchor. Both are correct answers. If a child says both, that's excellent scientific thinking — reinforce it.
  • Leaves → photosynthesis: At Kindergarten level, "catches sunlight to make food" is the right level of detail. If they ask how, you can say: "Leaves have a special ingredient called chlorophyll that turns sunlight, air, and water into sugar — the plant's food!"
  • Stem → vascular tissue: The celery experiment visually demonstrates this! The colored dots in a cross-cut celery stalk are the tiny tubes (xylem) that carry water. You don't need the vocabulary — just point and say "those are the water pipes!"

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Child can name all four plant parts when shown a real plant or diagram.
  • Child can describe at least one job for each part in their own words.
  • Child connects the celery experiment to what stems actually do.
  • Bonus: Child notices that both roots and flowers serve the plant's survival (water + reproduction).
