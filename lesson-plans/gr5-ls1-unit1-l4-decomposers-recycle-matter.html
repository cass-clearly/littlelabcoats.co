Decomposers Recycle Matter

Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs | Unit 1, Lesson 4 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs
🍄🍂
Decomposers help matter move back into the ecosystem.
📋 NGSS Standard:
5-LS2-1 — Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will explain how decomposers break down dead matter and return materials to the environment.

💡 The Big Idea

Decomposers are nature’s recyclers. They help matter move through the ecosystem instead of staying locked in dead material.

🔬 The Science

Decomposers are organisms that break down dead plants, dead animals, and waste into smaller pieces and simpler materials. In many ecosystems, fungi release chemicals that help digest dead matter outside their bodies, while bacteria break it down at a microscopic level. Detritivores such as earthworms and millipedes help by chewing and shredding dead material into smaller pieces, which makes decomposition happen faster.

As decomposers do their work, matter does not disappear. Instead, it is rearranged and returned to the environment. Nutrients from dead matter move back into soil and water, where producers such as plants and algae can use them again to grow. Animals then get that matter by eating plants or by eating other animals. This is why decomposers are an essential part of matter cycling in ecosystems.

A decomposing log, fungi, and leaf litter show how matter is broken down and returned to the ecosystem.

🧪 Investigation

🍂 Decomposer detective challenge: Students inspect a real or pictured example of decay, sort possible decomposers from non-decomposers, then build a matter-cycle model that shows exactly how dead material gets reused in the ecosystem.
Step 1 — Observe decay closely (8–10 min)
Show leaf litter, compost, a fallen log, or the decomposition photo from this lesson. Students record what they notice: color changes, softness, crumbling pieces, fungi, insects, moisture, and smell if appropriate. Ask: What evidence shows this material is changing?
Step 2 — Sort the possible decomposers (8–10 min)
Use the decomposer sort cards to decide which organisms help break down dead matter and which do not. Include debate. Students must justify each choice using evidence, not guesses.
Step 3 — Trace where the matter goes (10–12 min)
Pick one example, such as a dead leaf. Students explain the path: dead leaf → decomposers break it down → nutrients return to soil → plant uses nutrients → animal eats plant. This answers not just where matter goes, but how it gets there.
Step 4 — Build the matter cycle model (10–15 min)
Students create a diagram that includes dead matter, decomposers, soil nutrients, producers, and consumers. They draw arrows and label what is moving through the system.
Step 5 — Explain the ecosystem payoff (8–10 min)
Students explain why ecosystems would not work well without decomposers and what would happen if dead matter never broke down.

💬 Discussion Questions

What do decomposers do?They break down dead material and waste.
How do decomposers help plants?They return nutrients to the environment.
Why is this called recycling matter?The matter gets used again in new parts of the ecosystem.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Go outside and look for evidence of decomposition in a place you can safely explore, such as a leaf pile, garden bed, under a shrub, near a tree, a compost bin, a fallen log, or damp soil. If you do not have access to those, check a houseplant pot, a yard waste bin, old mulch, or photos and videos of forest floor decay. Record what you see, what might be breaking the material down, and what clues tell you matter is being recycled.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Students may know “gross” examples like mold. Use that engagement to reinforce the ecosystem payoff.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample Explanation

Decomposers break down dead matter and return nutrients to soil or water, where producers can use them again. A correct cycle might look like this: dead leaf → fungi, bacteria, and detritivores break it into smaller parts → nutrients return to soil → plant roots absorb nutrients → an herbivore eats the plant → matter continues moving through the ecosystem.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student identifies decomposers correctly.
  • Student explains how decomposition returns nutrients to the environment.
  • Student includes dead matter, decomposers, soil nutrients, producers, and consumers in the cycle model.
  • Student connects decomposition to matter cycling.

Suggested decomposers from the sort cards

  • Correct decomposer examples: mushroom, bacteria
  • Helpful detritivores that support decomposition: earthworm, millipede
  • Not decomposers: hawk, deer, oak tree, rabbit

Sample matter cycle

Dead plant or animal matter → decomposers break it down → nutrients return to soil → producers use nutrients to grow → consumers eat producers or other consumers → waste and dead matter return to decomposers.
