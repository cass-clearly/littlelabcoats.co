Plan a Testable Filter Prototype

Engineering — Clean Water Design Lab | Unit 1, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 50–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Clean Water Design Lab
✏️📐🧠
Grade 5 planning includes labels, variables, and a fair test procedure.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem that reflects a need or a want, includes specified criteria for success and constraints on materials, time, or cost; generate and compare multiple possible solutions; and plan and carry out fair tests with variables controlled.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will create a labeled prototype plan and a fair test procedure that identifies variables to keep the same and one feature to compare.

💡 The Big Idea

A strong engineering plan does more than show what to build. It also shows how the design will be tested fairly.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

Prototype plans should communicate structure and testing decisions clearly enough that someone else could follow them. Grade 5 students should label materials, layer order, and support structures.

Fair tests matter because engineers need trustworthy evidence. If several variables change at once, it becomes difficult to tell why one design performed better than another.

Students should think about controlled variables such as the amount of dirty water, pouring speed, cup size, and observation method.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review the design brief: Revisit criteria, constraints, and the selected idea from Lesson 2.
Step 2 — Draw the prototype: Students create a labeled plan showing the container, layers, and support parts.
Step 3 — Name the variables: Identify what will stay the same in every trial and what feature could change later.
Step 4 — Write a fair test procedure: Students list step-by-step directions for how they will test the prototype.
Step 5 — Peer check for clarity: Another student reads the plan and says whether it is specific enough to follow.

Discussion Questions

Why is a labeled diagram better than a simple sketch?It communicates parts and jobs more clearly.
What could happen if you change two things at once during a test?You would not know which change caused the result.
What are examples of variables we should control?Same water amount, same dirt mixture, same containers, same pouring method.
Why should another person be able to follow your plan?Good engineering plans are clear and reproducible.

Extensions

Extension: Add a prediction section estimating which criterion may be hardest to meet.

Extension: Compare two possible test metrics: a clarity score and a flow-time measurement.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Students often draw a design but skip how it will be tested. Treat the test plan as part of the engineering plan.

Upward-rigor move: Ask students to justify each controlled variable by explaining how it protects the fairness of the test.

1️⃣ Draw and Label Your Prototype — Sample Use

Look for a labeled container, clear layer order, and named materials rather than an unlabeled picture.

2️⃣ Variables to Keep the Same — Sample Ideas

Students might keep the water amount, particle mixture, pouring speed, cup size, and scoring method the same.

3️⃣ Write the Test Procedure — What Strong Work Includes

The procedure should be ordered, specific, and fair enough that another student could repeat it.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student creates a labeled and testable plan.
  • Student identifies controlled variables accurately.
  • Student writes a clear fair-test procedure.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If the plan lacks labels, ask how another engineer would know what each part is.
  • If students list vague steps, prompt them to include measurable actions such as amount and timing.
