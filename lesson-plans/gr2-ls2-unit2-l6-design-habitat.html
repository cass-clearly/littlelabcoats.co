🏗️ Design a Habitat: Build a Home for a Creature

Grade 2 Life Science, Unit 2 — Lesson 6 (Unit Culminating Project)

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 60+ minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Ecosystems 🌱 Unit: 2-LS2 Ecosystems — Culminating Project
📦🌿🐛🏡🔬
Every animal needs the right food, water, shelter, and space — can you build it?
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-LS2-1 + K-2-ETS1-1 — Make observations of plants and animals to compare the diversity of life in different habitats AND ask questions, make observations, and gather information to define a simple problem and design a solution.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Engineer a model habitat that meets the needs of a chosen animal
  • Apply ecosystem knowledge from the whole unit to a real design challenge
  • Explain the purpose of each element in a habitat model

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

  • A shoebox or small cardboard box (the habitat container)
  • Soil or dirt from outside
  • Pebbles or small rocks
  • Grass clippings or dry leaves
  • A few small sticks or twigs
  • A bottle cap with a little water (for a water source)
  • Construction paper and markers (for labels and decorating)

✅ All household or backyard materials — no specialty purchases!

📖 Vocabulary

Engineer A person who designs and builds things to solve problems
Model A small version of something real, used to study and test ideas
Need Something a living thing must have to survive (food, water, shelter, space)
Shelter A safe place that protects an animal from weather and predators
Design A plan for how to build or make something to solve a problem
Criteria The requirements a design must meet to be considered successful

🌍 Background

Every animal has a habitat that meets its needs. A habitat must provide food, clean water, shelter from predators and weather, and enough space. When scientists want to protect an endangered species, they first study its natural habitat and then try to create or preserve places that meet all those same needs.

Engineers design things to solve problems. When you design a habitat model, you are acting as both a scientist (understanding the animal's needs) and an engineer (building something to meet those needs). This kind of thinking — identifying needs, designing solutions, and testing and improving — is called the Engineering Design Process.

Today you will choose one small animal: an earthworm, a ladybug, or a snail. You will find out what it needs, then build a model habitat from everyday household materials. By the end, you will be able to explain why every part of your habitat is there and what animal need it meets.

🐾 Know Your Animal

Read the animal info card for your chosen animal before you start designing:

🪱 EARTHWORM
  • Food: Dead leaves, decaying plant matter, soil particles
  • Water: Moist (not soaking wet) soil
  • Shelter: Underground tunnels in dark soil
  • Space: A few square inches per worm
  • Build tip: Damp soil + torn dry leaves on top for food
🐞 LADYBUG
  • Food: Tiny aphids (plant pests) and some pollen
  • Water: Dew or tiny water droplet
  • Shelter: Under a leaf or bark, protected from rain
  • Space: Very small — a few square inches
  • Build tip: Leaf "roof" + small sticks to climb + grass for aphid habitat
🐌 SNAIL
  • Food: Soft plant matter, algae, tender leaves
  • Water: Moist environment — dries out in sun
  • Shelter: Shady, cool corner (carries its own shell)
  • Space: Small — but needs room to move
  • Build tip: Mist with water + large leaves for shade and food + bottle-cap pond

🔬 Activity: Design and Build Your Habitat

Step 1 — Choose: Pick your animal: earthworm, ladybug, or snail. Write its name on your Student Worksheet.
Step 2 — Research: Read the animal info card above. Fill in the needs chart on your Student Worksheet: what does your animal need for food, water, shelter, and space?
Step 3 — Plan: On the design sketch page in your Student Worksheet, draw a top-down view of your habitat and label what you plan to include and where.
Step 4 — Build: Gather your materials and build the habitat inside the shoebox. Add each element one at a time. As you add each piece, ask: "Which need does this meet: food, water, shelter, or space?"
Step 5 — Label: Make small flag labels (paper folded over a stick or pencil) for every part of your habitat. Each label should say what the element is AND which animal need it meets.
Step 6 — Present: Explain your habitat to a family member. Tell them: what animal lives there, what each part provides, and how you know your animal could survive there.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: What would happen to your animal if one part of the habitat was removed? Pick one part and explain.

Suggested answer: Every part of the habitat serves a need. If you removed the water source, the snail or earthworm would dry out and die. If you removed the leaves, the earthworm would have nothing to eat. If you removed the shelter (the leaf roof), the ladybug would be exposed to predators and weather. Every element is there for a reason — this shows how fragile real habitats are when even one thing changes.

🗣️ Question 2: How is your model habitat the same as a real habitat? How is it different?

Suggested answer: The same: it provides food, water, shelter, and space. Different: it is much smaller, it does not have weather or seasons, it lacks predators and the community of other animals the real animal would interact with. A model is always a simplified version used to test ideas. Scientists use models all the time and always note their limitations — a model helps us understand without being as complex as reality.

🗣️ Question 3: If you could add one more thing to improve your habitat, what would it be and why?

Suggested answer: This is open-ended by design! Students might add more food, a better shelter, other species to make it more realistic, or more space. The key is that students justify their choice by linking it to a specific animal need. This is engineering iteration — engineers are always looking for ways to improve a design after testing it.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • This is the UNIT CULMINATING PROJECT. Give it 60+ minutes and let the student truly own it. Ownership of the animal choice dramatically increases engagement.
  • Let them choose: Earthworm for a child who likes dirt and digging; ladybug for a child interested in insects; snail for a child who likes careful, slow observation. All three options work with backyard materials.
  • If you have the real animal nearby (earthworms in garden soil, ladybugs on plants), consider letting the student observe the real animal before building its model habitat. This connects the model to reality in a powerful way.
  • Engineering Design Process: This lesson hits K-2-ETS1-1 directly. Students define the problem (what does the animal need), design a solution (plan and build the habitat), and evaluate it (does it meet the criteria checklist). Consider doing a brief "test" — if you can find the actual animal, place it in the habitat briefly and observe its behavior.
  • Differentiation: For advanced students, have them research a more complex animal (a specific bird or amphibian) and add complexity. For younger siblings, have them help build while the 2nd grader plans and labels.
  • Photography suggestion: Photograph the finished habitat! It makes a great portfolio piece and motivates students to do their best work. If printing this unit, the photo could be added to a student science portfolio.
  • Unit synthesis connection: Ask students to name the ecosystem their animal is part of. What food chain includes their animal? What would pollinate the plants in their habitat? This lesson is the synthesis of the entire unit — draw connections back to Lessons 1-5.
