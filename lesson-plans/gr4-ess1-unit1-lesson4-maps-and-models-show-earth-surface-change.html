🗺️ Maps and Models Show Earth’s Surface Change

Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 4 of 6

Grade: 4Duration: 50 minutesSubject: Earth Science, Maps & ModelsMaterials: Tray model, crayons, clay
NGSS Standard: 4-ESS2-2 — Analyze and interpret data from maps to describe patterns of Earth’s features.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Create a simple before-and-after map from a top-down view of a stream model.
  • Use map and model evidence to describe where erosion and deposition happened.
  • Explain why maps and models are useful for studying landform change.

📦 Materials

  • Tray with sand or soil
  • Water cup or squeeze bottle
  • Paper and crayons or colored pencils
  • Small amount of clay or playdough
  • Ruler

📖 Vocabulary

map evidenceInformation you can gather from a top-down drawing
modelA smaller or simpler representation used to study something
top viewLooking from above, like a bird flying overhead
patternSomething that repeats or can be noticed in the same kind of evidence
interpretTo use evidence to decide what it means

🔬 Background

Science Snapshot
Maps and models compress large Earth processes into forms we can study and compare.

What is happening?

Maps and models help scientists see landform patterns that are too large or too slow to watch directly. They let us compare locations, track change over time, and test explanations about what shaped the surface.

Why it works that way

A model is useful when it keeps the important relationships, even if it simplifies the real world. In Earth science, maps can show rivers, slopes, elevation, sediment deposits, and places where change is likely to happen next.

Look for this in the lesson

Students should treat maps as evidence tools. The goal is not only reading symbols, but connecting what the map shows to processes such as weathering, erosion, and deposition.

Ask while you read: What clues on the map or model help you infer how the land changed?

🧪 Activity

Step 1: Shape a stream path or small channel in a sand tray. Before adding water, draw a top-view map of the tray on your worksheet. Use blue for the water path and brown for land.
Step 2: Pour water through the same starting point two or three times.
Step 3: Draw a second map of the tray after the water flowed. Add arrows or labels where you see widening, new channels, or piles of sediment.
Step 4: Build a quick clay model of the “after” surface so you can show the height and shape changes in 3-D.
Step 5: Compare your maps and model. Identify one place where erosion happened and one place where deposition happened.
Step 6: Write a short explanation using the map and model as evidence.

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why is a before-and-after map useful even if the drawings are simple?

Hint: It helps you compare change over time in the same place.

Q2: What can a model show that a flat map cannot?

Hint: A model can show height, slope, and 3-D shape more clearly.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Keep the map simple: Students do not need perfect cartography. The goal is evidence-based comparison.
  • Push precision: Encourage wording like “the channel widened on the right side” instead of “it changed.”
  • Cross-unit value: This lesson supports later science work that uses maps, diagrams, and models as evidence.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Maps: Student drawings should show the stream path before and after.
  2. Evidence table: Strong answers name a visible change and connect it to either erosion or deposition.
  3. Explanation: Good responses mention both the evidence and the process, for example, “The channel widened after water flowed, so erosion removed material from the bank.”
