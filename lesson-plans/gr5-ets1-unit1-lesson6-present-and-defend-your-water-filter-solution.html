Present and Defend Your Water Filter Solution

Engineering — Clean Water Design Lab | Unit 1, Lesson 6 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 50–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Clean Water Design Lab
🗣️💧🏁
Grade 5 engineers defend design choices with criteria, evidence, and trade-off reasoning.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem that reflects a need or a want, includes specified criteria for success and constraints on materials, time, or cost; generate and compare multiple possible solutions; and plan and carry out fair tests with variables controlled.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will present a final filter prototype, compare version 1 and version 2, and defend their design choices using evidence from fair tests.

💡 The Big Idea

Engineering is not just building. It also includes defending why a solution is strong, where it still has limits, and what evidence supports the claims.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

Final engineering explanations should connect the entire design story: the problem, the criteria, the constraints, the first test, the redesign, and the evidence from the comparison.

Grade 5 students can begin to talk about trade-offs in a more mature way. A strong design may not be perfect on every criterion, but students should justify why their solution is reasonable based on the evidence collected.

By the end of the unit, students should be able to explain not only what they built, but how engineering reasoning helped them improve it.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review the whole design cycle: Students revisit the problem, criteria, constraints, and both test rounds.
Step 2 — Prepare a design defense: Students organize what changed from version 1 to version 2 and which evidence matters most.
Step 3 — Present the final solution: Students explain the design, the key improvement, and the most important evidence.
Step 4 — Name a trade-off or remaining limitation: Students explain one area where the design still has a weakness or compromise.
Step 5 — Reflect like engineers: Students identify what they would test next if given more time.

Discussion Questions

What makes a design defense stronger than “it worked”?Specific evidence and reasoning about the criteria.
Why compare version 1 and version 2 directly?It shows whether the redesign actually improved the prototype.
Why should engineers admit limits or trade-offs?Real solutions are judged honestly, not as perfect.
What would you test next if you had another round?Students should name a specific future variable or improvement.

Extensions

Extension: Convert the design defense into a short CER paragraph: claim, evidence, reasoning.

Extension: Ask students to compare two different groups’ designs and argue which best met the criteria overall.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Push students away from performance-only talk like “ours was the best.” Require them to cite what the evidence showed.

Upward-rigor move: Invite students to acknowledge a remaining limitation and explain why their design is still reasonable.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Problem and Criteria Review — Strong Response

Students should restate the need, key criteria, and constraints in clear engineering language.

2️⃣ Version 1 vs. Version 2 — Sample Use

Look for a specific change plus evidence such as better clarity, faster flow, or improved stability.

3️⃣ Final Design Defense — Sample Acceptable Answer

“Our design problem was to make model dirty water clearer using limited materials. Version 2 improved because we added a fine filter layer after a coarse layer, which trapped more particles while keeping the structure stable. The evidence is that the filtered water looked clearer than version 1 and the system did not tip. A remaining trade-off is that the flow was slower, but the design met our most important criterion better.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student connects the whole design cycle into one explanation.
  • Student compares version 1 and version 2 with specific evidence.
  • Student acknowledges a limit or trade-off honestly.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If students only celebrate the final result, ask what changed and how they know it improved.
  • If students claim perfection, ask what they would test next with more time.
