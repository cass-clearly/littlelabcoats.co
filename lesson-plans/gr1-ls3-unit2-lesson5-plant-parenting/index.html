🌻🌱🍎 Plant Parenting

Parents and Offspring Unit, Lesson 5

📚 Grade: 1st Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science 📋 NGSS Standard: 1-LS3-1
🌻🌱🍎
Every seed holds a tiny recipe for a new plant, passed down from its parent. What will grow from yours?
📋 NGSS Standard:
1-LS3-1 — Make observations to construct an evidence-based account that young plants and animals are like, but not exactly like, their parents.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Recognize that plants also pass traits to offspring through seeds.
  • Observe that plant offspring resemble the parent plant in shape, color, and type.
  • Understand that seeds carry "instructions" (DNA) for growing a new plant just like the parent.

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need (Household Only)

  • 2-3 different seeds from your kitchen (apple seeds, sunflower seeds from a snack bag, dried beans)
  • Paper plates (one per seed type)
  • Magnifying glass (optional, but fun!)
  • Paper and pencil
  • One small cup or container with soil (for planting one seed at the end)

✅ All seeds can come from your kitchen pantry! No purchasing required.

📖 Vocabulary

🌰 Seed
A small structure that can grow into a new plant, made by the parent plant.
🔍 Trait
An inherited feature passed from parent plant to offspring plant.
📋 Instructions
The information inside a seed that tells it how to grow. Scientists call this DNA. Think of it like a recipe!
🌻 Parent Plant
The plant that made the seed. The offspring plant will look like it.

📚 Background (Read Aloud to Student)

We have been learning that animal offspring look like their parents. But what about plants? Plants do not have babies the way animals do. Instead, they make seeds. Every seed is like a tiny package with two things inside: a baby plant (called an embryo) and a set of instructions for how to grow. Those instructions are called DNA, but you can think of them as a recipe. Just like a recipe for chocolate chip cookies will always make chocolate chip cookies, a sunflower seed's recipe will always grow a sunflower.

This is why, when you plant a sunflower seed, you get a sunflower, not a rose. The instructions in the seed are specific to the parent plant. The new plant (the offspring) will have the same traits as its parent: the same leaf shape, the same flower color, the same height, the same type of fruit. Traits are passed from the parent plant to the seed to the new plant, just like they are passed from parent animals to their offspring.

Here is something wonderful to discover today: if you carefully peel open a dried bean or a large seed, you can actually see the tiny baby plant waiting inside! This tiny plant is called the embryo, and it has everything it needs to start growing. All it needs is water, soil, and sunlight, and those hidden instructions will take over and build a brand new plant.

🌱 Try it! Soak a dried bean in water overnight, then peel it open the next day. You can see the baby plant (embryo) inside with a tiny root and tiny leaf shapes already formed!

🔬 Procedure

Step 1 — Gather and Observe Seeds
Collect 2-3 different seeds from your kitchen and place each type on its own paper plate. Look carefully at each seed before touching them. How are they different? Notice size, shape, color, and texture.
Step 2 — Seed Detective 🔍
For each seed, draw it on the left side of your paper. On the right side, draw the plant you predict will grow from it. Label each drawing with the seed name and plant name.
  • 🍎 Apple seed grows into... an apple tree!
  • 🌻 Sunflower seed grows into... a sunflower!
  • 🫘 Dried bean grows into... a bean plant!
Step 3 — Could It Grow Into Something Else?
Ask together: "If you plant a sunflower seed, could it ever grow into a rose? Why not?" Give the student time to reason through it. Then introduce the instructions idea: the seed only knows how to follow the sunflower recipe!
Step 4 — The Instructions Inside
Introduce this idea: "Inside every seed is all the information it needs to make a new plant just like its parent. We call these instructions DNA, but you can think of them like a recipe. The seed always follows its own recipe."
Step 5 — Seed Investigation (Magnifying Glass)
Look at each seed up close with a magnifying glass if available. Compare sizes and textures. Now carefully peel open a dried bean to see the tiny plant inside (the embryo). Look for the tiny root and leaf shapes. This is the baby plant!
Step 6 — Plant Your Seed! 🌱
Choose one seed and plant it in a small cup of soil. Water it lightly. Place it in a sunny spot. Record the date and your prediction in the seed journal on your worksheet. Check back in 2 weeks!

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: If you plant a sunflower seed, can you get a rose? Why not?

No! The instructions inside the sunflower seed only know how to make a sunflower. The seed follows its recipe, and a sunflower recipe only makes sunflowers. It cannot follow a rose recipe because it does not have one!

🗣️ Question 2: What traits does a baby plant inherit from its parent plant?

Leaf shape, flower color, height, the type of fruit it makes, the shape of its seeds. Everything about the plant comes from the instructions in the seed. The new plant is programmed to look like its parent before it even starts growing.

🗣️ Question 3: How is a seed like an egg?

Both contain a baby organism inside, protected by a shell or outer coat. Both have all the starting information and initial nutrition to begin growing. A seed even has a food supply inside (called the cotyledon) to feed the baby plant until it can make its own food from sunlight!

🗣️ Question 4: Why might two sunflowers from the same parent plant look slightly different from each other?

Just like human children from the same parents, plant offspring can have small variations. One might be a little taller, or have slightly different petal colors. This is what makes each living thing unique, and it is a normal part of inheritance.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Sourcing seeds from your pantry: Apple seeds (from a cut apple), dried kidney or pinto beans from the bag, sunflower seeds from a snack pack (raw, unroasted work best), or even popcorn kernels (a corn variety). Unroasted seeds are much more likely to germinate.

If seeds are unavailable: Have the student draw seeds and parent plants from memory or description. The observation steps can be done imaginatively: "Imagine you are holding a tiny sunflower seed. What do you notice?" The conceptual learning still happens.

Caring for the planted seed: Plant in a sunny window. Water when the top layer of soil feels dry (roughly every 2-3 days for a small cup). Beans sprout within 5-10 days. Sunflower seeds sprout in 7-14 days. Apple seeds take much longer and need cold stratification, so stick to beans and sunflower seeds for this activity.

Connecting to DNA for Grade 1: Use the "recipe" analogy consistently. You do not need to explain the chemistry. The key idea is: the instructions are inside the seed before it even touches soil, and those instructions were put there by the parent plant. That is inheritance.

The bean embryo dissection: Soak a dried bean in water for several hours or overnight before the lesson. When soft, it peels open easily to reveal the embryo inside. This is a genuinely magical moment for young students. Point out the two halves of the bean (cotyledons, the food supply) and the tiny plant tucked between them.
