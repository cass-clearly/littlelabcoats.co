🧲 Magnets Pull Without Touching 🧲

Physical Science — Forces & Motion | Unit 1, Lesson 3 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🧲 Subject: Physical Science🎒 Kit Lesson
🧲📎
Magnets can make some objects move even when they are not touching them.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-PS2-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence of the effects of balanced and unbalanced forces on the motion of an object.

💡 The Big Idea

Some forces work through contact, but magnetic force can act from a short distance. Magnets can pull some materials without touching them.

🔬 The Science

A magnet creates an invisible magnetic force around it. This force can pull some materials, especially iron and steel, even when the magnet is not touching them yet. That makes magnetism a useful example of a force that acts from a distance.

Not every material responds to magnets. Testing many objects helps students gather evidence and look for patterns about which materials are magnetic and which are not.

Key vocabulary:
magnetmagnetic forceattractmotionevidencematerial

📦 Materials

  • 2 bar magnets or refrigerator magnets
  • 6–10 test objects such as paperclips, foil, plastic spoon, coin, craft stick, fabric, key, twist tie, eraser
  • Try to include a mixed set: one clearly magnetic object (like a paperclip), one metal object that may not be magnetic (like some coins), and one non-metal object.
  • 1 sheet of paper or index card
  • Pencil
  • Printed Student Worksheet

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Make Predictions (5 min)
Look at the test objects and predict which ones the magnet will pull. Record your predictions first.
Step 2 — Test Without Touching (10 min)
Hold the magnet close to each object without touching it. Watch for any movement. Record whether the object was pulled or not.
Step 3 — Try Through Paper (8 min)
Place a paperclip under a sheet of paper. Move the magnet above the paper. Notice that the paperclip can still move even though the magnet is not touching it directly.
Step 4 — Sort the Results (10 min)
Sort your test objects into magnetic and not magnetic groups. Ask what pattern you notice about the materials that were attracted.
Step 5 — Explain the Evidence (10 min)
Use your chart to explain how magnetism shows that a force can act without touching.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: Which objects were pulled by the magnet?Look for: objects made from iron or steel, such as paperclips or some keys.
🗣️ Question 2: How do you know the magnet was exerting a force?Look for: the object moved toward the magnet even before it was touched.
🗣️ Question 3: Did every material respond to the magnet?Look for: No. Only some materials were magnetic.
🗣️ Question 4: Why was the paper test important?Look for: It showed the magnetic force could act through the paper without direct contact.

✨ Extensions

  • 🏠 Magnet Hunt: Walk around the house and test safe objects to find more magnetic and non-magnetic materials.
  • 📝 Pattern Sentence: Write a sentence explaining what types of materials were usually magnetic.
  • 🧲 Two Magnets: If you have two magnets, test whether they pull together or push apart.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Safety note: Keep magnets away from electronics, credit cards, and anything medically sensitive.
  • Choose a mixed test set: Include one object you expect to be magnetic, one metal object that may not be magnetic, and one clearly non-metal object so the pattern is easier to see.
  • Mixed results are useful: If a key, coin, or household metal item gives an unexpected result, treat that as good science evidence to talk about rather than a lesson failure.
  • Misconception check: Students often think all metals are magnetic. Let the evidence show that only some are.
  • Assessment check: Students should explain that the object's motion is evidence of a force acting from a distance.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: This page is for parent/teacher reference. Student wording may vary if the science idea is correct.

Part 1

Predictions vary. Paperclips and some steel objects should be attracted. Plastic, wood, fabric, and many coins should not be.

Part 2

  • Objects that were magnetic usually contained iron or steel.
  • The investigation shows a force can act without touching because the object moved before direct contact happened.

What Success Looks Like

  • Student can describe magnetism as a force that can act from a distance.
  • Student can identify that only some materials are magnetic.
  • Student uses observations as evidence.

Troubleshooting / Misconceptions

  • If a student says all metal is magnetic, compare results from a paperclip and a coin.
  • If a key or coin gives a surprising result, remind students that household objects can vary and that the evidence still helps us build the pattern.
  • If an object response is weak, move the magnet closer and repeat the test carefully.
