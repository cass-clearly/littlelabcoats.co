Designing for Local Weather

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
🏡🌦️📐
A good design solution should fit the actual weather problem in a place.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS3-1 — Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare possible design solutions for a local weather hazard and explain which one best fits the problem and conditions.

💡 The Big Idea

Not every solution works equally well for every place. A strong solution matches the local hazard, local conditions, and the kind of impact a community wants to reduce.

🔬 The Science

Engineers and communities design gutters, shade structures, drainage systems, wind barriers, snow plans, and shelters to reduce harm from weather. A useful solution should match the actual hazard it is trying to reduce.

In this lesson, students compare more than one solution and think about constraints. This helps them prepare for Lesson 6, where they defend which design has the most merit.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review one local hazard (5 min)
Choose a hazard such as flooding, strong sun/heat, snow, or wind.
Step 2 — Compare possible solutions (10 min)
Look at two or three ideas that might reduce the impact.
Step 3 — Discuss criteria and constraints (10 min)
Talk about what the solution must do and what limits the design.
Step 4 — Rank the solutions (10 min)
Students record which solution fits the local problem best and why.
Step 5 — Share a design claim (10 min)
Students explain which design seems strongest and what evidence supports that choice.

Discussion Questions

Why might one solution work better in one place than another?Local weather conditions and hazards are different.
What makes a design solution strong?It reduces the hazard and fits the real problem.
Why should students compare more than one solution?Comparison helps them defend which solution has more merit.
How do criteria help when choosing a design?Criteria define what the solution needs to do well.

Extensions

Extension: Draw a quick redesign that would work even better for your chosen local weather hazard.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep the engineering tied to the Earth Science context by asking “What hazard is this solving?” in every comparison.

Important misconception to catch: Students may choose the biggest or fanciest design instead of the one that best matches the local weather problem.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Compare Design Solutions — Sample Acceptable Answers

Answers will vary, but each row should connect a design to a specific hazard and explain how that design could reduce harm.

2️⃣ Best Fit for Local Weather — Sample Response

Students should choose the design that best matches the local hazard and conditions, not just the most complicated one.

3️⃣ Explain Your Choice — Sample Response

“This solution is a good fit because it moves water away from the building during heavy rain.” or “This shade structure is a good fit because our area has strong sun and heat in summer.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student compares more than one solution.
  • Student connects the chosen solution to the correct local hazard.
  • Student explains why the solution fits the local conditions.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student names a solution with no hazard, ask “What problem is this solving?”
  • If a student chooses a design only because it looks impressive, ask whether it matches the actual local weather problem.
