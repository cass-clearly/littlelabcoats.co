Weather Hazards and Impacts

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 4 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
⛈️🌪️🏠
A weather event becomes a hazard when it can harm people, places, or living things.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS3-1 — Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will identify weather-related hazards and explain how those hazards can affect people, homes, land, and transportation.

💡 The Big Idea

Strong wind, heavy rain, heat, ice, or snow can become hazards when they affect safety, homes, roads, crops, or other parts of a community.

🔬 The Science

A hazard is not just an interesting weather event. It is a weather condition that can cause damage or make life harder for people, places, or living things.

Scientists and communities study hazards so they can prepare and reduce harm. In this lesson, students connect each hazard to its likely impact instead of stopping at the weather word alone.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Name common weather hazards (5 min)
Invite students to list storms or weather events that can cause problems.
Step 2 — Connect hazard to impact (10 min)
Match each event to what might happen to roads, homes, trees, or people.
Step 3 — Discuss local examples (10 min)
Talk about which hazards are more likely in your area and why they matter.
Step 4 — Complete the impact chart (10 min)
Students write which hazard causes which impact and explain the connection.
Step 5 — Share one cause/effect explanation (10 min)
Students complete a sentence such as “Heavy rain can cause flooding because…”

Discussion Questions

When does weather become a hazard?When it can cause harm or dangerous conditions.
How can one hazard affect more than one part of a community?It can affect roads, homes, crops, power, and people at the same time.
Why is it important to describe the impact clearly?That helps people prepare and choose a good solution later.
How are hazards connected to local weather patterns?Some hazards are more likely where certain weather patterns happen often.

Extensions

Extension: Make a “hazard + impact” mini poster for one local weather event your family might actually prepare for.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep pushing for cause/effect language such as “This hazard matters because it can…”

Important misconception to catch: Students may think a hazard is always the same as a disaster. Clarify that a hazard is a risky condition that can lead to harm.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Match Hazard to Impact — Sample Acceptable Answers

Flood: can affect homes and roads because water can cover land and buildings. Blizzard: can affect travel because snow and ice make roads dangerous. Heat Wave: can affect people and plants because very high temperatures can be harmful. Strong Wind: can damage trees, roofs, and power lines.

2️⃣ Which Hazard Is Local? — Sample Response

Answers will vary by location, but students should connect the local hazard to real weather conditions in their area.

3️⃣ Cause + Effect — Sample Response

“Heavy rain can be a hazard because it can cause flooding.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student connects each hazard to a specific impact.
  • Student explains why the impact happens.
  • Student uses clear cause/effect wording instead of only listing weather words.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student names a hazard but no impact, ask “What could happen because of that weather?”
  • If a student names an impact but not the cause, ask “Which weather hazard would create that problem?”
