Weather Hazards and Safe Solutions

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 4 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
⛈️🌪️🏠
Weather hazards can cause harm, but good design solutions can help keep people and places safer.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS3-1 — Make a claim about the merit of a design solution that reduces the impacts of a weather-related hazard.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare weather-related hazards and make a claim about which design solution best reduces the impact of a specific hazard in a community.

💡 The Big Idea

Weather hazards matter because they can cause harm, and communities can design tools, structures, or plans that reduce that harm.

🔬 The Science

Science Snapshot
A good design solution lowers the harm a weather hazard can cause.

What is happening?

Weather hazards can hurt people, animals, plants, homes, and roads. Scientists and engineers look at the hazard, predict the harm it could cause, and think of ways to reduce that harm.

Why solutions matter

Not every solution works equally well. A design solution should match the hazard. Sandbags help with floodwater, while a lightning safety plan helps people respond to thunderstorms.

Look for this in the lesson

Students should compare two possible solutions, then explain which one does a better job reducing the hazard's impact and why.

Ask while you read: Which solution best fits the hazard, and what evidence supports that choice?

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Match the cards (5–10 min)
Lay out the hazard cards and impact cards. Students match each hazard to an impact card and explain their reasoning.
Step 2 — Identify the hazard (5 min)
Use the matched cards to name the hazard and describe what could be harmed.
Step 3 — Brainstorm possible solutions (10 min)
For each hazard, use the design solution cards or list two possible ways a community could reduce harm. Examples: sandbags, storm drains, shade structures, warning systems, staying indoors, or stronger roofs.
Step 4 — Compare two designs (10 min)
Pick one hazard and compare two design solutions. Ask: Which one matches the problem better? Which one protects more people, places, or living things?
Step 5 — Make a claim with evidence (10 min)
Students complete a claim sentence such as: For flooding, sandbags are better than umbrellas because they block or slow the water before it reaches homes.
Step 6 — Think locally (10 min)
Students choose one hazard that could happen in their area and explain which design solution would help most there.
Step 7 — Share and defend (5–10 min)
Students share their claim and support it with evidence about the hazard and the design solution.

💬 Discussion Questions

What makes a weather event a hazard?A weather event becomes a hazard when it can cause harm to people, living things, or property.
Why might one solution work better than another?A strong solution matches the hazard and reduces the most important harm.
How does evidence help you choose a solution?Evidence helps students explain why a solution actually fits the problem instead of just sounding good.
Why can the best solution depend on the place?Different communities face different hazards, so the best response depends on local weather and local needs.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Design a quick “community safety card” that names one local hazard, one design solution, and one reason that solution helps.

👩🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Push students past naming hazards. Keep asking, Which solution is better, and why?

Important misconception to catch: Students may think any response counts as a design solution. Clarify that the solution should actually reduce the impact of the hazard.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Hazard, harm, and solution — Sample Acceptable Answers

Flood: may harm homes or roads; solution: sandbags, storm drains, or raised barriers. Blizzard: may harm travel and safety; solution: snow plows, road treatment, or shelter plans. Heat Wave: may harm people, pets, and plants; solution: shade, cooling centers, or water access. Strong Wind: may harm roofs, trees, and power lines; solution: stronger roof design, storm shutters, or securing loose objects.

2️⃣ Which solution is better? — Sample Response

Answers vary, but the stronger response clearly matches the solution to the hazard, explains how it helps, and explains why it reduces harm better than the alternative.

3️⃣ Make a claim with evidence — Sample Response

“For flooding, sandbags are the better solution because they help block or slow water before it reaches buildings.”

4️⃣ Local connection — Sample Response

Answers will vary by location, but students should name a realistic local hazard and a matching solution.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student connects a weather hazard to likely harm.
  • Student proposes a reasonable design solution.
  • Student makes a claim about which solution is better and supports it with evidence.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student only names a hazard, ask “What problem does that create?”
  • If a student names a solution without evidence, ask “How does it reduce the harm?”
