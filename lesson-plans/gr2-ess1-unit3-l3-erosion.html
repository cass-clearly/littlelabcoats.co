💧 Water Shapes the Land

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 3 of 6

Grade: 2 Duration: 45 minutes Subject: Earth Science, Erosion Materials: Baking pan, sand/dirt, water, pencil
NGSS Standard: 2-ESS2-1 — Compare multiple solutions designed to slow or prevent wind or water from changing the shape of the land.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define erosion as the movement of soil and rock by water or wind
  • Observe how water shapes a sand hill model
  • Understand that real erosion happens slowly over millions of years

📦 Materials

  • A baking pan or cookie sheet with a rim (to contain the mess)
  • Sand or dirt (about 2 cups)
  • A small cup or pitcher of water
  • A pencil for making channels
  • Paper for sketching before/after

📖 Vocabulary

erosion The movement of soil and rock from one place to another by water, wind, or ice
weathering The breaking down of rocks into smaller pieces by water, wind, or temperature
sediment Tiny pieces of rock, sand, or soil that are carried and dropped by water
channel A path or groove carved by flowing water
deposition When water slows down and drops the sediment it was carrying

🔬 Background

Erosion is one of the most powerful forces shaping Earth's surface. Erosion happens when water, wind, or ice moves pieces of rock and soil from one place to another. Over millions of years, erosion carved the Grand Canyon (by the Colorado River), created beaches (by ocean waves), and wore down ancient mountains into rolling hills. What looks like dramatic change to us took unimaginably long periods of time in nature.

Water erosion starts when raindrops hit the soil and loosen tiny particles. Then the water runs downhill, carrying those particles with it. Wherever the water slows down, it drops (deposits) the sediment. This is called deposition. Over time, this cycle of erosion and deposition sculpts entire landscapes. Rivers twist and turn because water always finds the easiest path around obstacles, carving new channels as it goes.

Erosion can be a problem for people too. Farmers lose topsoil when heavy rain washes it away. Coastlines can crumble as waves erode cliffs. Engineers and scientists work together to find solutions, like planting trees (roots hold soil), building retaining walls, and adding rocks or barriers to slow water. We will explore these solutions in Lesson 6!

🧪 Activity: Erosion Experiment

Step 1: Set your baking pan on a table and put a folded towel or book under one end to make a gentle slope.
Step 2: Scoop the sand or dirt into the center/upper part of the pan and shape it into a rounded hill about 4-5 inches tall.
Step 3: Sketch your hill in the "Before" box on the worksheet.
Step 4: Slowly pour a cup of water from the top of the hill. Watch carefully where the water goes and where the sand moves.
Step 5: After the water settles, observe and sketch the "After" picture. Notice: where did sand move to? Did channels form? Where did sand pile up at the bottom?
Step 6: Rebuild the hill. This time, use your finger or a pencil to press a shallow channel down one side of the hill before pouring water.
Step 7: Pour water at the top again. Does the water follow your channel? Sketch this result too.
Step 8: Discuss: What did you observe? How does this model compare to a real river?

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why do rivers twist and turn instead of going in a straight line?

Hint: Water always flows toward the lowest point. When it hits a harder piece of rock or raised land, it goes around it. Over time, the bends get bigger and bigger. This is called meander formation. The Mississippi River has huge, looping meanders that have changed dramatically just in the past few hundred years.

Q2: How do mountains get smaller over millions of years, even without volcanoes?

Hint: Rain, frost, wind, and ice are all slowly breaking rocks into smaller pieces (weathering) and carrying them downhill (erosion). Even a tiny amount of erosion every year adds up to enormous change over millions of years. The Appalachian Mountains in the eastern USA used to be as tall as the Rockies. They have been eroding for hundreds of millions of years.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Mess alert! This can get messy. Set up the baking pan on a protected surface or outside. A towel under the pan is helpful.
  • Sand vs. dirt: Sand works better than regular dirt for this activity because the erosion patterns are more visible. However, dirt works fine too.
  • Key concept: The pencil channel step is important. It shows that water will follow the path of least resistance. This is the key concept for understanding rivers.
  • Save the setup! This lesson connects directly to L6 (Engineering: Slow the Erosion). Save the baking pan setup. You will use it again!

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Before and After: "Before" should show a rounded hill shape. "After" should show the hill flattened or carved, with sand spread toward the bottom of the pan. Channels or grooves in the sand are a great observation to include.
  2. Sentence completion: Water erosion happens when water moves soil (or sand/sediment) from one place to another.
  3. Observation questions: Accept reasonable observations. Good answers: "The water ran down the steepest side," "Sand piled up at the bottom of the pan," "The water followed the channel I made instead of spreading out."
  4. Bonus: Accept any bird's eye drawing that shows a winding/curving river with land on either side. Extra credit for showing wider areas where the river bends.
