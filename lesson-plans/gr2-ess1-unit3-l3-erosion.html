Water Shapes the Land

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 3

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 50 minutes 🔬 Subject: Earth Science: Earth's Changes 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
💧🏔️🌊🏜️
Water is one of the most powerful forces on Earth! Even tiny raindrops can move mountains, slowly.
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-ESS2-1: Compare multiple solutions designed to slow or prevent wind or water from changing the shape of the land.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Define erosion as the movement of rock and soil by water (or wind).
  • Model water erosion by pouring water on a sand hill.
  • Observe how water carries sediment and deposits it elsewhere.

💡 Background

Water is one of Earth's most powerful shapers. When rain falls or rivers flow, they carry tiny bits of rock and soil called sediment. This process of wearing away and moving material is called erosion. The Grand Canyon was carved by the Colorado River over millions of years! When sediment stops moving and settles, it is called deposition. Rivers, oceans, glaciers, and even wind all erode Earth's surface, and all of Earth's landforms have been shaped by these forces.

💡 Key vocabulary: erosion, weathering, sediment, deposition, runoff

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

  • A baking pan or cookie sheet (to contain the mess)
  • Sand or dirt (about 2 to 3 cups)
  • Water in a small cup for pouring
  • A small container to collect the water that flows off
  • Optional: a straw for blowing (wind erosion demonstration)

✅ All household items, nothing to buy!

📖 Vocabulary

erosionthe process of water or wind wearing away and moving rock and soil
weatheringthe breaking down of rock by water, wind, ice, or plants
sedimentsmall particles of rock, sand, or soil carried by water or wind
depositionwhen sediment settles and builds up in a new location
runoffwater that flows over the surface of the land and does not soak in

🔬 Procedure

Step 1 (5 min): Introduction: Ask: 'How do you think the Grand Canyon formed?' Show a picture if available. Introduce the word erosion.
Step 2 (10 min): Build the Hill: Pile sand or dirt into a hill in the center of the baking pan. Pack it gently so it holds its shape.
Step 3 (10 min): Simulate Rain: Hold the cup above the hill and slowly pour water. Observe: where does the water go? What happens to the sand? Does the hill change shape?
Step 4 (10 min): Observe and Record: Where did the sediment go? (It should move downhill and deposit at the base or in the pan.) Draw the before and after in the worksheet.
Step 5 (10 min): Wind Erosion: Let the sand dry slightly, then gently blow through a straw at the hill. Observe: wind also moves sediment! Compare wind erosion to water erosion.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Q1: Where did the sand go when water was poured on the hill? What does this tell us?

Suggested answer: The water carried the sand particles downhill and deposited them at the base of the hill (or flowed into the pan). This is erosion: water moved the sand from a high place to a lower place. The same thing happens when rivers carry sediment to the ocean.

🗣️ Q2: How do you think people could protect a hillside from erosion?

Suggested answer: Planting grass or trees on a hillside helps! Plant roots hold soil in place, preventing water from washing it away. Retaining walls, rocks, and barriers also slow erosion. We will engineer an erosion solution in Lesson 6!

🚀 Extensions

  • 🏔️ Grand Canyon Comparison: Look up photos of the Grand Canyon and discuss how the Colorado River carved it over millions of years.
  • 🌱 Roots Hold Soil: Try the experiment again with a piece of grass sod (if available). Does grass slow the erosion?
  • 📚 Book Connection: 'Rocks: Hard, Soft, Smooth, and Rough' by Natalie Myra Rosinsky

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Setup tip: Do this activity in the kitchen or outside to manage the mess. A cookie sheet with raised edges works great.
  • Erosion vs. weathering: Weathering is the breaking down of rock in place. Erosion is the movement of those broken pieces. Both happen together.
  • Real-world connection: Farmers plant cover crops and build terraces on hillsides to prevent soil erosion. Coastal engineers build seawalls to prevent ocean erosion.
