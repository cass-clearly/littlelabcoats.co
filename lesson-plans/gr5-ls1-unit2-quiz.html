✍️ Short Response — Use Evidence

Support an argument that plants get the materials they need for growth chiefly from air and water, not from soil alone. Use evidence from the unit.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Use the quiz and short response together: recognition plus explanation gives a stronger picture of understanding.
  • Good evidence sounds specific: look for at least one water example, one air/model example, and a clear reasoned explanation.
  • Fast grading tip: scan for whether the student uses unit vocabulary correctly and connects evidence to their claim.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Parent note: Student wording may vary if the science reasoning is correct, but the answer key below should read like concrete content answers, not repeated lesson titles.

Multiple-Choice Answer Key

  1. Air and water are material inputs for growth, while sunlight provides energy and soil helps with support and minerals.
  2. The colored-water investigation showed water entering the plant and moving through stems and leaves.
  3. Plants use carbon dioxide from the air as part of building new matter.
  4. Soil supports roots and provides minerals, but it is not the chief source of plant growth matter.
  5. A strong model shows water entering roots, carbon dioxide entering leaves, and sunlight labeled separately as energy.
  6. A strong argument combines water evidence, air-input evidence, and the clarification that soil is not the chief source.
  7. Using evidence from observations, tests, or models to support explanations
  8. To make hidden relationships easier to see and explain

Short Response — What Success Looks Like

  • Student states the argument clearly that air and water are the chief material sources.
  • Student includes at least one water evidence piece and one air/model evidence piece from the unit.
  • Student correctly distinguishes soil as support/mineral source and sunlight as energy.

Short Response — Sample Acceptable Answer

Our celery investigation showed that water entered the plant and moved through its structures. Our plant-input model showed carbon dioxide from the air entering the leaves while sunlight provided energy. Soil helps support roots and provides minerals, but most of the material for growth comes chiefly from air and water.
