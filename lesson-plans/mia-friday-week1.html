📊 Weather Tracker 10 min

Math — Mean, Median & Data Graphing

You've been watching the weather all week — now it's time to crunch the numbers! 🌤️📈 Record each day's high temperature, then calculate the mean (average) and median (middle number). Finally, graph the trend!

  1. Fill in the high temperature for each day on the worksheet below.
  2. Add all five temps together, then divide by 5 — that's the mean.
  3. Put the temps in order from lowest to highest. The middle number is the median.
  4. Plot each day's temp on the graph and connect the dots to see the trend! 📈
✅ Quick Reminder

Mean: Add all numbers → divide by how many numbers there are.
Example: 50 + 55 + 48 + 60 + 52 = 265 ÷ 5 = 53°

Median: Put numbers in order → pick the middle one.
Example: 48, 50, 52, 55, 60 → Median = 52°

💡 Meteorologist Tip: Is the trend going UP ↗️, DOWN ↘️, or ZIGZAGGING ↗️↘️? Write a sentence about what the weather did this week!

🔍 Mini Research Project 15 min

Research — Using Multiple Sources

Time to become an expert on something cool! 🧑‍🔬 Pick any animal, country, or famous person that interests you. Your mission: find info from at least 2 different sources and combine them into one awesome paragraph.

  1. Choose your topic — something you're curious about!
  2. Find 2 or more sources (a book + a website, two websites, an encyclopedia + a video, etc.).
  3. Write down 3–4 interesting facts from each source.
  4. Combine the best facts into one paragraph in your own words.
✅ Example — Combining Two Sources

Topic: Red Pandas

Source 1 (book): Red pandas live in the bamboo forests of China and Nepal. They use their bushy tails as blankets to stay warm.

Source 2 (website): Red pandas are endangered — fewer than 10,000 are left in the wild. They eat mostly bamboo but also snack on berries and insects.

Combined paragraph: "Red pandas are adorable but endangered animals that live in the bamboo forests of China and Nepal. Fewer than 10,000 remain in the wild. They mostly eat bamboo but also enjoy berries and insects. One fun fact: they use their fluffy tails as blankets to keep warm in the cold mountains!"

💡 Research Tip: Using your own words doesn't mean changing one word — it means understanding the facts and explaining them like you'd tell a friend!

🧠 Connect Two Texts 10 min

Reading — Comparing & Making Connections

Great thinkers don't just read one thing — they find connections between ideas! 🔗 Take a book you're reading and compare it to something else: a movie, an article, another book, or even a song. Find a deeper connection between them.

  1. Pick the book you're currently reading (or recently finished).
  2. Think of something else — a movie, show, article, or another book — that reminds you of it.
  3. Find one deeper connection: same theme? Similar character? Same lesson?
  4. Explain the connection on the worksheet.
✅ Example — A Deeper Connection

Text 1: Wonder by R.J. Palacio

Text 2: The movie Inside Out

Connection: Both stories are about how hard it is to fit in and be yourself. Auggie (in Wonder) tries to be brave at a new school even though people stare, and Riley (in Inside Out) struggles with being the "new kid" after moving. Both show that it's okay to feel sad and that true friends accept you as you are.

👆 Notice: It's not just "they're both about kids" — it's a specific theme they share.

✏️ Read Aloud & Revise 10 min

Writing — Self-Editing & Revision

Writers don't just write — they revise! 🔄 Pick something you wrote this week (a freewrite, a journal entry, anything!) and read it out loud. Your ears catch mistakes your eyes miss. Then fix anything that sounds off.

  1. Pick one piece of writing from this week.
  2. Read it out loud — slowly, like you're reading to an audience.
  3. Listen for anything that sounds confusing, choppy, or "off."
  4. Use the revision checklist to make it even better!
💡 Pro Writer Tip: Try reading it to a stuffed animal, pet, or family member. If they look confused (or if the dog tilts its head 🐕), that part probably needs fixing!
