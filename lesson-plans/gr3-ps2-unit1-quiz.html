✍️ Short Response — Use Evidence

Pick one idea from the unit and explain it using evidence from an investigation or design test.

Prompt: Explain how evidence from the unit shows one of these ideas: balanced vs unbalanced forces, stronger pushes changing motion, magnets acting without touching, or version 2 improving a design.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Use the quiz and short response together: The multiple-choice section checks recognition, while the short response checks whether the student can explain an idea with evidence.
  • Good evidence sounds specific: Look for references to repeated trials, measured distances, magnetic test objects, or version-1 vs version-2 comparisons.
  • Shell parity note: This quiz now mirrors the lesson pattern more closely by including parent-facing support and an evidence-writing task.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: This page is for parent/teacher reference. Student wording may vary if the science idea is correct.

Multiple-Choice Answer Key

  1. It does not change motion because the forces match.
  2. A push or a pull.
  3. To get better evidence and check for a pattern.
  4. Paperclip.
  5. The object moves toward the magnet before contact.
  6. They attract many iron or steel objects but not most other materials.
  7. Use the evidence to improve the design.
  8. My strong pushes usually moved the car farther than my gentle pushes.

Short Response — What Success Looks Like

  • Student states a correct science or engineering idea from the unit.
  • Student uses specific evidence such as repeated push trials, a magnetic/non-magnetic object pattern, or a version-1 vs version-2 comparison.
  • Student connects the evidence to the idea instead of only restating vocabulary.

Short Response — Sample Acceptable Answers

  • Forces: “Balanced forces did not change the object’s motion, but unbalanced forces did. In our tests, the object stayed still when the pushes matched and moved when one push was stronger.”
  • Motion changes: “The stronger pushes usually moved the car farther than the gentle pushes. I know this because I compared the distances from both strong trials and both gentle trials.”
  • Magnetism: “A magnet can act without touching because the paperclip moved before the magnet touched it. The pattern also showed that some materials were magnetic and others were not.”
  • Design improvement: “Version 2 worked better because I changed the design after testing version 1. My proof is that version 2 completed the job more reliably than version 1.”

Troubleshooting / Misconceptions

  • If the student gives only a definition, ask “What evidence from the lesson or test supports that?”
  • If the student overgeneralizes magnets to all metals, point them back to the mixed test results.
  • If the student says version 2 was better without proof, ask what specifically changed in the results.
