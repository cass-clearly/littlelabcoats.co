From the Sun to Producers

Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Life Science — Ecosystems & Food Webs
☀️🌿
Producers capture the Sun’s energy and support the whole web.
📋 NGSS Standard:
5-LS2-1 — Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will explain how producers capture energy from the Sun and support the rest of the ecosystem.

💡 The Big Idea

Plants are producers because they use sunlight to make food. That stored energy becomes available to other organisms.

🔬 The Science

The Sun is the main source of energy for almost every food web on Earth. Producers, such as plants and algae, capture sunlight and use it in photosynthesis to make sugars. Those sugars store chemical energy that can move through an ecosystem when one organism eats another.

Producers are different from consumers because producers do not need to eat other organisms to make food. Instead, they use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to build sugars. Consumers cannot do that. They must get energy by eating plants or other animals. That means even top predators depend on producers indirectly.

Energy moves through a food web in one direction: from the Sun to producers, then to consumers. Matter also moves through the ecosystem, but energy begins with sunlight. If producers disappeared, the rest of the food web would eventually collapse because the original energy source would no longer be captured and stored in food.

Scientists ask two key questions when they study food webs: Which organisms are producers? and How does energy move from one organism to another? In this lesson, students should build a simple model and use evidence to explain why producers are the foundation of an ecosystem.

🧪 Investigation

🌞 Energy detective challenge: Students investigate a real plant clue, sort organism cards, and build full energy chains to prove where ecosystem energy begins.
Step 1 — Start with the producer clue (5–8 min)
Hold up the real leaf or plant sample. Ask: Does this leaf eat anything? If not, how does it get food? Use the sample to introduce the idea that producers make food using sunlight instead of eating other organisms.
Step 2 — Sort the organism cards (8–10 min)
Cut out the producer and consumer cards. Sort them into two groups. Students must defend each choice with evidence, not guesses. If they disagree, they must explain which organism makes its own food and which one gets energy by eating.
Step 3 — Solve the energy chain challenge (12–15 min)
Challenge students to build at least two correct energy chains that begin with the Sun. Each chain must start with a producer and then continue to one or more consumers. Students can use string, arrows, or drawn lines to connect the cards in order.
Step 4 — Play “Find the broken chain” (8–10 min)
Create one wrong chain on purpose, such as Sun → rabbit → hawk. Students act like detectives and explain what is wrong, where the chain breaks, and how to fix it.
Step 5 — Explain the science clearly (10 min)
Students write a short explanation of why producers are the base of the food web and what would happen if producers disappeared. Encourage them to use evidence from the cards and the leaf sample.

💬 Discussion Questions

What evidence tells you a plant is a producer?It does not eat other organisms. It uses sunlight to make food.
Why does an energy chain need to begin with the Sun and a producer?Because producers are the organisms that capture sunlight and store that energy as food.
How does a hawk still depend on producers even if it never eats grass directly?Its prey got energy from producers earlier in the chain.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Invent a mystery organism card and challenge another student to decide whether it belongs in the producer or consumer group, then place it into an energy chain.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Run this like a science investigation, not a worksheet-first lesson. Keep asking for evidence: What makes this one a producer? Where did this animal’s energy begin? The leaf sample helps anchor the lesson in a real object before students move into the model.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample Explanation

Producers are the base of a food web because they capture energy from the Sun and store it as food. Consumers depend on that stored energy, either by eating producers directly or by eating animals that ate producers.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student correctly identifies producers and consumers.
  • Student builds energy chains that begin with the Sun and a producer.
  • Student can find and fix a broken energy chain.
  • Student explains that energy moves through the ecosystem from producers to consumers.
  • Student explains that consumers would lose their energy source if producers disappeared.

Sample classifications

  • Producers: grass, algae, oak tree, sunflower
  • Consumers: rabbit, deer, caterpillar, hawk

Sample energy chains

  • Sun → grass → rabbit → hawk
  • Sun → oak tree → caterpillar → hawk

Sample broken chain

  • Sun → rabbit → hawk is incorrect because rabbits are consumers, not producers. The chain is missing the producer that captured the Sun’s energy first.
