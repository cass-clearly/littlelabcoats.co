Present and Defend Your Shelter Solution

Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design | Unit 1, Lesson 6 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design
🗣️📄🏆
Engineers defend solutions by connecting the whole design story to evidence.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem with criteria and constraints, generate and compare solutions, and plan fair tests with variables controlled and failure points considered.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will present a storm shelter solution and defend it using the problem, criteria, constraints, evidence, and redesign choices.

💡 The Big Idea

A strong engineering defense explains not only what was built, but why it deserves trust.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

Final presentations should connect the design problem, version 1 evidence, redesign choices, and final performance. Students should defend why their solution has merit rather than saying it is simply “the best.”

💡 Key Vocabulary
defendsolutionmeritcriteriaconstraintevidence

Materials

  • Printed lesson pages and worksheet
  • Notes from previous lessons
  • Prototype or labeled drawing

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Organize the design story
Review the problem, plan, first test, redesign, and final result.
Step 2 — Choose strongest evidence
Select observations that best show the solution has merit.
Step 3 — Prepare a short defense
Explain how the design meets criteria and handles constraints.
Step 4 — Present to a partner or family audience
Share the solution and answer follow-up questions.
Step 5 — Reflect on the process
Name one thing learned about designing for weather problems.

Discussion Questions

What makes a design defense strong?It uses criteria, constraints, and test evidence.
Why should engineers mention constraints in a final defense?A realistic solution must work within limits.
What does it mean to say a solution has merit?It has good evidence in its favor, even if it is not perfect.
Why include redesign history?It shows learning and improvement over time.

Extensions

Extension: Present your shelter solution to a family member and ask which evidence sounded most convincing.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Encourage short, evidence-rich explanations instead of long vague speeches.

Misconception to catch: Students may think confidence equals proof. Remind them that evidence is what makes the case stronger.

Student Worksheet

Name: ________________________     Date: ________________________

1️⃣ My claim

2️⃣ Evidence that supports my claim

3️⃣ How my design meets the criteria and constraints

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample ideas

Strong presentations name the design problem, describe the shelter, cite evidence from testing, explain redesign changes, and show how the final design better met the criteria.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student makes a clear claim about the solution.
  • Student uses specific evidence from tests or redesigns.
  • Student connects the final design to criteria and constraints.
