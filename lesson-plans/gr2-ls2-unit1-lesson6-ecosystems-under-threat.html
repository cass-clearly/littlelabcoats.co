🌿 Ecosystems Under Threat: What Can We Do? 🌿

Life Science — Unit 1, Lesson 6 of 6

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Plants & Ecosystems 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🌸🦋🌻🐝🌾
Healthy ecosystems are full of life — and we can help keep them that way!
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-LS4-1 — Make observations of plants and animals to compare the diversity of life in different habitats.
K-2-ETS1-1 — Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will identify at least 2 threats to local ecosystems, design a simple solution to one threat, and explain how their solution helps plants, pollinators, or animals in the ecosystem.

💡 The Big Idea

Ecosystems are fragile — pollution, habitat loss, invasive species, and climate change can unravel food chains. But humans can also help! Even small actions (like planting native flowers or reducing pesticide use) make a real difference. You don't have to be a scientist or a grown-up to protect an ecosystem — you just have to care and take action.

💡 Key vocabulary: habitat loss, invasive species, native plants, pollinator corridor, pesticide, conservation, biodiversity

📦 Materials

🏠 All from Around the House — No Purchases Needed!

  • Paper for garden design (plain or grid paper)
  • Crayons or colored pencils
  • Pencil
  • Index cards (parent writes out the 4 Threat Cards — see Activity 2)
  • Ruler (optional, for drawing garden grid)
  • Scrap paper or cardboard tubes (optional, for bee hotel bonus activity)

🌱 Activity 1 — Design a Pollinator Patch

Students design a small "pollinator garden" patch on paper — even just a window box or a corner of a yard. This is a real project your family could actually do!

🌼 Native Plants That Attract Pollinators

These plants thrive across much of North America and are loved by local pollinators:

🌻 Black-eyed Susan 🐝 Bees & butterflies
💛 Wild Bergamot 🐝 Native bees & butterflies
🌿 Milkweed 🦋 Monarch butterflies only!
🌸 Coneflower 🐝🦋 Bees & butterflies
🍀 Clover 🐝 Bumblebees & honeybees
Step 1 — Look at the Prompt Cards (5 min)
Discuss each plant card above. Ask: "Why do you think different pollinators like different flowers?" Let students share ideas before explaining that flower shapes, colors, and bloom times match the bodies and needs of specific pollinators.
Step 2 — Design Your Garden Patch (15 min)
On the Student Worksheet (next section), students draw a garden plot and:
  • Draw where each plant goes in the patch
  • Label each plant by name
  • Write or draw which pollinator it attracts
  • Include at least 3 different plants in their design
Encourage creativity — it can be a window box, a corner of a yard, a school garden bed, or any size they like!
🌟 Bonus — Design a Bee Hotel (10 min, optional)
Discuss: "Did you know most native bees don't live in hives? They nest in hollow stems and holes in the ground!"

Using scrap paper and cardboard, students can:
  1. Roll sheets of paper into tight tubes (these become bee nesting tunnels)
  2. Bundle them together and fit into a small box or cardboard container
  3. Draw or label their bee hotel — where would they hang it? Why does a bee hotel help native bees?
Key point: Providing nesting habitat is just as important as food — many native bees are declining partly because their nesting spots have disappeared.

⚠️ Activity 2 — Ecosystem Threat Cards

Before the lesson, a parent writes out 4 index cards with the scenarios below (one scenario per card). Present each card one at a time and discuss as a family: "What kind of threat is this? Who does it hurt? What could we do?"

🏗️
"A new apartment building is being built where a meadow used to be."
⚠️ Threat: Habitat Loss
✅ Solutions: Green roofs on buildings, pollinator wildflower strips along roads, community gardens
🧪
"Farmers are using pesticides that kill all insects, not just the pests."
⚠️ Threat: Pesticide Harm
✅ Solutions: Targeted pesticides, organic farming, buffer zones around wildflower areas
🌱
"An invasive plant from Asia is spreading and crowding out native plants."
⚠️ Threat: Invasive Species
✅ Solutions: Removal programs, replanting with native species, don't plant invasives in gardens
☀️
"A drought has lasted 3 years and the pond is drying up."
⚠️ Threat: Climate Change
✅ Solutions: Water conservation, shade planting, drought-tolerant native species

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: "Your pollinator garden design uses native plants. Why do you think native plants are better for local pollinators than non-native plants?"

Think about it: How long have native plants and local pollinators been living in the same place? What might have happened over all that time?

🗣️ Question 2: "We learned that removing one species from a food chain affects everything. Can adding a species also cause problems?"

Think about it: What would happen if a new animal arrived in an ecosystem where nothing had ever hunted it before? Could it take over?

📖 Culminating Project: My Ecosystem Field Guide

Create a mini-book with 6 pages — one page per lesson. On each page, draw a picture and write 1–2 sentences about what you learned.

  1. Page 1: What Plants Need — Draw a plant and label what it needs to grow
  2. Page 2: Pollinators — Draw a pollinator visiting a flower and explain what they're doing
  3. Page 3: Seed Dispersal — Draw your favorite seed travel method
  4. Page 4: Food Chains — Draw a 4-link food chain from your favorite habitat
  5. Page 5: Habitats — Draw an animal in its habitat and explain why it belongs there
  6. Page 6: Protecting Ecosystems — Draw your pollinator garden design and explain how it helps

📎 How to make it: Fold 3 sheets of paper in half and staple in the middle — instant 12-page booklet! Decorate the cover and share it with someone you love. 🌿

🚀 Extensions & Going Deeper

  • 🦋 Monarch Migration: Look up the monarch butterfly migration map online — they travel thousands of miles to Mexico every winter. How does milkweed loss threaten their whole lifecycle?
  • 🏙️ Map Your Neighborhood: Walk around your block and spot any native plants, "weeds" (many are native!), or places that could become pollinator habitat.
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "The Lorax" by Dr. Seuss (classic habitat loss tale); "Wangari's Trees of Peace" by Jeanette Winter (real conservation hero)
  • ✉️ Write a Letter: Have students write a pretend letter to a city planner explaining why they should add a pollinator strip along a new road.
  • 🌱 Make It Real: If you have any outdoor space — even a window box — actually plant some of the native plants from today's lesson!

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • All materials from around the house — no purchases needed. Index cards can be any scraps of paper.
  • The garden design can be real: If your family has any outdoor space — even a window box on an apartment sill — this is a project you could genuinely do together. That real-world connection makes the lesson stick.
  • 🦋 Milkweed is critical: Monarch butterflies are entirely dependent on milkweed — it's the only plant their caterpillars can eat. Planting even one milkweed plant is one of the most impactful things a family can do for monarch conservation. Make this a memorable moment in the lesson.
  • Threat Cards prep: Write each of the 4 threat scenarios on an index card beforehand. Presenting them as physical "cards" to turn over one at a time builds anticipation and makes the discussion feel like a game.
  • Invasive species examples to share: Kudzu vine smothering forests in the Southeast; lionfish in the Atlantic Ocean; cane toads in Australia. These are vivid, memorable examples — kids love how wild these stories are.
  • Vocab to reinforce throughout: habitat loss, invasive species, native plants, pollinator corridor, pesticide, conservation, biodiversity
  • Unit Wrap-Up timing: The Ecosystem Field Guide culminating project works well as a weekend project after finishing Lesson 6, or as one page per day across the following week.
  • Assessment: Can the student name at least 2 ecosystem threats? Can they explain how their garden design solution helps pollinators or animals? Can they connect the idea of invasive species to what they learned about food chains?

💬 Discussion Question Answer Guide

Q1 — Why are native plants better for local pollinators?
Native plants and local pollinators evolved together over thousands of years — the flower shapes, bloom times, and pollen types match perfectly. Native bees often have mouth parts shaped specifically for certain native flowers. Non-native ornamental plants may look pretty but often have little pollen or nectar, and their bloom times may not match when local pollinators need food. Example: Monarch caterpillars can only eat milkweed — a non-native ornamental would be useless to them.

Q2 — Can adding a species also cause problems?
Yes! Invasive species are a perfect example — when a species that evolved elsewhere is introduced to a new ecosystem, it has no natural predators, so it can multiply unchecked and outcompete native species. Real examples: kudzu vine smothering forests in the Southeast U.S.; lionfish in the Atlantic (no predators there); cane toads in Australia (introduced to control beetles, became a massive pest themselves). Adding the wrong species can be just as damaging as removing a native one.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student can name at least 2 ecosystem threats (habitat loss, pesticide harm, invasive species, climate change).
  • Student's garden design includes at least 3 correctly labeled native plants with matching pollinators.
  • Student can explain how their pollinator garden helps local pollinators or animals.
  • Student can connect invasive species to the food chain concept from Lesson 4.
  • Bonus: Student can explain why milkweed is uniquely critical for monarch butterflies.
