🎓 Teaching — When Parents Show Offspring What to Do 🎓

Life Science — Parents & Offspring | Unit 2, Lesson 4 of 6

📚 Grade: 1st Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Parents & Offspring 🏠 Materials: Household only
🦁 🐒 🦅 👶
Some behaviors are built-in from birth. Others must be taught — or practiced — over time.
📋 NGSS Standard:
1-LS3-1 — Use observations to describe patterns of what plants and animals (including humans) do to survive and how the behavior of parents helps offspring survive.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will distinguish between inherited behaviors (born knowing) and learned behaviors (taught or practiced), and give examples of each.

🔬 The Big Idea

Some behaviors come "pre-loaded" — animals are born knowing how to do them without ever being taught. These are called instincts. Other behaviors have to be learned by watching, practicing, and being taught. Parents play a huge role in passing on learned behaviors — that's a form of parenting too!

💡 Key vocabulary: instinct, learned behavior, inherited behavior, teach, practice, culture

📦 Materials

🏠 Household Items to Gather

  • Scissors to cut the behavior cards (worksheet page)
  • Glue stick or tape for sorting
  • Paper and crayons/pencil

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Warm-Up: Did You Learn That? (5 min)
Ask: "Do you think a baby knows how to breathe when it's born, or does someone teach it?" (Born knowing!)
"Do you think a baby knows how to ride a bike when it's born?" (Learned!)
Introduce the two categories: instincts (born knowing) and learned behaviors (taught or practiced).
Step 2 — Examples Together (10 min)
Go through the examples. For each one, ask: "Was this animal born knowing this, or did it have to learn it?"

🟠 Born Knowing (Instinct)

  • 🐢 Baby sea turtle crawling to the ocean the moment it hatches — no parent to guide it
  • 🕷️ Spider spinning a web — never watches another spider do it first
  • 🦅 Bird flying south in fall (migration) — first-year birds do it without being shown
  • 👶 Baby crying when hungry — no one teaches babies to cry
  • 🐝 Bee doing a waggle dance to tell others where flowers are

🔵 Taught or Practiced (Learned)

  • 🐒 Chimpanzee using a stick to fish termites out of a mound — taught by watching the mother
  • 🐋 Orca using a specific hunting technique — taught by the pod
  • 👶 Human language — no child is born knowing English or Spanish
  • 🦁 Lion cubs learning to hunt — parents bring wounded prey for cubs to practice on
  • 🚴 Riding a bike — learned through practice and guidance
Step 3 — Behavior Sorting Game (10 min)
Cut out the behavior cards on the worksheet. Sort them into "Born Knowing" and "Taught/Learned" columns. Then glue them down.
Step 4 — Connect to Yourself (10 min)
Ask: "What did your parents teach you?" — brainstorm: walking (well, practicing!), talking, riding a bike, cooking, reading, manners, songs.
"What did you 'just know' from birth?" — breathing, crying, sucking, blinking, startling at loud noises.
Step 5 — Wrap-Up (5 min)
Ask: "Is teaching a kind of parenting? Can you explain why?"
Key idea: passing on knowledge and skills is one of the most important things parents do — especially for animals with complex behaviors like humans and chimps.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • Why do you think baby sea turtles have to know how to crawl to the ocean without a parent showing them? (Their mother laid the eggs and left — no parent is there!)
  • Why is it important for orca pods to teach hunting to young orcas? (Different pods have different techniques for different prey — it's a local specialty!)
  • If you could only know 3 things from birth, what would you choose?
🌟 Big Question: "If a baby chimpanzee is raised by humans and never sees another chimp, can it learn chimp behaviors later?"

Partially. Basic chimp instincts (like a tendency to use tools) may still emerge. But complex social behaviors — like specific hunting techniques or communication signals learned from the group — would be absent or impaired. Studies of isolated chimps show they struggle with chimp social norms. This shows how much of animal "intelligence" is actually cultural: passed down through teaching, not genes.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🎭 Teaching Game: Have your student teach you something they know how to do. Discuss: how did they learn it?
  • 🐦 Bird Migration: Look up how young birds navigate on their first migration. Some use magnetic fields, star patterns, and learned landmarks!
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "Mama Built a Little Nest" by Jennifer Ward — instincts in action.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Gray area is good: Some behaviors have both instinct and learned components. Walking is instinct-driven (the wiring is there) but requires practice. Language has instinctive basis (babies are wired for language) but the specific language is learned. Embrace the nuance!
  • Chimp discussion: Keep it age-appropriate — focus on the idea that some behaviors need to be passed down from older to younger members of a group, just like human families teach children.
  • Assessment: Can the child sort at least 6 behaviors correctly? Can they name 1 instinct and 1 learned behavior from their own experience?

💡 Notes on Tricky Cards

  • Walking: Humans are wired to walk — but it still requires months of practice to develop the skill. It's both! Celebrate this nuance if a child raises it.
  • Bird migration: The drive to migrate is instinct, but some navigation landmarks may be partially learned from flock members. Both answers are defensible.
