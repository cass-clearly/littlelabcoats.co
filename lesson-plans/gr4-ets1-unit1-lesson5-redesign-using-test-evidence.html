Redesign Using Test Evidence

Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Disaster-Resistant Design
🔁📊🏠
Redesign works best when changes connect directly to evidence.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 / 3-5-ETS1-2 / 3-5-ETS1-3 — Define a design problem with criteria and constraints, generate and compare solutions, and plan fair tests with variables controlled and failure points considered.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will redesign their storm shelter by choosing evidence-based improvements and explaining why those changes should help.

💡 The Big Idea

Redesign is stronger when it responds to real failure points instead of random new ideas.

🔬 Engineering Thinking

Students should use version 1 evidence to decide what to change. Grade 4 rigor means they should explain the expected effect of each change, not only name the change itself.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review version 1 evidence
Identify the most important problem from the first test.
Step 2 — Choose targeted changes
Select one or two changes likely to improve performance.
Step 3 — Predict version 2 performance
Explain why the redesign should work better.
Step 4 — Sketch version 2
Label the new parts or changes.
Step 5 — Prepare to defend the redesign
Students connect each change to a specific piece of evidence.

Discussion Questions

Why should redesign connect to evidence?Evidence helps engineers make purposeful improvements.
What is a weak redesign?A change made for no clear reason.
How many changes should students make at once?Usually one or two clear changes so the evidence stays useful.
Why compare version 1 and version 2?Comparison shows whether the redesign improved performance.

Extensions

Extension: Ask someone at home whether your redesign directly solves the biggest problem from version 1.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Ask students to finish the sentence “I changed ___ because version 1 showed ___.”

Misconception to catch: Bigger or heavier is not always better if it creates new problems.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample ideas

If the roof leaked, a student might add a steeper angle or overlap layers because version 1 let water through the top seam. If the shelter tipped, a student might widen the base or strengthen supports because version 1 leaned or fell during the wind test.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student names a specific failure point or piece of version 1 evidence first.
  • Student connects each redesign choice to that evidence instead of proposing a random change.
  • Student predicts how version 2 should improve the shelter under the same kind of test.
