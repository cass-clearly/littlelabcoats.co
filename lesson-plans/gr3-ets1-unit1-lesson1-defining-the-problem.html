Defining the Problem

Engineering — Design Process | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Design Process
🎯🧠📦
Engineers begin by naming the need, the user, and what success should look like.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 — Define a simple design problem reflecting a need or a want that includes specified criteria for success and constraints on materials, time, or cost.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will define a grade-appropriate design problem by naming the user, the need, and clear success criteria for a fragile-object protection challenge.

💡 The Big Idea

A strong design starts before building. Engineers first decide who needs help, what problem needs to be solved, and what a successful solution must do.

🔬 The Science

This unit uses a familiar engineering challenge: protect a fragile object, such as an egg or small breakable model, during a drop test using only a small set of household materials.

In Lesson 1, the important work is not building yet. Students learn that a design problem needs a user, a need, and criteria for success. For example, a strong problem statement might say the object must stay unbroken after a drop from table height.

When students define the problem clearly, the rest of the unit becomes more focused. Their later material choices, plans, tests, and redesigns all connect back to this first engineering decision.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Launch the Challenge: Introduce the unit challenge: design something that protects a fragile object during a drop from table height.
Step 2 — Name the User and Need: Discuss who or what needs help in the challenge and what the design must prevent.
Step 3 — Create Criteria for Success: Generate clear success goals such as staying unbroken, holding the object in place, and working with allowed materials.
Step 4 — Compare Weak and Strong Problem Statements: Model why “make something cool” is weak but “protect a fragile object during a drop” is strong.
Step 5 — Write a Problem Statement: Students write a problem statement using the challenge user, need, and success criteria.

Discussion Questions

Why do engineers define the problem before building?Look for answers like: They need to know what they are trying to solve and what success should look like.
What makes this challenge more specific than “build something cool”?Look for answers like: It names the fragile object, the drop test, and the success goal.
Why is the word fragile important in this challenge?Look for answers like: It tells why protection matters and what the design must prevent.
How do criteria help engineers?Look for answers like: Criteria tell what the design must do in order to succeed.
Why is it useful to say who or what needs help?Look for answers like: It keeps the design focused on the real need instead of random ideas.

Extensions

Extension: Look around your home and name one other fragile item that might need protection during a move or fall.

Extension: Ask a family member to help rewrite your problem statement so it sounds even more specific.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Students often jump to building ideas. Pause them and bring them back to the problem statement first.

Teaching note: Keep the language concrete: table height, fragile object, limited materials, protected on landing.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Name the Challenge — Sample Use

Accept answers such as protecting an egg, toy, or other fragile item during a drop from table height.

2️⃣ What Must the Design Do? — Sample Ideas

Strong criteria may include keeping the object from cracking, holding it in place, and working with only the allowed materials.

3️⃣ Write a Strong Problem Statement — Model Response

“I need to design something that helps protect a fragile egg by cushioning the landing so the egg can stay unbroken after a drop.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student names the fragile-object challenge clearly.
  • Student lists at least two useful success criteria.
  • Student writes a problem statement that includes the need and success goal.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student starts sketching immediately, redirect them to the problem statement and criteria first.
  • If a student gives a broad answer, ask what exactly has to stay safe and how success will be measured.
