☁️ Cloud Detectives & Condensation Clues

Earth Science — Weather Detectives & Wild Storms | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45–55 minutes 🌎 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Water in Air 🧪 Hands-on Hook: Condensation lab + cloud match
☁️🥶💧🔍
Clouds and water droplets give clues about what the air is doing above us.
📋 NGSS-aligned focus:
Using observations and simple models to explain how cooling air can lead to condensation, clouds, and changes in weather.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare common cloud patterns, observe condensation on a cold surface, and use evidence from clouds and water droplets to make a short-term weather prediction.

💡 The Big Idea

Clouds are made of tiny droplets of water or tiny ice pieces. They form when water vapor in the air cools down and condenses. Because different clouds form in different conditions, the sky can give us clues about what weather may happen next.

🔬 The Science

Air always contains some water vapor, even when we cannot see it. When warm, moist air cools, the water vapor changes into tiny liquid droplets. If that happens high in the sky around dust or other tiny particles, clouds form. If it happens on a cold cup or can in your kitchen, you see water droplets collect on the outside. That water did not leak through the can — it came from the air.

Grade 2 does not need full meteorology vocabulary, but it is a great age to notice that some cloud patterns are thin and wispy, some are flat and gray, and some grow tall and dark before storms. This lesson is a weather-evidence lesson, not just a cloud-naming lesson.

💬 Key vocabulary:
water vapor condensation cumulus stratus cirrus prediction

🧰 Materials

  • 1 metal can or clear glass
  • Ice cubes
  • Water
  • Blue paper or construction paper
  • Cotton balls or torn white paper scraps
  • Glue or tape
  • Pencil and crayons
  • Optional: access to outdoor sky or a window

☁️ Cloud Clue Cards

Cumuluspuffy, piled-up clouds; often fair weather when small
Stratusflat, spread-out cloud layer; often gray and gloomy
Cirrusthin, wispy, high clouds; often made of ice crystals
Dark storm cloudsthicker clouds that can signal rain or storms

🔍 Let’s Get Started

Step 1 — Sky launch: Ask, “Can the sky give us clues before rain starts?” Let the child share ideas before naming cloud patterns.
Step 2 — Condensation test: Fill a metal can or glass with ice water. Wait a few minutes. Watch tiny droplets form on the outside. Ask where that water came from.
Step 3 — Explain the clue: Tell the child the water did not pass through the cup. It condensed from invisible water vapor in the air when the outside surface got cold.
Step 4 — Build cloud models: Use cotton balls or paper scraps on blue paper to model cumulus, stratus, cirrus, and dark storm clouds. Label each one.
Step 5 — Connect clouds to weather: For each cloud type, talk about what a meteorologist might notice. Which clouds usually look calm? Which ones might warn us that rain is closer?
Step 6 — Make a prediction: Look at the real sky, or choose one of the cloud cards. Have the child write a short prediction using evidence words like “because,” “I noticed,” and “I think.”

💬 Discussion Questions

Why did water appear on the outside of the cold can?Look for: water vapor in the air cooled and condensed into droplets.
Why are clouds useful to meteorologists?Look for: clouds can signal what kind of weather conditions are building.
Are all clouds the same? How do you know?Look for: different clouds have different shapes, heights, and clues about weather.
Why is a prediction not the same thing as a wild guess?Look for: a prediction uses observations and evidence, not random hoping.

🌟 Extension

Extra challenge: Revisit the same sky later in the day. Did the cloud pattern stay the same or change?

Family extension: Watch weather news or a weather app and compare the cloud symbols you see to the clouds you studied today.

👩‍🏫 Parent / Teacher Notes

  • This lesson avoids repeating Kindergarten water-cycle work by focusing on evidence from condensation and cloud clues, not just the cycle diagram.
  • Keep the cloud types simple. The goal is not memorizing every cloud name on Earth.
  • If your child struggles with the can experiment, touch the droplets and ask, “Was this water here when we started?” That usually makes the condensation idea click.
  • Do not force a perfect forecast. What matters is the child giving a reason for the prediction.

✂️ Parent Answer Key — Detach Before Giving Worksheet to Students

1️⃣ Condensation Clue

Expected idea: water vapor in the air cooled on the cold outside surface and turned into liquid droplets.

2️⃣ Match the Cloud to the Clue

  • Cumulus: puffy, cotton-like; often fair weather when small
  • Stratus: flat layer; often gray or overcast conditions
  • Cirrus: thin and wispy; high in the sky
  • Dark storm cloud: thick and heavy-looking; may signal rain or storms

3️⃣ Draw Today’s Sky

Accept any honest observational drawing with at least one descriptive label.

4️⃣ My Prediction

There is no single right answer. The important part is the evidence sentence. A strong response sounds like, “I think it might rain later because the clouds looked darker and thicker than yesterday.”

✅ What success looks like

  • The child can explain that condensation came from water in the air.
  • The child can compare at least two cloud patterns.
  • The child uses evidence to support a weather prediction.
