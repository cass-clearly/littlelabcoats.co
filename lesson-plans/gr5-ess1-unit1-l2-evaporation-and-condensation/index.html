Evaporation and Condensation

📚 Grade: 5th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth & Space Science — Water on Earth Systems
☀️💨💧
The Sun drives evaporation, and cooling causes condensation.
📋 NGSS Standard:
5-ESS2-1 — Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interact.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will model how evaporation and condensation move water between Earth’s surface and the atmosphere.

💡 The Big Idea

The Sun adds energy that can turn liquid water into water vapor. Cooling can make that vapor condense into droplets again.

🔬 The Science

Evaporation happens when liquid water gains enough energy to become water vapor. Condensation happens when water vapor cools and turns back into liquid droplets.

These processes connect water at Earth’s surface with water in the atmosphere and help drive the larger water cycle.

📦 Materials

🏠 Household Items to Gather

📋 Printables

  • Student worksheet page from this lesson

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Set up the model (5 min)
Place warm water in a clear container and cover it with plastic wrap or a cool plate.
Step 2 — Add cooling on top (10 min)
Place ice on the cover and observe over time.
Step 3 — Watch for droplets (10 min)
Notice where water appears and where it came from.
Step 4 — Explain each process (10 min)
Students identify evaporation and condensation in the model.
Step 5 — Connect to clouds (10 min)
Discuss how this model is similar to water moving in the atmosphere.

💬 Discussion Questions

What caused the water to evaporate?Heat energy from the warm water or the Sun in the real world.
Why did droplets form on the cool surface?Water vapor condensed when it cooled.
How does this model connect to weather?Clouds form from condensation in the atmosphere.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Leave a shallow dish of water out in a sunny vs shady place and compare how it changes over time.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Help students avoid saying the droplets came “through” the plastic. They came from water vapor condensing on the surface.
