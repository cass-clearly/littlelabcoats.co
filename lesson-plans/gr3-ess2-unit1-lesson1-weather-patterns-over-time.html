Weather Patterns Over Time

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
⛅📅🌦️
Daily weather can change, but a detailed weather record helps students notice patterns over several days.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS2-1 — Obtain and combine information to describe climates in different regions of the world.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will record weather observations over several days and describe a short-term weather pattern using evidence.

💡 The Big Idea

Weather can change from day to day, but a weather record helps us notice repeated patterns like rainy afternoons, cooler mornings, or windy days after a storm.

🔬 The Science

Weather is what the air and sky are doing right now. Scientists observe temperature, cloud type, cloud cover, wind, and precipitation. Precipitation means water that falls from clouds, such as rain, snow, sleet, or hail. One observation is helpful, but a series of observations makes patterns easier to see.

In this lesson, students look across several days of weather data and explain what stayed the same, what changed, and what pattern they can defend with evidence. This prepares them for climate thinking later in the unit.

Use more specific weather words

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Go outside and notice the weather for today (5 min)
If the weather allows, step outside briefly and ask students to describe today like young meteorologists. Prompt for temperature or warm/cold, cloud type, cloud coverage, wind speed, wind direction, and precipitation instead of only broad words like cloudy or windy.
Step 2 — Read the included five-day weather record (10 min)
Use the sample chart on this lesson page. Help students read the temperature, cloud type, cloud coverage, wind, and precipitation (rain, measured in millimeters when possible) across several days.
Step 3 — Build a simple weather vane and rain gauge (10 min)
Make a simple weather vane by taping two straws into a T shape. Add a paper arrow to the horizontal straw, then balance the straw on a pencil or skewer secured upright in a ball of play dough. If you want to measure rain, use a plastic cup marked in millimeters as a simple rain gauge. See the simple weather vane sketch in the Parent/Teacher Notes for a quick visual.
Easy tool ideas: The paper arrow should point the direction the wind is coming from. If the weather vane is not a hit, students can still record wind as no wind, light wind, or strong wind. If you do not have an outdoor thermometer, students can write warm or cold in the temperature space.
Step 4 — Record evidence on the worksheet (10 min)
Students use the sample weather record and any outside observations to complete the worksheet with specific evidence.
Step 5 — Explain the pattern out loud (10 min)
Students share one complete sentence that names the pattern and the evidence that supports it, such as repeated cloud cover, changing temperature, or increasing precipitation.

Discussion Questions

Why is one weather observation not enough to show a pattern?A pattern needs more than one day of evidence.
What changed over the five days?Students might notice temperature, rain, clouds, or wind changes.
What stayed the same over the five days?Students may notice repeated clouds, repeated afternoon rain, or similar temperatures.
How do your observations help you describe the weather more clearly?Observations give evidence instead of a guess.

Extensions

Extension: Keep a family weather chart for three more days and see whether the same pattern continues.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Push students to use the weather record itself as evidence instead of relying on memory alone.

Important misconception to catch: A pattern does not mean every day looks exactly the same. A pattern can include changes that repeat in a recognizable way.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Read the Weather Record — Sample Acceptable Answers

Students should use the sample chart on this lesson page and name specific observations such as cumulus clouds, overcast skies, fast wind from the south, or 4 mm of rain.

2️⃣ Name a Pattern — Sample Idea

“The weather became cooler and wetter over the five days.” or “Three days in the middle of the week were cloudy and rainy.”

3️⃣ Use Evidence — Sample Idea

Students should point to specific days and observations, such as repeated rain, lower temperatures, or stronger wind later in the week.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student uses more than one day of evidence.
  • Student names a short-term weather pattern clearly.
  • Student supports the pattern with specific observations.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student only says “it changed,” ask what changed and on which days.
  • If a student confuses a pattern with a prediction, remind them this lesson is about describing evidence we already have.
