Climate Regions and Conditions

Earth Science — Weather & Climate | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Weather & Climate
🌍☀️❄️🌴
Climate describes the usual weather conditions in a place over a long time.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-ESS2-1 — Obtain and combine information to describe climates in different regions of the world.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare different climate regions and explain how climate is different from daily weather.

💡 The Big Idea

Weather tells us what it is like right now. Climate tells us the usual pattern of weather in a place over a long time.

🔬 The Science

A desert, rainforest, tundra, and temperate region can all have weather every day, but each region has different typical conditions over time. Those typical conditions are part of climate.

Scientists describe climate using long-term patterns such as usually warm, usually dry, often rainy, or very cold for much of the year. In this lesson, students compare regions so they do not confuse climate with a single day of weather.

🌍 Climate Cards for This Lesson

Use these four climate cards during the region-comparison activity. Each card shows the usual conditions in that region over time, not just the weather on one day.

☀️
Desert
Hot + Dry

Usual conditions
Deserts are usually very dry. Many deserts are hot during the day and get very little rain over time.

What you might expect

  • Dry sand or rocky ground
  • Very few puddles, streams, or wet places
  • Plants and animals that can live with little water
🌧️
Rainforest
Warm + Rainy

Usual conditions
Rainforests are usually warm, wet, and rainy. They get a lot of precipitation over time.

What you might expect

  • Lots of green plants and tall trees
  • Wet ground, puddles, or dripping leaves
  • Air that often feels damp or humid
❄️
Tundra
Very Cold

Usual conditions
Tundra regions are usually very cold for much of the year. The ground can stay frozen, and there are very short growing seasons.

What you might expect

  • Snow, ice, or frozen ground
  • Very short grasses, mosses, or small plants
  • Animals with thick fur or other ways to stay warm
🍂
Temperate Region
Mild + Seasonal

Usual conditions
Temperate regions usually have moderate temperatures and seasons. They are not extremely hot, extremely cold, or extremely dry all year.

What you might expect

  • Weather that changes with the seasons
  • Trees that may bloom in spring or lose leaves in fall
  • Rain or snow at different times of year
What to notice: Each card describes a long-term pattern. A single cold day in a temperate region or a rainy day in a desert does not change the climate by itself.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review weather from Lesson 1 (5 min)
Ask: “What could change tomorrow?” to remind students weather is short-term.
Step 2 — Introduce four climate regions (10 min)
Use the included climate cards on this lesson page to compare a hot dry desert, a rainy rainforest, a cold tundra, and a temperate region.
Step 3 — Sort examples into weather or climate (10 min)
Students decide whether a statement describes today’s weather or a usual long-term pattern, using the included climate cards to notice the word usual.
Step 4 — Compare regions on the worksheet (10 min)
Students use the included climate cards to record typical conditions and explain how the regions are different.
Step 5 — Write a contrast statement (10 min)
Students complete a sentence that clearly distinguishes weather from climate.

Discussion Questions

How is climate different from weather?Weather is short-term; climate is the usual long-term pattern.
Why can two places have different climates?Different regions have different usual temperatures and precipitation patterns.
Can a cold day happen in a warm climate?Yes, one day of weather does not change the whole climate.
Why do scientists use the word “usual” when talking about climate?Climate is about what is common over time, not one event.

Extensions

Extension: Choose one climate region and draw what clothing, plants, or homes might fit its usual conditions.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep repeating the contrast: weather = right now, climate = usual pattern over time.

Important misconception to catch: Students may think one rainy day means a place has a rainy climate. Bring them back to “usual over time.”

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Compare Climate Regions — Sample Acceptable Answers

Students should use the included climate cards from this lesson page as their evidence source.

Desert: usually hot and dry, with very little rain over time. Rainforest: usually warm and rainy. Tundra: usually very cold for much of the year. Temperate: moderate conditions with seasons.

2️⃣ Weather or Climate? — Answer Key

“It is raining today.” = weather. “This place is usually hot and dry.” = climate. “The winter here is often snowy.” = climate. “The sky is cloudy this morning.” = weather.

3️⃣ Explain the Difference — Sample Response

“Weather is what it is like right now, but climate is the usual weather pattern in a place over a long time.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student correctly distinguishes weather from climate.
  • Student uses the included climate cards to describe at least two climate regions accurately.
  • Student uses “usual” or “over time” language when explaining climate.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student keeps naming one day’s weather, ask whether that statement would still be true most of the time on the included climate cards.
  • If a student gets stuck on the region chart, have them re-read the “Usual conditions” and “What you might expect” parts of the included climate cards.
  • If a student thinks climate never changes, remind them climate describes long-term patterns, not one frozen moment.
