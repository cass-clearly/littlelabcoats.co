Weather is what the sky and air are like today, and daily observations help us notice patterns.
📋 NGSS Alignment:
K-ESS2-1 — Use and share observations of local weather conditions to describe patterns over time.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will observe the weather from a window or doorway, choose evidence symbols, and begin a simple weather pattern chart.

💡 The Big Idea

Weather is what the sky and air are like today, and daily observations help us notice patterns.

Window weather station

The window scene gives families a beautiful anchor for the routine: look, record, compare, and talk about what changed.

  • Observe sky and air clues
  • Record today’s weather
  • Start noticing patterns

🔬 Key Vocabulary

weathersunnycloudywindyrainypattern

📦 Materials

  • Window, doorway, or safe outdoor spot
  • Weather symbol cards or crayons
  • Thermometer optional
  • Pencil
  • Worksheet

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Look carefully
Observe the sky and air from a safe spot.
Step 2 — Choose sky words
Pick sunny, cloudy, rainy, snowy, or partly cloudy.
Step 3 — Choose air words
Pick windy/calm and warm/cool.
Step 4 — Draw a symbol
Draw today’s weather in the chart.
Step 5 — Make a prediction
Predict whether tomorrow will match today or change.
Step 6 — Share the report
Student gives a one-sentence meteorologist report.

💬 Discussion Questions

What did you notice in the sky?Use evidence from the activity in your answer.
How could you tell whether the air was windy?Use evidence from the activity in your answer.
Why do scientists record weather more than once?Use evidence from the activity in your answer.

🚀 Extension

Try this next: Ask your child to repeat one part of the test tomorrow or with one changed material, then explain what stayed the same and what changed.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • At-home win: This free sample is complete enough to teach today and polished enough to show how a full Little Lab Coats lesson feels.
  • Evidence move: Press students to point to a mark, drawing, comparison, or observation before they explain.
  • Family-friendly pacing: If attention is short, do steps 1–4 today and the worksheet discussion later.

🔑 Parent Answer Key

What success looks like

A strong answer uses observable evidence: sky condition, wind, and warm/cool feel. It should not rely on guesses only.

  • Student names the important observation or test result.
  • Student uses unit vocabulary in a simple sentence.
  • Student connects evidence to the science idea instead of guessing.
