✍️ Short Response — Use Evidence

Explain one weather forecast or one storm design choice using evidence from the unit.

Prompt: Name the clue or problem, explain what you predicted or designed, and use one piece of evidence to show why your answer makes sense.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Use the quiz and short response together: the multiple-choice questions check recognition, but the short response checks science reasoning.
  • Look for evidence words: strong responses use words like because, clue, pattern, test, droplets, wind, or redesign.
  • Fast grading tip: scan for whether the child names a real weather clue or real test result instead of giving a vague opinion.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Parent note: Student wording may vary if the weather reasoning is correct.

Multiple-Choice Answer Key

  1. Tools help scientists collect evidence and compare one day to another
  2. Dark thick clouds can be a clue that rain or storms may be closer
  3. Moving floodwater can push people or cars and hide the road
  4. A forecast is stronger when it uses more than one clue or pattern
  5. Water vapor cools and condenses into droplets
  6. The droplets on the plastic came from water in the air, not through the plastic
  7. Engineers should test Version 1 and Version 2 in the same way so the comparison is fair
  8. A strong redesign explanation names a failure point and one change that improved the design

Short Response — What Success Looks Like

  • Student names a real weather clue or design problem.
  • Student explains the forecast or design choice clearly.
  • Student supports the answer with a specific clue, pattern, droplet observation, or test result.

Short Response — Sample Acceptable Answer

“I predicted rain later because the clouds got darker and the wind got stronger. Those clues showed that the weather pattern was changing.”
