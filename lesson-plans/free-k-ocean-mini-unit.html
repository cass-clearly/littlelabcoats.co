📦 Simple prep list for the whole mini unit

  • Blue paper or blanket
  • Shells, toy fish, or printed ocean animal pictures
  • Crayons, scissors, glue, and tape
  • Paper plates or cardstock
  • Small flashlight for the deep ocean lesson
  • A bin or bowl of water for optional sensory play

Lesson 1 — 🐚 Shore Treasure Hunt

Focus: Beach and tide poolSkill: Observe and sortWow factor: real shells + ocean treasure hunt
Students will notice that the shore is part of the ocean world and sort objects or animals by what belongs at the beach.

Opening wonder

Hide shells, toy sea animals, or printed ocean pictures around the room. Tell the children the tide has left treasures behind and their job is to be ocean explorers.

Activity flow

Step 1 — Hunt for treasures.
Students collect or point to shells, animals, and “not ocean” objects.
Step 2 — Sort the finds.
Make two piles: “belongs near the ocean” and “does not belong.”
Step 3 — Build a beach scene.
Glue shells, crabs, waves, or seaweed onto a page.
Step 4 — Share one clue.
Each child says how they knew one item belonged near the shore.

Discussion

What did you find at the shore?
Why might a crab or shell be near the beach?
Parent win: this lesson feels play-based, but it quietly teaches classification and habitat clues.

Lesson 2 — 🪸 Reef Rainbow Rescue

Focus: Coral reef lifeSkill: Match animals to habitatWow factor: bright reef collage
Students will learn that coral reefs are colorful ocean habitats where many animals find food and shelter.

Opening wonder

Ask: “If you were a tiny reef fish, where would you hide?” Show the reef part of the ocean zones diagram.

Activity flow

Step 1 — Look closely.
Notice the coral, fish colors, and places to hide.
Step 2 — Match and move.
Give students ocean animal cards and let them place each one where it belongs on a reef poster.
Step 3 — Make a reef collage.
Use torn paper, crayons, or tissue paper to create colorful coral and fish.
Step 4 — Reef rescue story.
Tell a short story about helping a lost fish find the reef again.

Discussion

Why do so many animals like the reef?
Which reef animal would you want to be for one day?
Make it sing: play gentle ocean music while they build their reef. Tiny theatricality, huge payoff.

Lesson 3 — ✨ Deep Ocean Glow

Focus: Deep ocean mysterySkill: Compare bright/shallow with dark/deepWow factor: flashlight deep sea reveal
Students will compare the bright upper ocean with the dark deep ocean and discover that some animals have special features that help them live there.

Opening wonder

Turn the lights down a little and shine a flashlight on deep-sea animal pictures. Whisper dramatically. The vibes matter.

Activity flow

Step 1 — Bright vs. dark.
Compare the sunny ocean from Lesson 1 to the dark deep ocean.
Step 2 — Meet strange animals.
Introduce jellyfish, anglerfish, or glowing squid in a gentle, kid-safe way.
Step 3 — Make a glowing deep-sea picture.
Use black paper with bright crayons or stickers.
Step 4 — Ocean expert share.
Students finish the sentence: “Deep ocean animals need ____ because ____.”

Discussion

What is different about the deep ocean?
How might a glowing body help an ocean animal?
Teacher note: keep the deep ocean magical, not scary. “Mysterious” beats “terrifying sea monsters” every time for this age.
