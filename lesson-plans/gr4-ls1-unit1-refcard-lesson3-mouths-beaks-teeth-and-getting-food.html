Mouths, Beaks, Teeth, and Getting Food

Grade 4 · Unit 1
Animals do not all eat the same things, so they do not all have the same feeding structures. A hooked beak tears, a flat beak scoops, sharp teeth rip, and broad teeth grind.
Key vocabulary
feeding structurebeaktoothgrind
What to notice
  • Show a few contrasting feeding structures and ask students what kind of food each one seems best for.
  • Model an evidence sentence: “This beak is narrow and long, so it could reach into a flower.”
  • Sort feeding structures into likely functions: tearing, crushing, filtering, sipping, or scraping.
Materials snapshot
  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • animal cards or photos showing beaks/teeth/mouths
  • small set of household “food” models if desired (rice, sponge bits, paper strips)
  • pencil
Talk-about prompts
  • Why would one kind of beak not work equally well for every bird?
  • How do teeth give evidence about an animal’s food?
  • What makes a strong structure-function explanation in this lesson?
Why it matters

A feeding structure helps an animal obtain, bite, tear, crush, filter, or chew food. The structure works together with the organism’s food source and behavior. A hummingbird’s long beak works differently from a beaver’s teeth or a hawk’s beak because the feeding challenge is different.

Try this next: Try a gentle biomimicry tie-in: test tweezers, a spoon, clothespins, and a straw with different “foods.” Which tool acts most like each feeding structure?
