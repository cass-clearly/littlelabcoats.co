External Structures for Survival

Grade 4 · Unit 1
Animals do not all have the same body plan because different external structures help them solve different survival problems. Thick fur helps keep heat in. Waterproof feathers help keep water out. Sharp claws help grip, dig, or catch food. Long legs can help an animal run away or reach new places.
Key vocabulary
external structure function survival body covering
What to notice
  • Start with a quick picture walk using the included animal cards on this lesson page. Ask students to notice one visible body part before naming its job.
  • Model structure-function thinking with the duck card: “A duck has waterproof feathers. That helps it move through water and stay warm.”
  • Sort the included animal cards by the kind of job the structure does: movement, protection, finding food, or staying warm/cool.
Materials snapshot
  • printed lesson pages, Student Worksheet, and the included animal cards on this lesson page
  • pencil or colored pencils
  • optional: magnifying glass for feather/fur/shell observation
Talk-about prompts
  • Why would two animals need different body coverings?
  • How can the same kind of structure do different jobs?
  • Why is it not enough to only name a body part?
Why it matters

External structures are the body parts we can observe from the outside. They can help animals find food, stay warm or cool, move safely, defend themselves, attract mates, or care for offspring. Structure and function are linked: the shape, texture, size, and placement of a body part often help explain what it does.

Try this next: Revisit the included animal cards on this lesson page and sort them a second way — by animals that move quickly, stay warm or dry, protect themselves, or get food. Then add one optional real-world observation from a pet, bird outside, or zoo memory.
