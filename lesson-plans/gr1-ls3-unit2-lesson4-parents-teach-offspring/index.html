🐦🦮🎓 Teaching: When Parents Show What to Do

Parents and Offspring Unit, Lesson 4

📚 Grade: 1st Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science 📋 NGSS Standard: 1-LS3-1
🐦🦮🎓
Some things animals just know. Other things they have to be taught. Can you tell the difference?
📋 NGSS Standard:
1-LS3-1 — Make observations to construct an evidence-based account that young plants and animals are like, but not exactly like, their parents.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Distinguish between inherited behaviors (instincts) and learned behaviors.
  • Recognize examples of parent animals teaching skills to offspring.
  • Connect the concept of learning to human experiences of being taught by parents.

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need (Household Only)

  • Paper and pencil
  • Optional: a simple skill to practice "teaching" a younger sibling during the lesson (for example, stacking blocks or folding paper)

✅ No materials to purchase.

📖 Vocabulary

⚡ Instinct
A behavior an animal is born knowing how to do. No teaching needed.
📚 Learned Behavior
A behavior taught by a parent or gained through experience.
🎓 Teach
To show someone how to do something they did not already know.
🧠 Behavior
Something a living thing does (an action, not a physical trait).

📚 Background (Read Aloud to Student)

Did you know there are two very different kinds of behaviors in the animal world? The first kind is called an instinct. An instinct is a behavior an animal is born knowing how to do, without any teaching at all. A baby spider that has never met another spider will still spin a perfect web. A newly hatched salmon will swim upstream even though no one showed it how. These behaviors are built into the animal's biology, passed down from parent to offspring through their genes, just like physical traits are passed down.

The second kind is called a learned behavior. A learned behavior is something the animal picks up through experience or through being taught by a parent. A dog that knows how to "sit" on command was taught by a human. A parrot that talks was taught by hearing humans speak. These behaviors are not built in at birth. Without the right teacher or the right experiences, the animal would not develop them.

Sometimes the line between instinct and learned behavior can be tricky! Some behaviors are partly instinct and partly learned. A baby bird has the instinct to fly, but it still needs practice and a little encouragement from its parents before it can soar. As you go through today's sorting game, you will discover some surprising answers. Science is full of surprises!

🕷️ Most surprising fact: Spider web spinning is instinct! A baby spider raised in complete isolation from all other spiders will still build a perfect, species-specific web on its very first try. It never needed a teacher.

🔬 Procedure

Step 1 — Born Knowing It?
Ask your student: "What is something you knew how to do the moment you were born?" Guide them to think of: breathing, blinking, crying, sucking (for feeding as a newborn). These are human instincts!
Step 2 — Things Parents Teach
Ask: "What is something your parents had to teach you?" Examples: talking, using a spoon, riding a bike, reading, tying shoes. These are learned behaviors.
Step 3 — The Sorting Game 🃏
Read each of these 10 behaviors aloud, one at a time. For each one, make a prediction: is it instinct (Born Knowing) or learned?
(a) A duck swimming
(b) A dog sitting on command
(c) A spider spinning a web
(d) A dog shaking hands (giving paw)
(e) A bird building a nest
(f) A cat using a litter box
(g) A baby crying
(h) A parrot talking
(i) A salmon swimming upstream
(j) A puppy learning to sit
Step 4 — Make the Chart
Draw two columns on paper: "Born Knowing It (Instinct)" and "Learned from Parent or People." Sort all 10 behaviors into the columns based on your predictions.
Step 5 — The Reveal!
Check your answers against the answer key below. Discuss any surprises:
  • 🕷️ Spider web spinning = Instinct! (Baby spiders spin perfectly with no teacher.)
  • 🐦 Bird nest building = Mostly instinct! (Though some species improve with practice.)
  • 🐱 Cat using a litter box = Learned! (Cats must be trained or shown by their mother.)
  • 🦜 Parrot talking = Learned! (Parrots hear and mimic human speech over time.)
  • 🐟 Salmon swimming upstream = Instinct! (They follow scent memories and natural drive.)
  • 🦆 Duck swimming = Instinct! (Ducklings swim within hours of hatching.)
Step 6 (Optional) — Be a Teacher!
If a younger sibling is present, choose a simple skill (like stacking blocks or folding a piece of paper) and practice teaching it. Reflect: Is this like what animal parents do? What is the same? What is different?

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: What is one thing you were born knowing how to do?

Breathing, blinking, crying, sucking. All babies are born with these instincts. They are built into the body's programming from day one and do not require any learning or teaching.

🗣️ Question 2: What is one thing your parents had to teach you that you were not born knowing?

Open-ended. Examples: talking, walking (partially learned), using a fork, reading, riding a bike, tying shoes. All of these took practice and guidance from a parent or teacher. A baby cannot just pick up a book and start reading on day one!

🗣️ Question 3: Is spider web spinning learned or instinct? How do you know?

It is instinct! A baby spider that has never seen another spider still spins a web correctly on its very first try. No one teaches it. If it were learned, a spider raised alone would not know how to spin a web at all.

🗣️ Question 4: Why would it be helpful for some behaviors to be instincts rather than learned?

If an animal had to be taught everything, it might not survive long enough to learn. Instincts help animals survive right away, even without a parent around. A duckling needs to swim immediately after hatching. It cannot wait for swimming lessons!

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Full answer key for the sorting activity:
  • (a) Duck swimming = Instinct
  • (b) Dog sitting on command = Learned
  • (c) Spider spinning a web = Instinct
  • (d) Dog shaking hands = Learned
  • (e) Bird building a nest = Mostly Instinct (varies by species; some improve with practice)
  • (f) Cat using a litter box = Learned
  • (g) Baby crying = Instinct
  • (h) Parrot talking = Learned
  • (i) Salmon swimming upstream = Instinct
  • (j) Puppy learning to sit = Learned

On nuanced cases: Bird nest building is a fascinating gray area. Many birds build species-typical nests instinctively, but studies show they improve with practice and experience over multiple breeding seasons. If your student catches this nuance and asks "but don't they get better at it?", celebrate that observation! It shows sophisticated thinking.

Extension ideas: Look up videos of orca mothers teaching their calves to beach themselves to catch seals. This is one of the most documented examples of deliberate teaching in the wild. Chimpanzee mothers have also been observed showing young chimps how to use tools (rocks to crack nuts). These cross the line from instinct into genuine teaching.

Human parallel: Walking is an interesting human case. Babies have the instinct to put one foot in front of the other, but the coordination develops through months of practice and encouragement. It is both instinct and learned. This is a great discussion point for curious students.
