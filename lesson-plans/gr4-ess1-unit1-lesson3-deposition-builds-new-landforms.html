🏞️ Deposition Builds New Landforms

Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 3 of 6

Grade: 4Duration: 45–55 minutesSubject: Earth Science, DepositionMaterials: Jar, sand, gravel, water
NGSS Standard: 4-ESS2-1 — Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define deposition as the dropping of sediment when water or wind loses energy.
  • Observe how larger particles settle before smaller particles.
  • Explain how deposition can build features such as deltas, bars, or layered sediments.

📦 Materials

  • Clear jar or bottle with lid
  • Small amount of gravel, sand, and soil
  • Water
  • Tray or plate
  • Spoon
  • Pencil

📖 Vocabulary

depositionThe dropping of sediment in a new place
deltaA landform built where a river drops sediment near a larger body of water
sediment layerA visible band of deposited material
energyThe ability of moving water or wind to carry material
settleTo sink or drop out of moving water

🔬 Background

Erosion does not move sediment forever. When water slows down, it can no longer carry as much material. The dropped material is called deposition. Deposition builds new landforms and adds layers to existing ones.

Large, heavy pieces usually settle first. Smaller pieces stay suspended longer and settle later. This pattern helps scientists infer what happened in the past when they study layered sediments.

Deposition is why rivers build sandbars, floodplains, and deltas. It is also why stormwater can leave muddy layers after a flood. Landforms are shaped not only by what gets removed, but also by what gets added.

🧪 Activity

Step 1: Put a spoonful each of gravel, sand, and soil into a clear jar. Fill it about three-quarters with water.
Step 2: Shake the jar strongly for 10 seconds so all the particles are mixed.
Step 3: Set the jar down and watch closely for one minute. Notice which particles settle first and which stay cloudy longer.
Step 4: After several minutes, sketch the layers you see from bottom to top.
Step 5: Carefully pour a little of the muddy water onto a flat tray. Watch what happens where the water slows and spreads out.
Step 6: Explain how the pattern you observed connects to river deltas, sandbars, or muddy flood deposits.

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Why do larger particles settle sooner than tiny ones?

Hint: They are heavier and harder for slower water to keep moving.

Q2: How can deposition make a place larger instead of smaller?

Hint: Added sediment can build up new surfaces such as bars, beaches, or deltas.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Observation timing: Coarse particles often settle within seconds, but very fine soil may stay cloudy for quite a while.
  • Rigor move: Push students to describe the order of settling, not just list the materials.
  • Link forward: In the next lesson, students will use maps and models to show where change happened.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Prediction: Gravel should usually be predicted first.
  2. Layer sketch: Strong sketches show larger particles at the bottom and finer particles higher up.
  3. Explain the pattern: Good explanations mention that slower water drops sediment instead of carrying it.
  4. Real-world connection: Accept delta, sandbar, beach, floodplain, or sediment layer.
