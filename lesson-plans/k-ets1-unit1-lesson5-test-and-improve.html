🧪 Test and Improve

Engineering Design — Build It! | Unit 1, Lesson 5 of 6

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🔧 Subject: Engineering Design — Build It! 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🧪 📊 🔄
Testing tells you the truth. Every result — pass or fail — is information. And information makes your next design better!
📋 NGSS Standard: K-2-ETS1-3 — Analyze data from tests of two objects designed to solve the same problem to compare the strengths and weaknesses of how each performs.

🎯 Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

  • Test their bridge against specific, measurable criteria
  • Record test results accurately in a data table
  • Redesign based on what the data shows

🔬 The Big Idea

In Lesson 4, you built your bridge and took a quick look at how it worked. Today, we get serious about testing. Real engineers define exact criteria — the rules a design must pass — and then test their design against those rules. Results are recorded, not just remembered. Then the magic happens: we use the data to make a better design!

📋 Official Bridge Test Criteria

🌉 Your Bridge Must Pass ALL 3 Tests:

1 The toy car or marble must cross the bridge completely from one side to the other.
2 The bridge must NOT touch the water at any point during or after the crossing.
3 The bridge must hold its shape for 10 seconds after the car crosses (no collapsing!).
💡 Key vocabulary: criteria, test, data, record, redesign, iterate, version

📦 Materials

🌉 For Testing and Rebuilding

  • Your bridge from Lesson 4 (or rebuild it quickly if it was taken apart)
  • 3–5 additional sheets of plain paper (for the redesign)
  • Additional tape (6–12 more inches)
  • Additional toothpicks (5–8)
  • Toy car or marble
  • Puddle tray with water
  • Timer (phone timer works great)
  • Student worksheet (last pages of this lesson)

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Official Test: Version 1 Bridge (5 min)
Run the official test on your Lesson 4 bridge. For each criterion:
  1. Test it clearly
  2. Record the result: ✅ Pass or ❌ Fail
  3. If it failed: write down WHY and what seemed to cause the problem
Be honest! If your bridge barely passed or the car almost fell in — record that too.
Step 2 — Analyze: What Needs to Improve? (5 min)
Look at your test results. Ask:
  • Which test(s) failed? Why did they fail?
  • What part of the bridge caused the problem?
  • What one change would most likely fix the biggest problem?
Write your answers on the worksheet before touching the bridge.
Step 3 — Redesign: Plan Version 2 (5 min)
Draw a quick sketch of your Version 2 design. What's different from Version 1? Focus on the ONE most important change first — don't try to fix everything at once! Mark what's changing and WHY.
Step 4 — Build Version 2 (10 min)
Build the improved bridge. You can use the old bridge as a starting point (modify it) or start fresh — whichever makes more sense based on your redesign plan.
Step 5 — Official Test: Version 2 (5 min)
Run the SAME 3-part official test on your Version 2 bridge. Record results. Compare:
  • Did Version 2 pass more tests than Version 1? ✅
  • Did anything get worse? (This happens sometimes — good to know!)
  • What would Version 3 fix? (Even if you don't build it)

🔄 The Engineering Iteration Cycle

Design → Build → Test → Analyze → Redesign → (repeat!)

Version 2 is always better than Version 1 — because Version 2 uses real information from the test, not just a guess. Celebrate the redesign! Getting to Version 2 is a real achievement.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ "Failing Forward" Discussion

Question: "In engineering, a test that shows your design DOESN'T work is called a 'failure.' But is it really a failure?"

→ Not at all! Every failed test tells you something important — what doesn't work, and why. Engineers call this "failing forward." A test that fails isn't wasted time — it's data you couldn't get any other way.

Real examples:

  • The Wright Brothers' first attempts at flying didn't work — but each attempt taught them something that made the next attempt better. Eventually, they flew!
  • Thomas Edison said he didn't fail 1,000 times making the lightbulb — he found 1,000 ways that didn't work. Each "failure" got him closer to the one way that DID work.

→ The question isn't "Did it work?" but "What did I learn?" A test that teaches you something is NEVER a failure.

🗣️ More to explore:
  • Why is it important to test against specific criteria instead of just seeing how it goes?
  • What would happen if engineers stopped after Version 1, even if it barely worked?
  • Can you think of something you've gotten better at through practice? Is that like engineering iteration?
🌟 What Real Engineers Know

The smartphone in your pocket went through thousands of test cycles before it reached you. Every app, every material, every curve was tested, failed, analyzed, and improved. What you hold is not Version 1 — it's Version 1,000. Engineering is patience + curiosity + willingness to try again. You're doing it right now.

🚀 Extensions

  • ⚖️ Load Test: How much weight can your bridge hold? Add pennies one at a time to the bridge until it collapses. Record how many it held.
  • 🌧️ Weather Test: What if it rained? Gently drip water on the bridge from above. Does it still hold?
  • 🏗️ Version 3: Based on Version 2 results, what would Version 3 look like? Draw it — even if you don't build it yet.
  • 📊 Bar Graph: Make a simple bar chart showing how many tests each version passed (out of 3). Visualizing data is a real science skill!

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Enforce the "one change at a time" rule: When redesigning, students often want to change everything. Coach them to pick the single most important change. This isolates variables — a key scientific thinking skill.
  • Be specific about "why it failed": "It fell in" is not specific enough. Coach: "Why did it fall in? Which part? What made that part weak?"
  • The "failing forward" discussion is critical: Many kids have been taught that failure is shameful. Explicitly reversing this framing — with the Wright Brothers and Edison examples — is one of the most important mindset lessons of this unit.
  • Assessment: Did students record data from BOTH version tests? Can they explain what changed between versions and WHY? Can they articulate what a "Version 3" might fix? That's success for Lesson 5.

🔑 PARENT/TEACHER ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

🧪 What Good Test Data Looks Like

There's no single right answer — but here's what to look for:

  • Version 1 should have at least 1 failure — if all 3 passed, challenge with a tougher test (faster car, heavier marble, longer hold time)
  • Version 2 should pass at least one more test than Version 1 — if not, discuss what might have gone wrong in the redesign process
  • "Notes" column should have observations, not just pass/fail — coaching prompt: "What did you see happen?"

🔄 What Good Analysis Looks Like

  • Strong: "Test 2 failed because the middle of the bridge sagged down and touched the water. The paper wasn't stiff enough in the middle."
  • Needs coaching: "It fell in." → Ask: "Which part? What happened right before it touched the water?"

🔄 What a Good Version 2 Change Looks Like

  • Changes ONE specific thing with a clear reason
  • Doesn't try to rebuild the whole bridge — focused improvement
  • Student can explain how the change addresses the specific failure

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Honest data recorded for both versions
  • Version 2 passed at least one more test than Version 1
  • Student can explain what changed and why
  • Student has thought about what Version 3 would improve
