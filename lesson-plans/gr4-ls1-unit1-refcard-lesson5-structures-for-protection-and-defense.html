Structures for Protection and Defense

Grade 4 · Unit 1
Not every survival structure helps an animal move or eat. Many structures protect the body by making the organism harder to bite, harder to grab, or harder to notice.
Key vocabulary
protectiondefenseshellspine
What to notice
  • Ask students which animal would be hardest for a predator to bite, grab, or injure and why.
  • Compare passive protection structures, like shells, with structures used during a defense behavior, like quills or horns.
  • Discuss that some structures protect by making the body harder to damage while others protect by making the animal harder to catch or approach.
Materials snapshot
  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • animal images showing different protection structures
  • optional object comparison items like a spoon, sponge, and hard shell
  • pencil
Talk-about prompts
  • Why is a shell a strong example of structure and function?
  • How is a defense structure different from a behavior alone?
  • Can one structure protect in more than one way?
Why it matters

Protection and defense structures include shells, quills, thick scales, horns, claws, and body coverings that make the organism safer. Some structures protect passively, like a shell. Others support a defense behavior, like spines raised when threatened. In each case, the structure contributes to survival.

Try this next: Design a simple “protection challenge” using soft fruit or crackers and different coverings. Which covering protects best? Then connect that idea back to shells, scales, or spines in animals.
