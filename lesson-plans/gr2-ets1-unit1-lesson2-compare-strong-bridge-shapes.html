Compare Strong Bridge Shapes

Engineering — Bridge Builders | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade⏱️ Duration: 40–50 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Bridge Builders
📄🔺🪙
Engineers compare shapes because the shape of a bridge changes how much weight it can hold.
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-2-ETS1-1 — Ask questions, make observations, and gather information about a situation people want to change to define a simple problem that can be solved through the development of a new or improved object or tool.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will compare simple bridge shapes and describe which shapes hold weight more successfully across the same gap.

💡 The Big Idea

Shape matters. A flat sheet and a folded sheet can be made from the same paper, but they do not act the same when they span a gap.

🔬 The Science

Engineers compare options before choosing a design. Even when the material is the same, changing the shape can make a structure stronger or weaker.

In this lesson, students test simple bridge shapes such as flat paper, folded paper, or a paper accordion between the same two supports.

The best choice is the one that fits the challenge criteria: cross the gap and hold more weight without sagging or slipping.

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review the Problem: Remind students that the bridge must cross the gap and stay up while holding weight.
Step 2 — Make Three Bridge Shapes: Create a flat strip, a folded strip, and a simple accordion or ridge shape from paper.
Step 3 — Test Fairly: Place each bridge across the same gap and add the same type of weight one at a time.
Step 4 — Observe What Happens: Notice which shapes sag quickly, which stay flatter, and which slide off the supports.
Step 5 — Choose the Best Starting Shape: Students explain which shape would be smartest to use in a bridge plan and why.

Discussion Questions

Why do engineers test more than one shape?Look for: it helps them compare and choose the stronger idea.
What made this test fair?Look for: the same gap and same type of weights were used each time.
What does it mean if a bridge sags?Look for: it bends down and may not be strong enough.
Why might a folded shape hold more than a flat sheet?Look for: the shape changes the strength even with the same paper.

Extensions

Extension: Try making one more new paper shape and predict where it would rank in strength.

Extension: Walk through your house and notice where folded or triangular shapes make objects stronger.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Keep the gap small enough that students can see success and failure quickly.

Teaching note: If results vary, that is okay. Focus students on comparing visible evidence instead of chasing one perfect answer.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Bridge Shape Test — Sample Reading

Accept results based on what the family actually observed. Strong answers compare sagging, slipping, or how much weight each shape held.

2️⃣ Best Starting Shape — What to Look For

Students should choose a shape and support it with test evidence, such as “The folded paper held more pennies before sagging.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student records observations for more than one shape.
  • Student identifies which shape performed best.
  • Student uses evidence from the test, not a random guess.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If one test slips instead of sagging, note that slipping is still useful evidence about the design.
  • If a student picks a favorite shape without evidence, ask what happened during the test.
