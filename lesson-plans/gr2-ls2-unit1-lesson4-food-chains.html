🌿 Food Chains: Who Eats Whom? 🦅

📋 NGSS Standard:

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will build a simple food chain with at least 4 links and explain the role of producers, consumers, and decomposers in an ecosystem.

producer consumer decomposer food chain food web ecosystem energy nutrients

🌱 The Big Idea

All living things need energy to survive. Plants get their energy directly from the sun — they make food using sunlight, water, and air. Animals can't do this, so they have to eat to get energy. A food chain shows how that energy moves from one living thing to another.

Here's the key insight: without plants, there would be no energy for anyone. Plants are the foundation of almost every food chain on Earth. Remove the plants, and the whole chain collapses.

🔬 The Science

Producers — Plants Make the Food

Plants are called producers because they produce (make) food. They use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide from the air to make sugar — this process is called photosynthesis. Every animal in the food chain is ultimately eating energy that started in a plant.

💡 Connection to Lesson 1: Remember those bean plants you've been growing? They're producers! Right now, those plants are capturing sunlight and turning it into food. Every leaf is a tiny solar panel.

Consumers — Animals Eat to Get Energy

Animals can't make their own food, so they eat other living things. They are called consumers. There are different kinds:

  • Herbivores — eat only plants (grasshoppers, rabbits, deer)
  • Carnivores — eat other animals (frogs, hawks, wolves)
  • Omnivores — eat both plants and animals (bears, raccoons, humans!)

Decomposers — Nature's Recyclers

When plants and animals die, decomposers break them down — mushrooms, worms, and bacteria. They return the nutrients back into the soil, which helps new plants grow. Without decomposers, dead things would just pile up forever and soil would run out of nutrients. They complete the cycle!

From Chain to Web

In real life, it's not a straight line. Grass also feeds rabbits. Rabbits also feed foxes. Foxes also eat frogs. These overlapping connections form a food web — much messier, but much more true to nature. When one species disappears from a web, the effects ripple outward in every direction.

📦 Materials

🧰 What You Need

  • Index cards or paper to draw food chain picture cards
  • Crayons or markers
  • Yarn or string (optional — to show food web connections)
  • Tape
  • Scissors
  • Printed worksheets (last pages of this lesson)

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Warm-Up: Who Eats Whom? (5 min)
Start a conversation: "What did you eat for breakfast today? Where did that food come from? And where did THAT come from?"
Keep tracing back. Cereal → grain → plants → sunlight. Orange juice → oranges → orange tree → sunlight. Almost every path leads back to plants and the sun.
Step 2 — Introduce the Chain (5 min)
Say: "Scientists have a special name for the path energy takes when one thing eats another — it's called a food chain."
Draw or display the chain: ☀️ → 🌿 Grass → 🦗 Grasshopper → 🐸 Frog → 🦅 Hawk → 🍄 Decomposer

Introduce vocabulary:
  • Producer — the plant at the start (makes food from sunlight)
  • Consumer — any animal that eats to get energy
  • Decomposer — the mushroom/worm/bacteria at the end (breaks things down, returns nutrients)
Step 3 — Make the Picture Cards (8 min)
Have kids draw and cut out 6 picture cards:
☀️Sunenergy source
🌿Grassproducer
🦗Grasshopperconsumer
🐸Frogconsumer
🦅Hawkconsumer
🍄Mushroomdecomposer
Arrange them in order with arrows showing energy flow. Label each one: producer, consumer, or decomposer.
Short on time? Print this lesson and let students cut out the picture cards from the role-card section instead of drawing every card from scratch.
Step 4 — Play "What If?" (10 min)
With the cards arranged, take turns removing one card and asking: "What happens to everything else?"
  • Remove the grass: Grasshoppers starve → frogs lose food → hawks lose food → whole chain collapses
  • Remove the frogs: Grasshoppers multiply out of control (eat too much grass) → hawks lose their main food source
  • Remove the hawks: Frog population explodes → they eat more grasshoppers → what happens to the grass?
  • Remove the decomposers: Dead things pile up, soil loses nutrients, new plants can't grow as well
Step 5 — Build a Food Web (7 min)
Add new cards: 🐇 Rabbit and 🦊 Fox.
  • Grass also feeds rabbits → connect with yarn or draw an arrow
  • Rabbits also feed foxes → connect
  • Foxes also eat frogs → connect
Now the "chain" has become a web! Notice how much more complicated (and resilient) it is. If frogs disappear, foxes can still eat rabbits. Redundancy in nature is a good thing.
Step 6 (Optional Active Version) — Food Chain Tag! (10 min)
If you have siblings, a co-op group, or outdoor space:
🌿Plants (2–3)ROOTED — can't move!
🦗Grasshoppers (2–3)Tag plants to "eat" them
🐸Frogs (1–2)Tag grasshoppers
🦅Hawk (1)Tags frogs
When "eaten" → sit down. Watch the ecosystem respond in real time! What happens when there are too many hawks? Too few plants? This is population dynamics they'll remember forever.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: "If all the frogs in a pond disappeared due to a disease, what would happen to the grasshoppers? What would happen to the hawks?"

Think about what the frogs eat, and what eats the frogs. If frogs are gone, what changes above and below them in the chain?

🗣️ Question 2: "Plants are called 'producers.' What do you think they produce — and why does that matter to every other animal in the ecosystem?"

Hint: what do plants make that nothing else in the chain can make? Where does the energy in a grasshopper's body originally come from?

🚀 Extensions

  • 🌳 Backyard Food Chain: Go outside — what plants are there? What insects? What birds? Build a real food chain from your own backyard.
  • 🔬 Decomposer Lab: Put a small piece of fruit outside. Check it every few days. Who shows up to decompose it?
  • 🗺️ Different Habitats: Build a food chain for an ocean, a desert, and an arctic habitat. How are they similar? Different?
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "What Do You Do With a Tail Like This?" by Jenkins & Page, or "Who Eats Who? Food Chains and Food Webs" by Bridget Heos
  • 🎨 Food Web Poster: Draw a large food web mural for the wall — have each child draw and label one organism with arrows connecting to everything it eats and everything that eats it.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Tabletop vs. Active: The tabletop card version works perfectly for homeschool — rich discussion happens around the table. The active tag version is a bonus for co-ops or when siblings are involved. Both teach the same concepts; active just adds kinesthetic memory.
  • Don't forget decomposers! Mushrooms, worms, and bacteria are constantly overlooked, but they're the unsung heroes — they break down dead material and return nutrients to the soil, completing the cycle. Without them, dead things would pile up and soil would become barren. Worth emphasizing.
  • Vocab: producer, consumer, decomposer, food chain, food web, ecosystem, energy, nutrients. Have kids use these words during discussion — not just on paper.
  • Chain vs. Web: Kids often think of a straight chain, but nature is always a web. Reinforce that "web" = more connections = more resilient = better for the ecosystem. Straight chains are fragile; webs absorb shocks.
  • Connection to Lesson 1: Those bean plants they've been growing are producers! They're capturing sunlight and making food right now. That's a real, living link in a food chain — help them see this.
  • Assessment: Can students correctly label producer, consumer, and decomposer in a food chain? Can they predict what happens when one organism is removed? That's success for 2nd grade.

Part 4: "What If?" Table — Suggested Answers

🌿 All the grass disappears:
🦗 Grasshoppers: Would starve and their population would crash — they have no food source.
🐸 Frogs: Would also decline because their food (grasshoppers) has disappeared. The whole chain collapses.
🐸 All the frogs disappear:
🦗 Grasshoppers: Population would explode — nothing is eating them anymore. They'd eat too much grass.
🦅 Hawks: Would struggle — their main food source is gone. They'd need to find other prey (rabbits, mice) or their numbers would drop.
🍄 All the decomposers disappear:
🌿 Plants (over time): Would gradually decline — the soil loses nutrients as dead material stops breaking down and being recycled.
🦗 Grasshoppers (over time): Would eventually decline as plants weaken and the whole chain loses its foundation.

💡 Discussion tip: For the frog question, walk students through the logic step-by-step: "If frogs are gone, who does that help? Who does it hurt?" This is the heart of understanding food webs — every removal has both a winner and a loser.

Discussion Question Answers

Q1 — Frogs disappear: Without frogs eating them, the grasshopper population would explode — too many grasshoppers would devour too much grass. Hawks would struggle because their main food source is gone — they'd have to find other prey or their population would decrease. This illustrates how removing ONE species sends ripple effects through the entire web.
Q2 — Plants as producers: Plants produce food — specifically sugar/glucose — using sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide (photosynthesis). Every animal in the food chain ultimately depends on this energy. Without producers, there would be no energy entering the ecosystem and all animal life would eventually starve. The sun's energy can only enter the food chain through plants.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student can build a food chain with at least 4 links (sun → producer → consumer → consumer).
  • Student can correctly label producer, consumer, and decomposer.
  • Student can explain (in their own words) that plants make food from sunlight.
  • Student can predict what happens to at least one other organism when one is removed from the chain.
  • Bonus: Student understands the difference between a food chain (linear) and a food web (multiple overlapping connections).
