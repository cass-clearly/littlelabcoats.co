📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need

📖 Vocabulary

Observation Looking carefully and describing exactly what you see, hear, or touch
Habitat The specific place where an animal or plant lives and finds what it needs
Species One kind of living thing (all dogs are one species; all robins are one species)
Diversity Having many different kinds of living things in one place
Micro-habitat A tiny habitat inside a bigger one (example: under a rock is a micro-habitat)
Field Journal A notebook where scientists write down what they observe in nature

🌍 Background

Scientists who study ecosystems spend a lot of time outdoors, looking carefully and writing down what they observe. This careful looking is called observation. A good field journal records what you see, where you saw it, the time, and the weather conditions. Even a small backyard or a crack in the sidewalk is full of living things waiting to be discovered.

Every habitat has a mix of living and nonliving things. Even a patch of concrete has nonliving things (concrete, sunlight, shadow) and living things (ants, weeds growing through cracks, maybe a bird landing for a moment). The richer the habitat — the more plants, soil types, and moisture it has — the more diverse the life you will find.

Scientists discover new species every year, even in places humans have lived for centuries. An acre of healthy soil contains billions of living things too small to see. One square foot of leaf litter can hold hundreds of insects and microorganisms. The world is full of life — you just have to slow down and look carefully.

🔬 Activity: 20-Minute Habitat Hunt

💡 Before you go: Complete the "Predictions" section of your Student Worksheet. Predicting first helps you notice what was surprising when you come back inside.
Step 1: Head outside with your field journal (Student Worksheet) and pencil. Set a timer for 20 minutes.
Step 2: Go slowly. Look under rocks, in grass, on leaves, in soil, and around plants. The slower you move, the more you find.
Step 3: Record every living thing you spot in your observation log. Write its name if you know it, or describe it: "small brown beetle with spots," "gray spider near web on fence."
Step 4: Record nonliving things too — types of soil, rocks, water sources (puddles, streams), sunlight, shadows.
Step 5: Choose 2 things to sketch in detail. Take your time. Label the parts you notice (legs, wings, color, size, where found).
Step 6: Come back inside and count: how many total living things did you find? How many different types (species)? Compare your actual findings to your predictions — what surprised you?

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: What did you find the most of? Was that what you expected?

Suggested answer: Most students find insects (especially ants) are the most numerous, followed by plants. Most people are surprised by how much life is in a tiny area once they slow down and look carefully. What is common varies by habitat — a lawn has lots of grass and ants; a garden has more variety; a weedy area near a fence might have the most diversity of all.

🗣️ Question 2: Did you find any micro-habitats — tiny habitats within the bigger habitat?

Suggested answer: Under a rock, under a log, in a crack in the sidewalk, inside a flower, or under a leaf — these are all micro-habitats. Each one has slightly different conditions (darker, moister, more protected) that suit different organisms. Scientists call these "microenvironments." Even a single flower is a habitat for tiny insects and spiders.

🗣️ Question 3: What was the most surprising living thing you found? What do you think it needs to survive?

Suggested answer: Guide students to think about what the organism needs: food, water, shelter, and the right temperature. Then ask: does this place provide those things? Why is this animal or plant here and not somewhere else? Every organism is in its habitat for a reason — because that specific place meets all of its needs.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Any outdoor access works: A city sidewalk, a balcony with a potted plant, a park, or a driveway edge — anywhere living things can be found. If outdoor access is truly impossible, create an indoor "habitat hunt" using houseplants or a cup of soil from outside brought in to examine.
  • Bring a magnifying glass: Even a cheap plastic one transforms this activity. The world under a magnifying glass is endlessly fascinating for children. Under 10x magnification, an ant becomes an alien, a leaf surface becomes a landscape.
  • Best timing: Morning hours, when insects are active. Even 15 minutes outside is enough for this lesson.
  • Taxonomy tip: Students will find things they cannot name. That is great! Scientists encounter unnamed things all the time. Describe the features carefully and look it up later using a field guide app like iNaturalist (free, works offline, identifies from photos).
  • Photography extension: If your student has access to a tablet or phone camera, let them photograph their 2 detailed sketch subjects. This creates a digital field journal and often motivates more careful observation.
  • Connection to Lesson 1: Ask students to connect what they found to the Ecosystem Map from Lesson 1. Did they find more living or nonliving things? What nonliving things did the living things depend on?
