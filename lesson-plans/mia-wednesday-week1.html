📊 Probability Games 15 min

Math — Probability & Data Collection

Time to become a probability scientist! 🔬 You'll run real experiments with dice and coins, make predictions, then see if the real results match what you expected. Spoiler: sometimes math is surprising!

  1. Predict first! Before you roll or flip, write down what you think will happen.
  2. Experiment! Do 20 coin flips and 24 dice rolls. Tally every result on the worksheet.
  3. Compare! Were your predictions close? Why or why not?
🪙

Coin Flip

2 outcomes: Heads or Tails

Theoretical: 50/50 chance each

🎲

Dice Roll

6 outcomes: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6

Theoretical: each number = 1 in 6 chance

💡 Fun Fact: Even though each side of a coin has a 50% chance, you might get 14 heads and only 6 tails in 20 flips! The more you flip, the closer you'll get to 50/50. That's called the Law of Large Numbers!

🔍 Compare Two Sources 15 min

Information Literacy — Evaluating Sources

Real researchers never trust just one source! 🧐 Read both articles below about the same topic, then figure out: what's the same? What's different? Which one do you trust more — and why?

  1. Read Source A and Source B carefully.
  2. Fill out the comparison chart on the worksheet.
  3. Decide which source is more reliable and explain your reasoning.
Source A

🐝 "Why Are Bees So Important?" — National Wildlife Federation (nwf.org)

Bees are one of the most important pollinators on Earth. About one-third of the food we eat depends on pollination by bees, including apples, almonds, blueberries, and cucumbers. When bees visit flowers to collect nectar, pollen sticks to their fuzzy bodies and gets carried to the next flower. This is how many plants reproduce.

Scientists have found that bee populations are declining due to pesticides, habitat loss, and climate change. According to the U.S. Department of Agriculture, beekeepers lost about 48% of their honeybee colonies between 2022 and 2023. Researchers are studying ways to protect bees, including planting wildflower gardens and reducing pesticide use.

Published: March 2024 • Author: Dr. Sarah Chen, Wildlife Biologist

Source B

🐝 "Bees Are Dying and It's Really Bad!" — CoolAnimalFacts.blog

OMG, did you know that bees are going EXTINCT?! 😱 Basically ALL the bees are dying and if they disappear, we'll have NO food left at all. Every single fruit and vegetable needs bees to grow. Without bees, humans would starve in like 4 years!!

The main reason bees are dying is because of 5G towers and cell phone radiation. I saw a video about it and it totally makes sense. Also, some farmers are being really mean to bees. We need to save them ASAP or the whole planet is doomed! 🌍💀

💡 Source Detective Tip: Ask yourself — Who wrote it? Where was it published? Do they give evidence or just opinions? Do other sources agree?

🧠 Character Court 10 min

Reading — Argument & Text Evidence

Welcome to Character Court! ⚖️ Today you're both the lawyer and the jury. Pick a decision a character made in whatever you're reading, then argue both sides — FOR and AGAINST — using evidence from the text.

  1. Think of a big decision a character made. (Did they lie? Leave home? Trust someone? Break a rule?)
  2. Argue FOR the decision — why it was the right call.
  3. Argue AGAINST — why it was a mistake.
  4. Deliver your verdict: Was it ultimately the right decision?
✅ Example — Character Court in Action

Character: Charlotte (from Charlotte's Web)

Decision: Charlotte decided to write words in her web to save Wilbur.

FOR: "Charlotte saved Wilbur's life! On page 80, she says, 'I am not going to let them kill you.' She used her talent to help a friend — that's brave and selfless."

AGAINST: "Charlotte wore herself out and used all her energy. She could have focused on her own egg sac earlier. Some might say she risked her own life for a pig."

Verdict: It was the right decision because true friendship sometimes means sacrifice.

💡 Court Rule: You MUST use evidence from the text for both sides! A quote, a page number, or a specific detail. No "I just think so" allowed in this court! 🔨

✏️ Edit Someone Else's Work 10 min

Writing — Editing for Grammar, Structure & Clarity

Today you're the editor-in-chief! ✨ A student named "Alex" wrote this paragraph, but it's got problems — spelling mistakes, grammar goofs, confusing sentences, and weak structure. Your job: find the errors and make it shine!

  1. Read the paragraph below carefully.
  2. Use the worksheet to mark every error you find.
  3. Rewrite the corrected version in your best writing.

📝 Alex's paragraph — find and fix the errors!

Last summer me and my family went to the grand canyon. it was really really big and also very deep. We hiked down a trail that was called bright Angel trail and it taked us almost the hole day. My dad bringed sandwiches and waters but he forgetted the sunscreen so we all got sunburned real bad. Their were lots of other hikers to. The most best part was when we seed a condor flying over the canyon it was huge like a airplane almost. I want to went back their someday because it was the most beautifulest place I ever saw in my life ever.

🔍 What to look for
  • Spelling errors — words that aren't spelled right
  • Grammar mistakes — wrong verb forms, "me and my" vs. "my family and I"
  • Punctuation — missing capitals, periods, commas
  • Repetition — words used too many times or unnecessary words
  • Clarity — run-on sentences, confusing parts
💡 Editor Tip: Read the paragraph THREE times — once for spelling, once for grammar, and once for "does this sound good?" Good editors don't try to catch everything at once!
