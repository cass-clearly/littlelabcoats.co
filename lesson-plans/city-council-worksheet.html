🏛️ The City Council & the Mayor 🏛️

How They Work Together to Run Your City!

A city council meeting — where big decisions are made for your community!

🎯 The Big Idea

In our last lesson, we learned all about mayors — the leaders of our cities. But here's something really important: the mayor doesn't run the city alone! There's a whole team called the City Council that works with the mayor. Think of it like this: the mayor is the captain of the team, but the council members are the other players. You need EVERYONE working together to win! 🏆

🤔 What IS a City Council?

🏫 Classroom Analogy — Let's Make It Simple!

Imagine your school is a city. Now picture this:

👑 The Class President = the Mayor (one leader for the whole school)
🙋 The Class Representatives = the City Council (one person from each classroom who speaks for their classmates)
📋 The School Rules = City Laws (called ordinances)
🧒 The Students = the Citizens (that's you!)

The class president can't just make rules all by themselves. They have to talk to the class representatives, and the representatives vote on whether a new rule is a good idea. If MOST of them agree, the rule passes! 🗳️

A City Council is a group of people — usually between 5 and 15 members — who are elected by the people of the city to make important decisions together. They meet regularly (usually every week or two) to discuss problems, hear from citizens, and vote on new rules and plans for the city.

🌟 Key Point: The city council is a GROUP that makes decisions TOGETHER by voting. No single council member can make a decision alone — they need a majority (more than half) to agree! That's what makes it fair.

🗳️ How Are Council Members Elected?

Cities are often divided into districts — like puzzle pieces! Each district picks one council member.

This is where it gets interesting! There are two main ways cities choose their council members:

🍕 Way #1: Districts (or Wards)

Think of it like a pizza! 🍕

Imagine your city is a big pizza. Now cut it into slices — maybe 5, 7, or 9 slices. Each slice is called a district (some cities call them wards).

Each slice/district gets to pick ONE person to represent them on the city council. So if you live in District 3, you only vote for the person who will represent District 3. Your neighbor in District 4 votes for a different person!

Why is this good? Every neighborhood has someone who speaks for THEM specifically. If your part of town needs a new stoplight, YOUR council member fights for it!

🌐 Way #2: At-Large Elections

Think of it like a school-wide vote!

In some cities, there are NO districts. Instead, everyone in the whole city votes for ALL the council members. It's like if the entire school voted for every single class representative — not just their own classroom's representative.

Why is this good? Council members care about the WHOLE city, not just one neighborhood.
💡 Fun Fact: Some cities use BOTH methods! They might have 5 council members from districts AND 2 members elected at-large. Every city gets to decide for itself! 🏙️

📋 Who Can Run for Council?

The rules are usually pretty simple:
  • You must be a citizen (live in the city)
  • You must be old enough (usually 18 or older)
  • If it's a district election, you usually have to live in that district
  • You sign up, collect signatures from neighbors who support you, and start campaigning!
Campaigns at the local level are usually much simpler than presidential campaigns. Council candidates might knock on doors, put up signs in yards, go to neighborhood meetings, and talk to people at the grocery store! It's very personal. 🤝
⏰ How Long Do They Serve?
Most council members serve a term of 2 or 4 years. After that, they can run again if they want to keep their seat. Some cities have term limits — meaning you can only serve a certain number of terms and then you have to let someone else have a turn!

🗳️ Mayor vs. Council: How Elections Are Different

This is a really important difference, so let's make it super clear:

👑 Mayor Election The WHOLE CITY votes for ONE person
✅ Everyone in the city votes
✅ Only ONE winner
✅ Represents the entire city
🙋 Council Election Each DISTRICT picks its own person
✅ Only your district votes
✅ Multiple winners (one per district)
✅ Represents one neighborhood
🎯 Easy Way to Remember:
Mayor = Everyone picks ONE leader for the whole city (like choosing a class president — everyone votes!)
Council = Each neighborhood picks their OWN representative (like each classroom picking one representative!)

📋 What Does the City Council DO?

Council members have some really important jobs! Here are their main powers:

📜 1. Vote on New Laws (Ordinances)
When the city needs a new rule — like "no parking on Main Street during parades" — the council discusses it and votes. If the majority says YES, it becomes a law! These city laws are called ordinances.
💰 2. Approve the City Budget
The city budget is like a giant allowance plan — it decides where ALL the city's money goes. New playground? More firefighters? Fix the library roof? The council votes on how to spend the money. This is one of their MOST powerful tools!
👂 3. Listen to Residents (Public Meetings)
Council meetings are open to the public! Citizens can come, stand up, and tell the council what they think. Want a stop sign on your street? Go to a council meeting and ask! This is called a public hearing.
✅❌ 4. Approve or Reject the Mayor's Plans
When the mayor has a big idea — like building a new community center — they can't just DO it. They have to present the idea to the council, and the council votes on whether to approve it. The council is like a checkpoint!
🔍 5. Oversee City Departments
The council keeps an eye on how the city's departments (police, fire, parks, etc.) are doing. They ask questions, review reports, and make sure everything is running smoothly.

⚖️ Mayor vs. Council — Who Has the Power?

This is the KEY part! The mayor and council have different powers, and they check each other — just like the national government has checks and balances! ⚖️

👑 The MAYOR... ⇄ 🏛️ The COUNCIL...
Proposes new ideas and plans → Approves or rejects those plans by voting
Runs the city day-to-day (like a manager) → Sets the rules the city has to follow
Hires department heads (police chief, fire chief) → Controls the budget (decides where money goes)
Speaks for the city publicly → Listens to what residents want
Can veto (say NO to) council decisions in some cities → Can override the veto with enough votes!
⚖️ Checks & Balances at the City Level! Neither the mayor NOR the council has ALL the power. They have to work together. If the mayor goes too far, the council can stop them. If the council makes a bad rule, the mayor can veto it. It's designed so no one person or group gets too much power!

🔑 Strong Mayor vs. Weak Mayor — It Depends on the City!

Not all mayors have the same amount of power! It depends on the city's rules (called a charter).

💪 "Strong Mayor" cities: The mayor has MORE power — they can hire and fire department heads, create the budget, and sometimes veto council votes. They're more like a boss. (Examples: New York City, Chicago)

🤝 "Weak Mayor" cities: The mayor has LESS individual power — the council makes most of the big decisions, and the mayor is more like a team captain who leads meetings but shares power equally. Sometimes there's also a city manager who handles daily operations.

Neither system is better or worse — they're just different! Each city picks the system that works best for them. 🏙️

🤝 How Do They Work TOGETHER?

Teamwork makes the dream work — the mayor and council need each other!

Let's walk through a real example of how the mayor and council work together. Let's say the kids in the city want a new playground! 🛝

Step 1: 👑 The Mayor has an idea: "Let's build a new playground in Oak Park!"
⬇️
Step 2: 🏛️ The Mayor presents the plan to the City Council at a meeting
⬇️
Step 3: 👂 The Council holds a public hearing — citizens come and share their thoughts! "We love it!" "Can it have swings?" "How much will it cost?"
⬇️
Step 4: 🗣️ Council members discuss and debate: "Is there enough money in the budget?" "Is Oak Park the best location?"
⬇️
Step 5: 🗳️ The Council VOTES! If the majority (more than half) say YES...
⬇️
Step 6: ✅ It's approved! The Mayor works with city departments to actually BUILD the playground! 🎉
💡 Notice: The Mayor had the idea AND does the building — but the Council had to say YES first. And citizens got to share their opinions too! That's democracy at work at the local level! 🇺🇸

💬 Discussion Questions

🔵 4th Grade Discussion
  • Why is it important that the mayor can't just make decisions alone?
  • If you were a council member, what's the first thing you'd want to vote on?
  • What are the advantages of districts? What about at-large elections?
  • Why do you think public hearings are important? What if citizens never got to speak?
  • Would you rather be the mayor or a council member? Why?
  • How is the mayor-council relationship similar to checks and balances in the national government?
  • Why might a city choose a "strong mayor" system? Why might another choose a "weak mayor" system?

🎭 Fun Activity: City Council Meeting Role-Play!

🗳️ Run Your Own Council Meeting!

How to play:

  1. Pick one student to be the Mayor
  2. Pick 5–7 students to be Council Members
  3. The rest of the class are Citizens who can speak during public comment
  4. Cut out one of the Scenario Cards below
  5. The Mayor presents the idea → Citizens share opinions → Council discusses → Council VOTES!
  6. Majority wins! Count the votes!

✂️ Scenario Cards (Cut These Out!)

🛝 Scenario #1: The New Playground
The mayor wants to spend $50,000 to build a brand-new playground in Riverside Park. Some citizens think the money should be used to fix potholes on Main Street instead. What does the council decide?
🐕 Scenario #2: The Dog Park Debate
Citizens are asking for an off-leash dog park in the empty lot on Elm Street. But some neighbors are worried about noise and mess. The mayor supports the idea. Does the council approve it? With any conditions?
🚗 Scenario #3: The Speed Limit Change
Parents near Oakwood Elementary want the speed limit on School Street lowered from 30 mph to 15 mph during school hours. Some business owners say it will cause traffic jams. How does the council vote?
📚 Scenario #4: Save the Library!
The city is running low on money. The mayor says the city might need to close the library on Saturdays to save $20,000. The librarian and many families say Saturday is the busiest day. Can the council find another solution?

🚀 Extensions

  • 🔎 Research Your Council: Find out how many council members YOUR city has! What are their names? What district do they represent?
  • 📺 Watch a Real Meeting: Many cities stream their council meetings online. Watch 10 minutes of one — what did they discuss?
  • ✉️ Write to Your Council Member: Write a letter about something you'd like to see changed in your neighborhood!
  • 📊 Compare Two Cities: Pick two cities and compare their government structures. How many council members? Strong or weak mayor? Districts or at-large?
  • 🗺️ Draw a District Map: Draw a map of your city and divide it into districts. How would you make the districts fair?

👩‍🏫 Parent / Teacher Notes

  • This is a follow-up to the "Who Runs Your City? — All About Mayors!" worksheet. Students should have that foundation before starting this lesson.
  • The #1 confusion point: Students struggle with the idea that the mayor can't just "do things" — they need council approval. The playground flowchart is designed to make this click. Walk through it step by step!
  • For the role-play: Give students 15–20 minutes. The Mayor should practice presenting, council members should ask questions, and citizens should advocate for their position. Rotate roles if time allows!
  • District vs. at-large: The pizza analogy works really well. Actually draw a circle on the board, divide it into slices, and label each one. Then contrast it with "the whole pizza votes together."
  • ⚠️ Common misconception: Students often think council members are appointed or hired. Emphasize they are ELECTED — people vote for them, just like the mayor!
  • Strong vs. weak mayor: Keep this simple. Don't get into council-manager systems in depth — just plant the seed that not all cities work the same way.
  • Keep it nonpartisan: Focus on the STRUCTURE and PROCESS, not specific political issues or parties.
  • Assessment: The worksheet pages (Venn diagram, fill-in-the-blank, true/false, matching, and short answer) provide thorough formative assessment.

🤔 Short Answer — Example Responses

Accept any reasonable response that shows understanding!

1. Steps from idea to stop sign:
The resident attends a public hearing or contacts their council member. The council member brings the idea to a council meeting. The council discusses it and votes. If a majority says yes, the mayor signs it (or can veto). If approved, the city's transportation department installs the stop sign.
2. Mayor vs. council member elections:
The mayor is elected by EVERYONE in the whole city — all citizens vote for the same person. In a district system, the city is divided into sections (like slices of pizza), and only people in each section vote for their own council member. So the mayor represents the whole city, while a council member represents their neighborhood.
3. Why both a mayor AND a council?
Having both prevents one person from having too much power (checks and balances). The council makes sure different neighborhoods are represented. If the mayor makes a bad decision, the council can vote against it. It's like having teammates — more perspectives lead to better decisions for everyone.
4. Strong mayor vs. weak mayor:
In a strong mayor city, the mayor has more power — they can hire/fire department heads, create the budget, and veto council decisions. In a weak mayor city, the council has more power and the mayor is more of a figurehead. The "weak mayor" system gives the council more power.
5. Disagreeing with the mayor about the pool:
As a council member, you could: speak against it at the council meeting, vote NO, rally other council members to vote no too (you need a majority), hold a public hearing so citizens can share their opinions, or propose an alternative (like reducing pool hours instead of closing it entirely).
