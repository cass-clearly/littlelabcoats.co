Water Transport Inside Plants

Grade 4 · Unit 2
Plants need more than roots that absorb water. They also need internal structures that move water upward to stems, leaves, and flowers.
Key vocabulary
internal structuretransportpathwayabsorb
What to notice
  • Set up or discuss a classic colored-water observation with celery or a flower.
  • Ask students what changed and what that change suggests about water moving inside the plant.
  • Connect the visible result to the hidden structure: internal pathways carry water upward.
Materials snapshot
  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • celery stalk or white flower
  • cup, water, food coloring
  • pencil
Talk-about prompts
  • What evidence suggests water moved inside the plant?
  • Why is water transport important after the roots absorb water?
  • How is this lesson about internal structures, not just visible parts?
Why it matters

Water moves from roots upward through internal transport tissues inside the plant. In classroom observations, colored water in celery or white flowers can help students see that water reaches different plant parts through internal pathways. These internal structures support survival because the plant must move water where it is needed for growth and food-making processes.
