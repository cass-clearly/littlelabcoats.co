🚗 Forces & Motion: Rocket Cars! 🎈

Engineering Challenge — Design, Build, Test, and Improve!

📚 Grades: 3rd & 5th ⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-PS2-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence of the effects of balanced and unbalanced forces
3-PS2-2 — Make observations and/or measurements of an object's motion to predict future motion
5-PS2-1 — Support an argument that gravitational force is directed down
3-5-ETS1-1 — Define a simple design problem

3-5-ETS1-3 — Plan and carry out fair tests to improve solutions

🎯 The Big Idea

Forces have direction and strength — and they determine how objects move! When forces are unbalanced, objects accelerate (speed up, slow down, or change direction). In this hands-on engineering challenge, students design and build balloon-powered cars, then test how changing the direction of force affects speed, distance, and path. This lesson builds on last week's theory with real experimentation and iteration!

💡 Key vocabulary: force, direction, acceleration, balanced forces, unbalanced forces, friction, gravity, Newton's laws

🔬 The Science

🔍 Forces: The Pushes and Pulls That Make Things Move

A force is a push or a pull. Forces can:

  • Start motion (make something move from rest)
  • Stop motion (bring something to a halt)
  • Speed things up (accelerate forward)
  • Slow things down (decelerate)
  • Change direction (turn, curve, bounce)
👐 Push Force away from you
🪝 Pull Force toward you
🧲 Gravity Pulls DOWN to Earth
🛑 Friction Slows things down

🎯 The Most Important Rule:

Forces have DIRECTION and MAGNITUDE!

Direction = which way the force points (→ ← ↑ ↓)

Magnitude = how strong the force is (small push vs. BIG PUSH!)

🛠️ The Engineering Challenge

🏗️ Your Mission: Design a Balloon-Powered Car!

The Problem: Build a vehicle powered ONLY by a balloon. Test how the direction of the force (where you point the balloon) affects the car's motion.

Design Requirements:
  • ✅ Must have wheels (or something that rolls)
  • ✅ Must be powered by ONE balloon (no motors, no hands pushing after launch!)
  • ✅ Must allow you to change the balloon's direction between tests
  • ✅ Must travel at least 3 feet in one test
Success Criteria (what makes a GREAT car?):
  • 🎯 Distance: How far can it go?
  • 🏎️ Speed: How fast does it accelerate?
  • 📐 Straight Path: Does it travel in a straight line or veer off?
  • 🔄 Adjustability: Can you easily change force direction?

💡 Engineering Tip: There's NO "right" design! You'll test, observe problems, and IMPROVE. Real engineers do this hundreds of times. Embrace failure — it teaches you what works!

📦 Materials

🔧 Building Supplies (per student or team)

Required:
  • 1 balloon (any size — bigger = more force!)
  • Straw (regular or bendy)
  • Tape (masking or duct tape works great)
  • 4 bottle caps OR 4 plastic lids (for wheels)
  • 2 wooden skewers OR 2 straws (for axles)
  • 1 piece of cardboard or foam board (for car body, ~6"×4")
Optional (for creative designs):
  • Rubber bands
  • Paper clips
  • Index cards
  • Scissors
  • Hot glue gun (parent supervision!)
  • Lightweight plastic cups or boxes

🧰 Testing Equipment

  • Measuring tape or meter stick
  • Stopwatch or phone timer
  • Masking tape (to mark starting line)
  • Smooth, flat testing surface (hallway, driveway, large table)
  • Pencils & printed Student Worksheet (last page)
💡 Setup Tip: Clear a straight path at least 10-15 feet long. Tile, hardwood, or smooth concrete work best. Carpet = too much friction!

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review Forces Concept (5 min):
Use the diagrams above. Ask: "What happens when you push something? What if you push it harder? What if two people push from opposite sides?" Review balanced vs. unbalanced forces. Emphasize that direction matters — a force to the left is different from a force to the right!
Step 2 — Introduce the Challenge (5 min):
Show the materials. Explain: "You're going to build a car powered by escaping air from a balloon. The air pushing OUT creates a force that pushes the car FORWARD. Your job is to make it go as far and as straight as possible — then test how changing force direction affects motion."
Step 3 — Design & Build (15–20 min):
  • Sketch first! Have students draw their design before building (use Student Worksheet)
  • Basic design idea:
    1. Poke axles (skewers/straws) through cardboard base
    2. Attach wheels (bottle caps) to axle ends with tape
    3. Tape straw to TOP of car body
    4. Thread balloon onto straw, secure with tape at balloon neck
    5. Blow up balloon through straw, pinch straw end, place car on floor, release!
  • Encourage creativity! Some kids may want vertical balloon placement, some horizontal. Let them experiment!
Step 4 — Test Run #1: Baseline (5 min):
Inflate balloon, place car at starting line, release! Measure distance traveled. Record on Student Worksheet. Note any problems (car veered left? Flipped over? Wheels fell off?).
Step 5 — Improve & Test Again (10 min):
Based on what happened, make ONE change at a time:
  • Adjust balloon angle (point it up/down/sideways)
  • Add weight to front or back for balance
  • Make wheels rounder or smoother
  • Reduce friction (tape over rough edges)
Test again. Record results. Compare to Test #1.
Step 6 — Force Direction Experiments (10 min):
Now systematically change the direction of the balloon force:
  • Point balloon straight back (force pushes forward) → measure distance
  • Point balloon angled up (force pushes forward + down) → measure distance
  • Point balloon angled down (force pushes forward + up) → measure distance
  • Point balloon sideways (force pushes to one side) → what happens?
Record everything! Which direction gave the longest distance? Why?
Step 7 — Discuss & Analyze (5–10 min):
Use discussion questions below. Connect observations to force concepts. Ask: "How is this like Newton's Third Law?" (For every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction — air pushes back, car pushes forward!)
⚠️ Common Build Issues & Fixes:
  • Wheels don't spin: Axle too tight. Use thinner axle or widen wheel holes.
  • Car flips backward: Too much upward force. Point balloon more horizontally or add weight to front.
  • Car veers to one side: Unbalanced weight or one wheel dragging. Check all wheels spin freely.
  • Balloon falls off: Secure balloon neck to straw with multiple wraps of tape.
  • Not enough power: Bigger balloon! Or reduce friction (smoother wheels, lighter car).

💬 Discussion Questions

🟠 For 3rd Graders
Q: What force makes the car move?
Answer: The air pushing out of the balloon creates a force. That force pushes the car forward (Newton's Third Law — air pushes backward, car pushes forward).
Q: What forces are working AGAINST your car (trying to stop it)?
Answer: Friction (wheels rubbing on floor, axles rubbing) and air resistance slow it down. Gravity pulls it down, keeping wheels on the ground.
Q: When you pointed the balloon in different directions, what happened?
Answer: Pointing straight back = farthest distance. Pointing up = car might lift off ground or flip. Pointing sideways = car curves or spins. Direction of force determines direction of motion!
Q: Is the force balanced or unbalanced when the car is moving?
Answer: UNBALANCED! The balloon force is stronger than friction (at first), so the car accelerates. As the balloon deflates, friction wins and the car stops.
Q: Draw arrows on your car showing all the forces acting on it.
Answer: Arrow pointing forward (balloon force), arrow pointing backward (friction + air resistance), arrow pointing down (gravity), arrow pointing up (normal force from ground). Good visual on Student Worksheet!
🟣 For 5th Graders (add these)
Q: Explain Newton's Third Law using your balloon car as evidence.
Answer: "For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction." The balloon pushes air OUT the back (action). The air pushes the car FORWARD with equal force (reaction). Evidence: the harder the air escapes (bigger balloon, faster release), the faster the car accelerates.
Q: How does the DIRECTION of force affect acceleration?
Answer: Only the component of force in the direction of motion contributes to forward acceleration. If you angle the balloon up, some force goes into lifting (fighting gravity) instead of pushing forward, so you get less forward acceleration. Straight-back balloon = maximum forward force.
Q: Graph your test results. What pattern do you see?
Answer: (Students create bar graph or line graph showing distance vs. test number.) Pattern: improvements increase distance until you hit limits (friction, balloon size). Consistent testing conditions = more reliable data.
Q: Why does the car eventually stop? Use the word "net force."
Answer: At first, balloon force > friction, so net force is forward → car accelerates. As balloon deflates, balloon force decreases. When balloon force = friction, net force = 0 → car moves at constant speed (briefly). When balloon is empty, friction > balloon force, so net force is backward → car decelerates and stops.
Q: How is gravitational force affecting your car? Where is it directed?
Answer: Gravity pulls the car DOWN toward Earth (always directed toward the center of Earth). This keeps wheels on the ground, which creates friction. Without gravity, there'd be no friction — but the car also couldn't "grip" the ground to move forward! Gravity helps AND hinders.
Q: Design experiment: How could you test if a heavier car goes farther or shorter than a light car?
Answer: Add weight (coins, washers) to your car. Keep balloon size and direction the same. Test 3 times with each weight. Compare average distances. Hypothesis: heavier = more friction = shorter distance (but also more momentum, so results may vary!).

🚀 Extensions

  • 🏁 Speed Challenge: Set up a 5-foot track. Time how fast each car completes it. Calculate speed (distance ÷ time). Who has the fastest car?
  • 📊 Friction Investigation: Test your car on different surfaces (tile, carpet, wood, concrete). Graph distance traveled on each. Which surface has the most friction? The least?
  • 🎯 Target Practice: Place a cup 6 feet away. Can you design your car to stop INSIDE the cup? This requires precise control of force magnitude!
  • 🔬 Balloon Size Test: Use 3 different balloon sizes (small, medium, large). Test the same car with each. Graph results. Does bigger always mean farther?
  • 🏗️ Advanced Engineering: Add a steering mechanism (moveable straw holder) so you can aim the car. Add a brake (drag flap that lowers). Add cargo space and test weight limits!
  • 🌍 Real-World Connection: Research rocket propulsion, jet engines, or how squid move through water. All use the same principle — push something one way, you go the other way!
  • 📐 5th Grade Math: Calculate acceleration. Measure distance traveled in 1 second, 2 seconds, 3 seconds. Is acceleration constant or does it change? Why?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Mess level: Low! Just tape and cardboard. Do it outside or in a hallway for maximum run distance.
  • Time management: Building takes longer than you think. If short on time, pre-build ONE example car so kids see the concept, then let them modify/improve it instead of starting from scratch.
  • For younger kids (3rd): Simplify to: build basic car together (adult helps), then kids experiment with balloon direction. Focus on observation, not complex data analysis.
  • For older kids (5th): Push the engineering process hard. Require sketches BEFORE building. Require data tables with multiple trials. Ask them to write a conclusion paragraph citing evidence.
  • Differentiation: Struggling kids: provide a "kit" (pre-cut cardboard, pre-poked holes). Advanced kids: add constraints ("car must carry a penny without dropping it" or "must turn a corner").
  • ⚠️ Physics misconception alert: Kids often think "the balloon pushes the car." Clarify: the balloon pushes air backward, and the air pushes the car forward. This is Newton's Third Law!
  • Safety: If using hot glue, adult supervision required. Skewers are pointy — clip off sharp ends with scissors or pliers.
  • Testing surface matters! Rough surfaces = high friction = short distances. Smooth tile or wood = low friction = long distances. This is a teachable moment!
  • Balloon tips: Long, skinny balloons (like modeling balloons) work GREAT — they release air slowly and steadily, giving longer run times.
  • What if it doesn't work? GOOD! Engineering is iterative. Ask: "What happened? Why do you think? What could you change?" Failure is data!

👩‍🏫 Parent Answer Key 👨‍🏫

Expected Observations & Common Results

Design Phase:
  • Most successful designs have: low center of gravity, lightweight body, freely-spinning wheels, balloon pointed straight backward
  • Common first-attempt problems: wheels don't spin (axle too tight), car flips (balloon angled up too much), car veers (unbalanced weight)
Test Results Patterns:
  • Straight-back balloon: Longest distance (all force goes into forward motion)
  • Angled-up balloon: Shorter distance, may lift front wheels or flip (force wasted fighting gravity)
  • Angled-down balloon: Moderate distance, may "jump" at launch (extra downward force increases friction initially)
  • Sideways balloon: Car spins in circles or curves sharply (unbalanced side force)
Forces Acting on Car (Question 1):
  • ➡️ Forward: Reaction force from balloon (air pushes out back, car pushes forward)
  • ⬅️ Backward: Friction (wheels + axles) and air resistance
  • ⬇️ Down: Gravity (always toward center of Earth)
  • ⬆️ Up: Normal force from ground (equal and opposite to gravity)

Sample Answers to Key Questions

Q2 (Balanced or Unbalanced):

"Unbalanced! At first, the balloon force was stronger than friction, so the car sped up (accelerated). As the balloon deflated, the forces became more balanced, and eventually friction was stronger, so the car slowed down and stopped."

Q3 (Forces Against the Car):

"Friction from the wheels rubbing on the floor and the axles rubbing inside the wheels. Also air resistance pushing against the front of the car."

Q4 (Why Did It Stop):

"The balloon ran out of air, so there was no more forward force. Friction was the only force left, so it slowed the car down until it stopped."

Q5 (Newton's Third Law - 5th grade):

"Newton's Third Law says 'for every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction.' The balloon pushed air out the back (action), and the air pushed the car forward (reaction) with the same amount of force but in the opposite direction. Evidence: bigger balloon = more air pushed out = stronger reaction force = car went farther."

Q6 (Direction & Acceleration - 5th grade):

"When I pointed the balloon straight back, all the force went into moving the car forward, so it went the farthest (12 feet). When I angled it up, some force went into pushing the car down instead of forward, so it only went 8 feet. Direction matters because only the force pointing in the direction you want to move contributes to acceleration in that direction."

Q7 (Gravity - 5th grade):

"Gravity pulled the car straight down toward the center of the Earth. This kept the wheels on the ground, which created friction. Without gravity, the car would float away! Gravity helps because we need friction to 'grip' the floor, but it also hurts because friction slows us down."

Discussion Prompts for Deeper Learning

  • "What would happen in space?" — No gravity = no friction from wheels. Car would float. Would the balloon still work? (YES! Rockets work in space using the same principle.)
  • "How could you make it go BACKWARDS?" — Point balloon forward! Air pushes out front, car goes back.
  • "Is this how real cars work?" — No, real cars use engine to turn wheels (different force source), but friction and gravity still apply!
  • "What if you used a bigger balloon?" — More air = more force = greater acceleration = farther distance (until friction/air resistance balance it out).

Troubleshooting Guide

Problem Likely Cause Fix
Car doesn't move Wheels don't spin, too much friction Loosen axles, use smoother wheels, reduce car weight
Car flips backward Balloon angled too far up Point balloon more horizontally, add weight to front
Car veers to one side Unbalanced weight or one wheel dragging Check all wheels spin freely, balance weight distribution
Balloon detaches Not enough tape Wrap tape around balloon neck AND straw multiple times
Car goes <1 foot Too heavy, too much friction, or small balloon Use bigger balloon, lighter materials, smoother surface
