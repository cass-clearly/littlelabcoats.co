🌱 What Do Plants Need? 🌱

Life Science — Plants & Ecosystems — Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Life Science — Plants & Ecosystems 📋 Unit: Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
☀️ 💧 🌱
What happens if a plant gets sun but no water? Water but no sun? Let's find out!
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-LS2-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to determine if plants need sunlight and water to grow.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will design a simple investigation to test whether plants need sunlight and water, and predict which conditions will produce the healthiest plant.

💡 The Big Idea

Plants need sunlight, water, air, and soil to grow — and we can test which ones are strictly essential for a seedling! Like scientists, we'll set up an experiment with three cups and observe what happens when we change just one thing at a time.

💡 Key vocabulary:
germinate sunlight photosynthesis water soil nutrients transpiration

🔬 The Science

Plants make their own food using a process called photosynthesis — they use sunlight and water to turn air into sugar for energy. Without sunlight, a plant can't make food and will weaken. Without water, it can't run any of its processes and will dry out.

Seeds are interesting — they can actually germinate (sprout) without light because they have stored energy inside. But once that energy runs out, a seedling without sunlight will grow pale, stretched, and weak as it desperately searches for light.

Plants also release water vapor through tiny pores called stomata — that process is called transpiration. You can actually see this water with the plastic bag demo!

📦 Materials

🏠 Gather These Items

  • Bean seeds or lentils (from the pantry — either works!)
  • 3 clear plastic cups
  • Potting soil
  • Water
  • Sunny windowsill
  • Dark closet or cardboard box
  • Plastic zip-top bag
  • Wet paper towel (for bonus demo)
  • Permanent marker (to label the cups)
  • Ruler or craft stick (optional, for measuring growth)

🧪 Let's Set Up Our Experiment!

We're going to grow three plants under different conditions. We'll change one thing at a time so we can see exactly what makes the difference.

☀️💧
Cup 1 — Control
✅ Soil ✅ Water ✅ Sunlight Full conditions — our "normal" plant
🌑💧
Cup 2 — No Sunlight
✅ Soil ✅ Water ❌ No sunlight Lives in a dark closet
☀️🏜️
Cup 3 — No Water
✅ Soil ❌ No water (dry!) ✅ Sunlight Sits in the sun but stays dry
Step 1 — Set Up the Cups (10 min)
Label three clear cups: "Cup 1 — Control," "Cup 2 — No Sun," "Cup 3 — No Water." Fill each cup about ¾ full with potting soil. Press 2–3 seeds or lentils about a finger-depth into the soil of each cup.
Step 2 — Water Cups 1 and 2 (2 min)
Give Cup 1 and Cup 2 a good drink of water until the soil is moist but not soaking. Do not water Cup 3. Place Cup 1 on the sunny windowsill. Put Cup 2 in a dark closet or cover it with a box. Put Cup 3 on the sunny windowsill next to Cup 1.
Step 3 — Make Your Predictions (5 min)
Before anything grows, fill out the Student Worksheet prediction boxes on the last pages. Draw what you think each cup will look like in 7 days. Circle YES or NO for "Will it grow?" Discuss with each other — it's OK to disagree!
Step 4 — Daily Observations (5–7 days, 5 min/day)
Each day, check the cups at the same time. Record observations in the 7-day chart on your worksheet:
  • Is there a sprout? How tall is it?
  • What color are the leaves (if any)?
  • Does the plant look healthy, stretched, or droopy?
  • Cup 2: Is it reaching toward the light? Which direction?
Remember: Keep watering Cup 1 and Cup 2 every 2–3 days. Do NOT water Cup 3. You do not need dramatic changes on day one—small differences over several days are enough.
Step 5 — Bonus Transpiration Demo ⭐ (5 min)
Put a wet paper towel inside a clear plastic zip bag. Seal it and tape it to a sunny window. Check back in 1–2 hours. What do you see on the inside of the bag?
Even better: seal a small plant or a few spinach leaves in a clear bag in sunlight for 2 hours. Water droplets will form on the inside — that's transpiration!

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1:
"Cup 2 has water and soil but no sunlight. What do you predict will happen, and why?"

Hint: Seeds have stored energy inside — but what happens once that energy is used up? What does a plant need to make more food?

🗣️ Question 2:
"Why do farmers plant in the spring instead of winter?"

Hint: Think about how much sunlight there is in spring vs. winter. What about the temperature of the soil?

🚀 Extensions

  • 🌀 Celery Color Experiment: Put a stalk of celery in colored water. Watch the color travel up the stem over 24 hours — that's water moving through the plant!
  • 🔦 Phototropism Test: After Cup 2 sprouts, open the closet door just a crack so a thin beam of light gets in. Which direction does the plant lean? Draw it!
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "A Seed Is Sleepy" by Dianna Aston or "From Seed to Plant" by Gail Gibbons
  • 📊 Graph It: After the experiment, make a bar graph comparing the heights of the three plants at Day 7.
  • 🌿 Start a Garden Journal: Keep a notebook just for plant observations — press leaves, sketch flowers, track the weather alongside plant growth.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Lentils vs. beans: Lentils from the pantry germinate in just 3–4 days — faster than beans (5–7 days). Either works great for this experiment.
  • Use clear cups if possible: Kids can watch roots develop along the sides — a genuine wow moment that reinforces what soil and water do.
  • Don't throw away the cups! This experiment runs across multiple lessons in Unit 1. The cups will be referenced again as we discuss pollinators and ecosystems.
  • The transpiration demo: Seal a few spinach leaves or a small herb in a clear bag in sunlight for 2 hours. Water droplets appear on the inside. Kids are always amazed — plants sweating!
  • Cup 2 note: The dark-closet plant will likely sprout (seeds germinate without light), but it will be pale yellow-green, stretched, and leggy. This is called "etiolation" — you don't need that word, but it's a spectacular visual demonstration.
  • Cup 3 note: The dry cup may not sprout at all, or sprout then quickly shrivel. Either result tells the story.
  • Assessment: Can the student predict before the experiment begins which cup will grow best? Can they explain why using the words sunlight and photosynthesis? That's the learning target.

🔑 PARENT/TEACHER ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

🔮 Predictions — What to Expect

  • ☀️💧 Cup 1 (Control — soil + water + sunlight): This plant will grow the best! Strong green shoots, healthy leaves. This is what a happy bean or lentil looks like.
  • 🌑💧 Cup 2 (No sunlight): The seed will likely sprout (seeds contain stored energy and can germinate in the dark), but the seedling will be pale yellow or white, stretched very tall and thin, and leaning desperately toward any light source. It will not thrive. This is called etiolation.
  • ☀️🏜️ Cup 3 (No water): This seed may not sprout at all, or may sprout briefly and then shrivel and die. Dry soil cannot support germination or growth.

💬 Discussion Question Answers

Question 1: "Cup 2 has water and soil but no sunlight. What do you predict will happen, and why?"

Suggested answer: The plant might sprout because seeds have stored energy inside that lets them germinate without light. But it will quickly become pale, stretched, and unhealthy because without sunlight, it can't make food through photosynthesis. It will grow tall and spindly as it reaches desperately toward any light source.
Question 2: "Why do farmers plant in the spring instead of winter?"

Suggested answer: In spring there's more sunlight (longer days) and warmer temperatures — two things plants need to grow. Winter has short, dark days and cold soil that slows or stops growth. Farmers time their planting to match what plants need!

✍️ Science Sentence — Accepted Answers

Plants need sunlight, water, and soil to grow. (Also accept: air, nutrients)

Note: The key takeaway is sunlight and water — those are the two conditions being tested. Soil is also correct and expected. Encourage kids who write "air" or "nutrients" too!

Sample observation sentence: "Cup 1 grew best because it had sunlight and water. Cup 2 sprouted but looked pale, and Cup 3 did not grow well because it had no water."

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student predicts Cup 1 will grow the best before the experiment begins.
  • Student can explain why sunlight matters: "Plants need sunlight to make food (photosynthesis)."
  • Student observes differences in the cups over 7 days, records them on the chart, and uses those observations as evidence.
  • Student can say in their own words: "Plants need sunlight, water, and soil to grow."

🌟 Bonus: Transpiration Demo Answer

Water droplets form on the inside of the sealed bag. This shows that plants release water vapor through a process called transpiration. The water comes out through tiny pores (stomata) in the leaves and condenses on the cool bag surface. Fun way to describe it: plants sweat!

📖 Vocab Quick Reference

  • Germinate: When a seed starts to sprout and grow into a plant.
  • Sunlight: Energy from the sun that plants use to make food.
  • Photosynthesis: The process plants use to turn sunlight + water + air into food (sugar).
  • Water: Plants absorb water through their roots; it carries nutrients and is essential for growth.
  • Soil: The ground plants grow in; it holds roots in place and provides nutrients.
  • Nutrients: Minerals in soil that plants absorb to stay healthy.
  • Transpiration: When plants release water vapor through tiny pores in their leaves.
