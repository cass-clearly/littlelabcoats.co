🍞 Bread Mold Terrarium

Decomposition, fungi, and experimental design in one surprisingly compelling gross-out lab.

Students build a sealed terrarium, choose a variable to test, track visible changes over time, and argue from evidence about what mold needs to grow.

⏱️ 45–60 min setup + multi-day observations 🧪 Challenge level: medium-high 🏠 Materials: mostly household

Mission

Investigate which condition helps mold grow best — and support your answer with actual evidence instead of guesses.

Question ideaDoes moisture, light, or bread type change growth speed?
Real science moveKeep everything else the same so your comparison is fair.
What kids trackSpots, color change, fuzz, smell warnings, and how fast changes appear.
Big payoffThis turns a “gross” phenomenon into real evidence-based reasoning.
Essential question: What conditions help bread mold grow fastest, and how can we prove it using a fair test?

Phenomenon launch

Sometimes one forgotten lunch gets moldy fast while another similar food lasts longer. Why? Students already know mold can appear on food — this lesson pushes them past “because it went bad” into fungal growth conditions, decomposition, and testable explanations.

Science fit: This lesson supports Grade 5 thinking around organisms, matter cycling, and using evidence from investigations. It also gives strong practice with variables, patterns, and claim-evidence-reasoning.
Students will design a simple comparison test, observe mold growth over several days, and explain which condition most supported fungal growth using specific evidence from their setup.

What you need

Materials

  • 2–4 slices of bread
  • 2–4 sealable plastic bags or clear containers with lids
  • Water spray bottle or spoon
  • Masking tape + marker for labels
  • Notebook or printed data chart
  • Optional: different bread types, dark cabinet, fridge comparison

Best variable choices

  • Moisture: dry bread vs damp bread
  • Light: sunny spot vs dark space
  • Temperature: warmer room vs cooler spot
  • Bread type: white vs whole wheat
Important safety rule: Once bags are sealed, do not open them again. Mold spreads by spores, and breathing those in is not part of the science. Observe through the plastic, then throw the sealed setup away when the experiment is complete.

Design the investigation

Challenge upgrade: Instead of just “watch mold happen,” have students choose one variable to test and identify their control setup. That makes this feel much more like real Grade 5 science.

Write your test question

Use one of these stems:

  • How does ______ affect mold growth on bread?
  • Will bread grow mold faster when ______?
  • Which condition produces visible mold first: ______ or ______?

Fair-test reminders

  • Change only one variable.
  • Use similar bread pieces.
  • Seal every setup the same way.
  • Label clearly before you start.
  • Observe at about the same time each day.

Procedure

Step 1: Label each bag with the condition you are testing. Example: dry and damp.
Step 2: Place one slice of bread in each bag. If you are testing moisture, lightly mist or drip water onto only the bread in the “damp” bag.
Step 3: Seal each bag completely. Put the bags in their planned locations.
Step 4: Record Day 0 observations right away. Bread may look the same now — that matters too.
Step 5: Observe once each day for at least 5–7 days. Look for spots, fuzz, color change, moisture, or smell warnings without opening the bag.
Step 6: At the end, compare your setups and decide which condition helped mold grow more or faster.
Teacher move: If students are impatient, have them sketch what they expect each setup to look like by Day 3 and Day 6. Prediction plus observation gives them more to compare later.

Make the science thinking visible

ClaimWhich condition helped mold grow best in your test?
EvidenceUse 2–3 specific observations. Mention the day, what you saw, and how the two setups differed.
ReasoningExplain why that evidence supports your claim. Connect it to what fungi need to grow and how decomposers break down dead material.
Reflection prompts:
  • What was your control?
  • What stayed the same in both setups?
  • Was there any observation that surprised you?
  • If you ran this again, what would you improve?

Why this matters

Mold is not just “gross food.” It is a fungus and a decomposer. Decomposers break down dead material and recycle nutrients so matter keeps moving through ecosystems. This lesson helps students see that even something ordinary like stale bread can reveal a lot about how living systems work.

Extension ideas

Math connection

Create a simple mold growth rating scale from 0–5 and graph the daily change for each bag.

Writing connection

Write a short lab report explaining whether your evidence was strong enough to answer the question confidently.

Redesign challenge

If results were unclear, plan a better second trial with tighter controls or a more dramatic variable.

Compare organisms

Talk about how fungi differ from plants and animals — especially in how they get energy and what role they play in ecosystems.

Parent/teacher note: This lesson gets stronger when students do not rush to the “right answer.” Let them argue a little. If their evidence is thin, ask, “What did you actually observe that proves that?” That one question upgrades the whole experience.
