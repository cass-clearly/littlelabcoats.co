What Do Plants Need to Grow?

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will identify the main inputs plants need for growth and distinguish materials from conditions.

💡 The Big Idea

Plants need air and water as material inputs for growth, while sunlight provides energy and soil helps with support and minerals.

🔬 The Science

Science Snapshot
Plants depend on several environmental inputs, and each one serves a different job.

What is happening?

Plants need light, water, air, and access to minerals, plus enough space and the right temperature to stay healthy. They do not eat food the way animals do; instead, they make sugars using light energy and materials from their environment.

Why it works that way

This matters because “plants need soil” is only partly true. Soil often helps by holding water, anchoring roots, and supplying minerals, but a plant’s matter mostly comes from water and carbon dioxide in the air, not from the soil itself.

Look for this in the lesson

Students should separate plant needs into what gives support, what gives materials, and what provides energy. That helps prevent the common misconception that plants mainly “eat dirt.”

Ask while you read: Which plant needs supply energy, which supply matter, and which provide support?

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Brainstorm plant needs
List what students think plants need.
Step 2 — Sort materials vs conditions
Separate things plants use as matter from things that help growth happen.
Step 3 — Discuss common misconceptions
Talk about why soil is important but not the main source of plant body matter.
Step 4 — Build a flow sketch
Show air and water going into the plant.
Step 5 — Write the first claim
State where plant growth materials chiefly come from.

💬 Discussion Questions

Why is sunlight different from water or air?Sunlight provides energy, not plant matter.
Why is soil important if it is not the chief source?It supports the plant and provides minerals.
What does chiefly mean?Mostly or mainly.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Look at a houseplant and label which inputs come from the air, from water, and from light.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: This lesson is about conceptual sorting. Keep the language precise: chiefly from air and water.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Sample claim

Plants get the materials they need for growth chiefly from air and water because those inputs become part of the plant. Sunlight gives energy, and soil mainly gives support, water access, and minerals.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student sorts air and water as material inputs.
  • Student explains that sunlight provides energy rather than plant matter.
  • Student gives soil a support/mineral role without naming it as the chief source.
