🏎️ Ramp Racers 🏎️

Unit 1, Lesson 2 — Forces & Motion: How Far Will It Go?

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Forces & Motion
🚗📐📚

Higher ramp = more speed = farther roll! Let's find out!

📋 NGSS Standard:
K-PS2-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to compare the effects of different strengths or different directions of pushes and pulls on the motion of an object.

🎯 The Big Idea

When you let a toy car roll down a ramp, gravity pulls it down. A higher ramp means gravity pulls the car for longer, so it goes faster and rolls farther! In this experiment, you'll build ramps at different heights and discover how changing height changes how far things move.

🔬 The Science

Gravity is an invisible force that pulls things down toward the ground. When a car sits at the top of a ramp, gravity pulls it downward along the slope. The higher the ramp, the longer gravity pulls on the car as it rolls down — so the car is moving faster by the time it reaches the bottom, and it rolls farther across the floor. Scientists test ideas by changing one thing (like ramp height) and seeing what happens. That's exactly what you'll do today!

👐 Push · 🧲 Pull · 💪 Force · 📐 Ramp · 📏 Height · ↔️ Distance · 🔮 Predict · 👀 Observe · 📊 Compare

🧱 Ramp Building Supplies

📚Books3–5 hardcover books

🚗Toy Cars2 small cars
(one per kid!)

📏 Measuring & Recording

  • 👟 Shoes — kids will measure distance in shoe-lengths (no ruler needed!)
  • ✏️ Pencil & crayons — for the Student Worksheet
  • 📋 Student Worksheet (last page of this lesson)
  • 🧱 Masking tape (optional) — to mark starting line and landing spots

🏠 Nice to Have

  • A smooth, hard floor (tile, wood, or laminate works best)
  • A second piece of cardboard for a different ramp comparison
  • Small blocks or LEGO to add extra height options
💡 Non-standard units are great for K! Measuring in shoe-lengths teaches the concept of measurement without requiring kids to read a ruler. Each child's shoe is a different size — and that's a bonus conversation about why scientists use standard units!

🔬 Let's Get Started

Setup diagram — books, ramp, car at top, tape start line, clear floor ahead
Setup (5 minutes):
Clear a stretch of floor space — you need at least 8–10 feet of open, smooth floor for cars to roll across. Set out the books, cardboard, and toy cars. Don't build the ramp yet — that's the kids' job! Print the Student Worksheet (one per child, last page). Grab crayons and a pencil.
💡 Tip: Carpet slows cars down a LOT. If you only have carpet, use a long piece of cardboard on the floor as a runway too!
Step 1 — Wonder & Predict (5 min):
"If you roll a car down a ramp, what makes it go far? What makes it go only a little?"
Let them guess. Then ask:
"What do you think will happen if we make the ramp really tall? Let's test YOUR prediction!"
Have each child circle their prediction on the Student Worksheet — will a tall ramp or a short ramp make the car go farther?
Step 2 — Build Ramp #1: LOW (5 min):
Together, stack 1 book and lean the cardboard against it to make a LOW ramp.
Place a piece of tape at the bottom of the ramp as the starting line. 🏁
Step 3 — Race #1: LOW Ramp (5 min):
⚠️ Important — don't push the car! Place it at the top and let go. We want gravity to pull the car down, not our hands. If kids push, the experiment isn't fair — we're testing the ramp, not arm strength!
  • Each child places their car at the top of the ramp.
  • Let go — don't push! (We want gravity to pull the car down.)
  • Watch where the car stops.
  • Walk shoe-to-shoe from the starting line to the car. Count how many shoe-lengths it rolled! 👟
  • Record the shoe-lengths on the Student Worksheet. 📝
  • Do it two times to make sure (scientists always repeat).
Step 4 — Build Ramp #2: MEDIUM (3 min):
"What do you think will happen if we make the ramp higher? Will the car go farther or not as far? Let's test YOUR prediction!"
Stack 3 books. Lean the same cardboard against the taller stack. Same starting line! 🏁
Step 5 — Race #2: MEDIUM Ramp (5 min):
Same as before — let go from the top (don't push!), measure in shoes, record. Do it two times.
Step 6 — Build Ramp #3: HIGH (3 min):
Stack 5 books (or as high as you can go!). Lean the cardboard against it. 🏔️
Step 7 — Race #3: HIGH Ramp (5 min):
Let go, measure in shoes, record. Do it two times. Did the car go farther this time? 👀
Step 8 — Look at Your Data (5 min):
Help the kids compare their results.
"Which ramp made the car go the farthest? Which one made it go the shortest distance?"
Color in the bar graph on the Student Worksheet together. 🎨
Step 9 — Wrap-Up & Big Idea (5 min):
"Gravity pulls the car down the ramp. The higher the ramp, the longer gravity pulls, so the car goes faster and rolls farther. That's what scientists mean when they say forces can have different strengths!"

💬 Discussion Questions

Core Questions (Kindergarten)
Which ramp made your car go the farthest? 🏆

Look for: "The high ramp" or "the one with five books." Any phrasing that identifies the tallest ramp is correct.

Which ramp made your car go the shortest distance?

Look for: "The low ramp" or "the one with one book."

What happens when the ramp gets higher? Does the car go farther or shorter?

Look for: "It goes farther!" Help them connect: higher ramp → car goes farther.

Did your car go the same distance both times? Why might it be a little different?

They might say: "It went a little different." That's great — talk about how even scientists get slightly different results each time.

What was pulling the car down the ramp? (Hint: you didn't push it!)

Look for: "Gravity!" If they say "the ramp," guide them: "The ramp gives gravity a path — gravity is the force that pulls the car down."

What would happen if we made the ramp even higher? Like a mountain? 🏔️

Look for: "It would go really far!" or "Super fast!" Accept enthusiastic predictions.

Challenge Questions (For Older Siblings!)
What would happen if we used a heavier car? A lighter one?

They might say heavier goes farther (momentum), or lighter goes farther (less to move). Both show good thinking — test it!

What if the ramp was bumpy instead of smooth? How would that change things?

Look for: "The car would go slower" or "not as far." Introduce the word friction.

Can you think of real-life ramps? (Slides, hills, skateboard ramps, wheelchair ramps)

Accept any real-world example. Great ones: playground slides, hills, driveways, parking garages.

What other forces might slow the car down? (Hint: rub your hands together — feel that?)

Look for: "Friction!" or "rubbing." The floor and the car's wheels create friction that slows it down.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🧸 Different Objects: Roll a ball, a marker, a crayon, and a LEGO brick down the ramp. Which rolls farthest? Why?
  • 🧱 Surface Test: Try rolling on tile, carpet, towel, and sandpaper. Which surface lets the car go farthest?
  • 🎯 Target Game: Put a cup or box at a certain distance. Can you pick the right ramp height to make the car stop right at the target?
  • 🏗️ Ramp Design Challenge: Build the longest possible ramp using only household items. How far can you make a car go?
  • 📖 Read Aloud: Pair with Motion: Push and Pull, Fast and Slow by Darlene Stille or Forces Make Things Move by Kimberly Bradley

👩‍🏫 Parent Notes

  • Mess level: Low. This one is pretty clean. Just watch for cars flying off tables.
  • Floor surface matters: Smooth, hard floors give the best results. Carpet adds friction and makes differences between ramp heights less obvious.
  • Floor space: The high ramp can send cars 8 feet or more. Clear at least 8–10 feet of open floor.
  • Measuring in shoes: Kindergartners measure in shoe-lengths — it's a non-standard unit, and that's perfect for K. Each child's shoe is different, which is a great conversation about why scientists use standard units later on.
  • Why two trials: This teaches the concept of repeated testing. If the car lands in a different spot each time, it's a great chance to talk about why scientists test things more than once.
  • If you don't have books: Use shoe boxes, couch cushions, canned goods — anything to create height. Creativity counts.
  • Sibling fairness: Give each kid their own car and let them take turns. They can compare whose car went farther — and discuss why (different car weight, wheel friction, etc.).

🔑 Expected Results & Discussion Guide

📊 Expected Data Patterns

  • LOW ramp (1 book): Car rolls the shortest distance — typically 1–3 shoe-lengths on a smooth floor.
  • MEDIUM ramp (3 books): Car rolls a moderate distance — typically 3–6 shoe-lengths.
  • HIGH ramp (5 books): Car rolls the farthest distance — typically 6–10 shoe-lengths (depending on floor surface, car weight, and shoe size).

Key pattern: Higher ramp = farther distance. Every time. If a trial doesn't match this pattern, discuss why (car went crooked, bumped into something, different release point, etc.).

💬 Discussion Question Guidance

Q — Which ramp made the car go farthest?
Answer: The HIGH ramp (5 books). Accept any phrasing — "the tall one," "the big one," "the one with lots of books."

Q — Why does a higher ramp make the car go farther?
Kindergarten-level answer: "Gravity pulls the car down. The higher the ramp, the longer gravity pulls, so the car goes faster and rolls farther."
Deeper (for curious kids): A higher ramp means the car falls a greater height, converting more potential energy into speed. More speed = more distance before friction stops it.

Q — Was your prediction right?
No wrong answer here. If they predicted wrong, celebrate that — "Scientists learn the most when their predictions are wrong!"

⚠️ Common Issues & Fixes

  • Car goes crooked: Add a "lane" using two rulers or books along the edges of the ramp.
  • Results seem random: Make sure kids are releasing from the same spot each time, and NOT pushing. Mark the release point with tape.
  • Car barely moves on low ramp: Normal! If the ramp is too flat, the car won't move much. That's good data!
  • Results vary between tries: This is GREAT — it naturally teaches why scientists repeat experiments!

🧠 The Science (For Your Reference)

This experiment demonstrates K-PS2-1: forces (pushes and pulls) can have different strengths. Gravity pulls the car down the ramp. A higher ramp means gravity pulls the car over a greater distance, converting more gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy — so the car moves faster at the bottom and rolls farther. For kindergartners, "higher ramp = gravity pulls longer = car goes faster and farther" is perfectly appropriate.
