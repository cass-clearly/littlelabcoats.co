Seeds, Fruits, and New Plants

Grade 4 · Unit 2
A plant does not finish the reproduction job when a flower is pollinated. Seeds must be protected, and many plants benefit when seeds spread away from the parent plant.
Key vocabulary
seedfruitoffspringprotection
What to notice
  • Show a few seed and fruit examples and ask what clues suggest these structures are helping the next generation.
  • Explain that seeds protect the young plant, while fruits often help move seeds to new places.
  • Compare examples that use animals, wind, or other methods to spread offspring.
Materials snapshot
  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • seeds, fruit, or seed photos
  • paper and pencil
  • optional: apple, bean, berry, or dandelion examples
Talk-about prompts
  • Why is seed protection important?
  • Why might a plant benefit if seeds spread away from the parent plant?
  • How can a fruit help reproduction?
Why it matters

Seeds contain the beginning of a new plant and a protective outer covering. Fruits often surround seeds and can help with dispersal when animals eat them, carry them, or when the fruit helps the seed travel another way. A seed’s structure and a fruit’s structure both support the success of the next generation of plants.

Try this next: Cut open a fruit and inspect where the seeds are stored. Then compare that example to a dry seed or a wind-dispersed seed picture and discuss how each structure helps the next generation.
