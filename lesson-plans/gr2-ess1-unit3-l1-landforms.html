🏔️ Landforms and Water in an Area

Earth Science Unit 3, Lesson 1 of 6

Grade: 2 Duration: 45 minutes Subject: Earth Science, Earth Features Materials: Playdough, blue paper, toothpicks, crayons
NGSS Standard: 2-ESS2-2 — Develop a model to represent the shapes and kinds of land and bodies of water in an area.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will develop a model that shows different landforms and bodies of water in one area, then use that model to describe the shapes and kinds of features they included.

💡 The Big Idea

Landforms and bodies of water have different shapes and kinds. We can build a model of one area to show where land is, where water is, and what each feature is like.

📦 Materials

  • Playdough (store-bought, OR homemade: 2 cups flour, 1/2 cup salt, 3/4 cup water, 1 tbsp oil... mix until smooth)
  • Blue construction paper or a bowl of water (to represent the ocean/lake)
  • Small paper scraps and toothpicks or pencil tips for "flag" labels
  • Crayons or markers

📖 Vocabulary

landform A natural shape on Earth's surface, like a mountain or valley
mountain A very tall, steep rise of land with a peak at the top
valley A low area of land between hills or mountains
plain A wide, flat area of land
island A piece of land completely surrounded by water
peninsula A piece of land that sticks out into water, connected on one side

🔬 The Science

Science Snapshot
A model can show the different shapes of land and where water is found around them.

What is a landform?

A landform is a natural shape on Earth’s surface. Mountains, valleys, plains, and islands all have different shapes that we can see, compare, and model.

Why model it?

Models help us show a shape that is too big to bring into the classroom. A playdough model can show tall, flat, low, or surrounded-by-water shapes.

How this fits NGSS

This lesson is about building a model of one area with land and water, then using that model to describe both the shapes and the kinds of Earth features in that area.

🧪 Activity: Build a Landform Area Model

Step 1: Look at pictures of real places and name the shapes you notice. Ask: Which landform looks tall? Which looks flat? Which is surrounded by water?
Step 2: Set up blue paper as your water area and playdough as your land. Tell students they are building one area model, not four separate random shapes.
Step 3: Build a mountain and a plain in the same area. Compare the shapes out loud: the mountain is tall and steep, while the plain is wide and flat.
Step 4: Add a valley between raised land or next to a hill. Have students press down the playdough to make a lower area.
Step 5: Add an island in the blue water area. Ask students to check: Is water on every side?
Step 6: Label each feature with a paper flag or place written labels beside the model.
Step 7: Use the model to describe the area: Where is the water? Which landform is highest? Which landform is lowest? Which one is surrounded by water?
Step 8: Draw a quick map of your finished model and label the land and water features. This helps students connect a 3D model to a simple 2D representation of an area.

💬 Discussion Questions

Q1: Which landform in your model is tallest, and how can you tell?

Hint: Students should use shape words like tall, steep, low, flat, or surrounded by water.

Q2: How did your model show the difference between land and water, and where each one was located?

Hint: Students should point to the blue paper or water area and explain which features were landforms.

Q3: Why is an island different from the other landforms you built?

Hint: An island is land all the way around by water, so its location matters as much as its shape.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Homemade playdough recipe: 2 cups flour, 1/2 cup salt, 3/4 cup water, 1 tbsp oil. Add food coloring if desired. It lasts about a week in an airtight bag.
  • No playdough? Use rolled-up paper or pillows to model landforms conceptually.
  • Mastery check: Students should be able to use a model to describe the shapes and kinds of land and water in one area, not just name isolated vocabulary words.
  • Preview: Connect to later lessons by asking how water might move around the landforms in the model.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY

Worksheet Answers

  1. Draw your area model: Accept any model that clearly shows multiple landforms and a body of water in one area, with labels that match the shapes.
  2. Match the shape word: mountain, valley, plain, island
  3. Circle the best answer: (b) island
  4. Use evidence from your model: Accept reasonable answers that match the student's own model, including identifying which feature represented water.
