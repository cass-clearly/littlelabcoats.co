Reading Landform Maps and Models

Grade 4 Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 4 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface
🗺️⛰️🔍
Maps and models help scientists compare landforms and infer how Earth’s surface may have changed.
📋 NGSS Standard:
4-ESS2-1 — Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation. / 4-ESS2-2 — Analyze and interpret data from maps to describe patterns of Earth’s features.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will use maps and models as evidence to compare landforms and infer how Earth processes shaped them.

💡 The Big Idea

Scientists use maps, cross-sections, and models to make large Earth-surface patterns easier to study and explain.

🔬 The Science

A model is a simplified version of a real system. A map is a visual representation of a place. Both can show patterns that are hard to notice otherwise.

Grade 4 students should practice using map or model evidence to support claims about where water flows, where sediment may collect, or where erosion may be strongest.

This is not just reading a picture. It is using a representation as evidence in an explanation.

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Study a landform map (10 min)
Look for high places, low places, valleys, river paths, and possible places where water collects.
Step 2 — Compare map and model (10 min)
Use a physical model or drawing to show how the map represents shape from above.
Step 3 — Make evidence-based inferences (10 min)
Students use the map to explain where erosion or deposition might happen.
Step 4 — Label patterns (10 min)
Mark likely slopes, flatter places, and paths water might follow.
Step 5 — Defend one claim (10 min)
Students write a claim about the landscape and support it with at least two pieces of evidence from the representation.

💬 Discussion Questions

Why do scientists use maps and models?They make big systems easier to study and explain.
What can a map show that a photo might not show clearly?Patterns of location, slope, pathways, and relationships across the whole area.
How can a model help explain erosion?It can show where water might move faster on a slope and where material may collect.
What counts as evidence from a map?Features you can point to on the map, such as river paths, lower areas, or steep slopes.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Build a quick hill-and-valley model from clay or towels and compare the model to a simple overhead sketch.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Encourage students to point to exact map features instead of making vague claims.

Misconception to catch: A model is not supposed to copy every detail. It is meant to highlight important relationships.
