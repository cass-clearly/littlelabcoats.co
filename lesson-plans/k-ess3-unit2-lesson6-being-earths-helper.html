🌱 Being Earth's Helper 🌱

Earth Science — Earth & Human Activity | Unit 2, Lesson 6 of 6

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🌍 Subject: Earth Science — Earth & Human Activity 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🌍 💚 🌱 ✨
Every small action adds up — and Earth needs helpers like you!
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-ESS3-3 — Communicate solutions that will reduce the impact of humans on the land, water, air, and/or other living things in the local environment.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will explore how to help as being Earth's Helper, identify useful actions, and explain why those actions matter.

🎯 The Big Idea

We've learned a lot this unit — natural resources, water conservation, soil, how humans change the Earth, and weather safety. Now it's time to put it all together and take action! Even a kindergartner can make real choices that help the environment. The key is being specific: not "save the environment" (too vague) but "I will turn off the tap while brushing my teeth, saving 8 gallons per day" (specific and measurable!).

🔬 The Science

Cumulative impact means that many small actions add up to big change. If a family of four each turns off the tap while brushing teeth twice a day, that's up to 32 gallons saved per day — just from one habit change. Multiply that by millions of families — and you're talking about saving billions of gallons of water per year. Individual actions matter, but they matter most when many people do them together. That's why sharing your Earth Helper pledge with your family is part of the project!

💡 Key vocabulary: solution, specific, measurable, habitat, pollution, conservation, pledge, reduce, reuse, protect

📦 Materials

🏠 For the Earth Helper Project

  • Pledge card worksheet (included)
  • Crayons, markers, or colored pencils
  • Scissors (optional — to cut out the pledge card)
  • Tape or magnet (to put the pledge card somewhere visible!)

🌍 The Four Earth Problems — Pick ONE!

🗑️ Problem 1: Litter & Trash

How it hurts Earth: Trash in parks, roads, and waterways harms animals (birds and fish can eat plastic by mistake), pollutes water, and makes communities less beautiful. Plastic doesn't break down for hundreds of years.

Example specific actions:

  • "I will pick up 10 pieces of litter every week during our walks."
  • "I will always carry a bag for trash when we go to the park."
  • "I will make sure all of my plastic bags go in a recycling bin, not the trash."

💧 Problem 2: Water Waste

How it hurts Earth: Fresh water is rare — less than 1% of Earth's water is drinkable. When we waste water, we use up a precious resource that animals, plants, and other people also need.

Example specific actions:

  • "I will turn off the tap while brushing my teeth, saving 2–4 gallons per brush."
  • "I will take 4-minute showers instead of 10-minute showers."
  • "I will save pasta water and use it to water a houseplant."

🦋 Problem 3: Habitat Loss

How it hurts Earth: When humans build on natural land, animals lose the food, shelter, and space they need to survive. Loss of habitat is the #1 reason animals become endangered.

Example specific actions:

  • "I will plant 3 native wildflowers in our yard to help bees and butterflies."
  • "I will convince my family to leave a wild patch in our yard for insects."
  • "I will put a bird feeder up and keep it filled through winter."

🏭 Problem 4: Air & Water Pollution

How it hurts Earth: Chemicals and exhaust in air and water harm human health and animal health. Many pollutants wash from land into waterways, harming fish and other aquatic life.

Example specific actions:

  • "I will tell my parents to use natural cleaning products instead of harsh chemicals."
  • "I will remind my family to never pour chemicals (paint, oil) down the drain or on the ground."
  • "When we walk to the store instead of driving, we create less car pollution."

💚 Example Earth Helper Actions

🦷
Turn off the tap while brushing
Saves ~2–4 gallons per brush → up to 8 gallons per day for one person brushing twice → ~2,920 gallons per year! Addresses: water waste.
🌸
Plant 3 native flowers for bees
Native plants need less water, provide food and habitat for local pollinators (bees, butterflies, hummingbirds). Pollinators help grow our food! Addresses: habitat loss.
🗑️
Pick up 10 pieces of litter per week
Over a year, that's 520 pieces of trash that won't reach a waterway or harm an animal. If your whole family does it: 2,080 pieces per year! Addresses: litter.
🐦
Keep a bird feeder filled through winter
Many birds struggle to find food when the ground is frozen. A feeder becomes critical habitat support for winter birds. Addresses: habitat loss.

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Review the Unit (5 min)
Quick recap: "We've learned about natural resources, water, soil, how humans change the Earth, and weather safety. What's one thing you remember?" Let each student share one thing they remember from the unit.
Step 2 — Choose Your Problem (5 min)
Each student picks ONE of the four problems: litter, water waste, habitat loss, or pollution. Help them think about which one they feel most connected to or most able to address.
Step 3 — Design Your Specific Action (10 min)
The key rule: Be SPECIFIC, not vague!
  • ❌ "Save the environment" — too vague, can't be done
  • ✅ "Turn off the tap while brushing teeth" — specific, measurable, can start today
  • ❌ "Be nice to animals" — not specific enough
  • ✅ "Plant 3 native flowers in our yard by the end of April" — specific, measurable, has a deadline
Help each student make their action specific. Ask: "When will you do it? How many times? How will you know you did it?"
Step 4 — Make Your Pledge Card (10 min)
Fill in and decorate the pledge card worksheet. This is meant to be cut out and put somewhere visible — on the refrigerator, bathroom mirror, or bedroom door. The pledge card = accountability!
Step 5 — Share Your Pledge (5 min)
Each student shares their pledge with the group (or family). Explain: Why did you pick this problem? What will you do? How does it help Earth?

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • What problem did you choose to work on? Why that one?
  • What's the difference between a vague goal ("help the Earth") and a specific action ("pick up 10 pieces of litter per week")?
  • If one person saves 8 gallons of water per day by turning off the tap, how much would 10 people save?
  • How does your action connect to the needs of animals and plants?
  • What's one thing from this unit that surprised you the most?
💡 Suggested discussion — making it specific and measurable: When we make a plan specific — "I will turn off the tap while brushing my teeth, every time, starting today" — we're much more likely to actually do it. Vague goals fade away. Specific plans stick. Scientists call this an "actionable intervention." The more specific you are, the easier it is to do the thing, check whether you did it, and celebrate when you succeed. Your pledge card is your contract with Earth!

🚀 Extensions

  • 📊 Track your pledge: Make a simple tally chart. Every day you complete your action, put a star. See how many stars you get in a month!
  • 🌱 Native plant project: Research which native plants grow in your area. Order seeds and start a native plant garden. Contact your local extension office — they often have free native plants!
  • 💌 Share your pledge: Write a letter or draw a picture for a grandparent, neighbor, or friend telling them about your Earth Helper pledge. Ask them to make one too!
  • 📚 Read aloud: "The Watcher: Jane Goodall's Life with the Chimps" by Jeanette Winter, or "Wangari's Trees of Peace" by Jeanette Winter — true stories of real Earth helpers!

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Specificity is the skill: The hardest part for kindergartners is making their action specific. Scaffold this by asking follow-up questions: "When?" "How many?" "How will you know you did it?" Don't accept "be nice to animals" — push for the specific version.
  • Post the pledge card: The physical pledge card on the fridge or bathroom mirror makes the commitment visible and real. It also prompts family conversations.
  • This is the capstone: Lesson 6 asks students to synthesize everything from the unit. If they struggle, it's fine to look back at previous lessons for inspiration.
  • Connection to K-ESS3-3: "Communicate solutions that will reduce the impact of humans on the local environment" — that IS the pledge card. Designing and sharing the specific action is the full performance expectation.

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

Evaluating Student Pledges — Look For:

  • Specificity: Does the action name a specific behavior (not just "help")? Does it say when and how often?
  • Feasibility: Can the student actually do this? Is it within their control?
  • Connection: Does the student understand HOW this action helps the environment? (Turning off tap = saves fresh water = more for animals and future people)
  • Ownership: Is this THEIR idea (even if prompted)? Or does it feel copied?

Strong vs. Weak Pledge Examples

✅ Strong Pledge ❌ Weak Pledge
"I will turn off the tap every time I brush my teeth." "I will save water."
"I will pick up 5 pieces of litter on every walk." "I will help clean up trash."
"I will plant 2 native flowers in our garden this spring." "I will be nice to animals."

✅ What Success Looks Like (Entire Unit)

  • K-ESS3-1: Student can name natural resources (water, soil, air, plants, sunlight) and connect them to what animals and humans need to survive.
  • K-ESS3-2: Student understands the purpose of weather forecasting and can explain how early warnings help families prepare for and respond to severe weather (addressed in Lesson 5).
  • K-ESS3-3: Student has designed a specific, feasible action to reduce human impact on a local natural resource and can explain why it helps.
