🌱 Grade 3 Life Science, Unit 1, Lesson 1

What Is a Life Cycle? | A gentle introduction to how living things grow, change, and make new life

In this lesson, students explore the idea that all living things go through stages. They compare plant, frog, butterfly, and human life cycles, then create their own simple life cycle model.

📋 NGSS Connection:
3-LS1-1 — Develop models to describe that organisms have unique and diverse life cycles but all have in common birth, growth, reproduction, and death.

🎯 Learning Objectives

Observe

Students will identify that plants and animals pass through stages as they grow.

Compare

Students will compare at least two life cycles and notice both similarities and differences.

Model

Students will create a simple model showing the stages of one organism’s life cycle.

Explain

Students will explain that all life cycles include birth, growth, reproduction, and death.

💡 The Big Idea

A life cycle is the pattern of stages a living thing goes through during its life. A butterfly does not stay a caterpillar forever. A seed does not stay a seed forever. A frog starts as an egg, changes into a tadpole, and later becomes an adult frog. Even humans have a life cycle. The stages look different for each organism, but all living things are born, grow, reproduce, and eventually die.

💡 Key vocabulary: life cycle, stage, organism, reproduce, model, metamorphosis

📦 Materials

  • Printed pictures of a butterfly, frog, bean plant, and human life cycle, or draw them on paper
  • Scissors
  • Glue or tape
  • Paper or notebook
  • Crayons, markers, or colored pencils
  • Optional: index cards for sorting stages

🔬 Let’s Get Started

Step 1 — Notice Living Changes (5–7 min)
Ask: “Do babies look exactly like grown-ups?” Talk about how kittens, frogs, oak trees, and people all change as they grow. Introduce the phrase life cycle and explain that it means the stages of life from beginning to end.
Step 2 — Sort the Stages (8–10 min)
Give students life cycle pictures or cards out of order. Have them put the stages in order for a butterfly, frog, or bean plant. Encourage them to explain why one stage comes before another.
  • Butterfly: egg → caterpillar → chrysalis → butterfly
  • Frog: egg → tadpole → froglet → adult frog
  • Bean plant: seed → sprout → young plant → mature plant with flowers or seed pods
Step 3 — Compare Organisms (8–10 min)
Make a quick chart with two columns: Same and Different. Compare a frog and a bean plant, or a butterfly and a human.
  • Same: both start small, both grow, both can make offspring
  • Different: frogs hatch from eggs, plants grow from seeds, butterflies change dramatically, humans do not go through metamorphosis
Step 4 — Build a Life Cycle Model (12–15 min)
Choose one organism. Students draw, cut, and arrange the stages in a circle or flow chart. Add arrows to show the cycle continues when new offspring are produced.
Step 5 — Explain the Pattern (5 min)
Have the student orally explain their model using this sentence starter: “The life cycle of a _____ begins with _____, then it _____, then it _____, and later it makes new _____.”

💬 Discussion Questions

1. Why is it called a cycle instead of just a list?

2. How is a frog’s life cycle different from a human’s?

3. What do all life cycles have in common, even when the stages look very different?

4. Why do scientists use models to study life cycles?

🧠 Teacher Notes

  • Keep the focus on big patterns: birth, growth, reproduction, death.
  • It is okay if students use simple language like “baby” and “grown-up” before you layer in more formal terms.
  • This lesson pairs well with future lessons on inherited traits, adaptations, and ecosystems.
  • If your child is fascinated by one animal, let them go deeper there. Curiosity is a feature, not a detour.

📝 Student Worksheet

Part A: Put the stages in order. Draw or label four stages for one organism.

Part B: Same or different? Write one way two organisms’ life cycles are the same and one way they are different.

Part C: Explain it. Finish the sentence: “All living things have a life cycle because...”

🌟 Extension Ideas

  • Make a spinning life cycle wheel for a butterfly or frog.
  • Read a picture book about metamorphosis and add new facts to the model.
  • Observe a real plant over several weeks and keep a growth journal.
  • Compare a fast life cycle and a slow life cycle, like fruit flies and oak trees.

Little Lab Coats note: this lesson is designed to be visual, discussion-rich, and easy to run at home with simple materials.
