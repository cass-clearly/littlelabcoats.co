🪵 Dead Log Habitat Mini Activity

Garden Detectives bonus habitat build + garden critter reference

Big idea: A garden is not only plants. It is also a living habitat. A damp old log can become a tiny home for decomposers and detritus-feeders that help break down dead material and keep the garden ecosystem healthy.
Gentle science rule: We are making a habitat, not a trap. Set it up, check in carefully, and put everything back the way you found it.

🌱 What younger vs older kids should notice

Younger kids

Habitat

A habitat is a place where living things can find what they need: shelter, moisture, food, and safety.

Older kids

Decomposers

Decomposers break down dead plants and animals into smaller parts that return to the soil system.

Older kids

Detritus

Detritus is dead organic matter like leaves, bark, roots, and old wood. Many garden critters feed on it directly or help break it apart.

🧺 Materials

Gather

What you need

  • An old log, rotting branch, or chunk of untreated wood
  • A shady or partly shady garden corner
  • Watering can or cup of water
  • Optional: dead leaves, bark, and a few stones
  • Gloves if desired
  • Notebook or the Garden Guest Log observation page
Safety

What to avoid

  • No painted or treated wood
  • No lifting very heavy logs
  • No reaching into places you cannot see
  • Avoid spiders, stinging insects, or anything that feels unsafe
  • Wash hands after the activity

🧪 Build the dead log habitat

  1. Choose the spot. Pick a quiet area with some shade where the log can stay undisturbed.
  2. Lay down a soft base. Add a little leaf litter, bark, or loose soil underneath if the ground is bare.
  3. Place the old log. Set it directly on the soil so small creatures can move in from below.
  4. Make it damp, not flooded. Splash water on and around the log so it feels moist. A wet/old log is more welcoming than a dry one.
  5. Add habitat extras. Tuck a few dead leaves or bark pieces nearby to create hiding spaces.
  6. Leave it alone. This is the important part. Habitat takes time. Come back in a few days and then again each week.
Check-in idea: Lift only one edge gently, peek underneath, notice who is there, then place the log back carefully so the habitat stays intact.

🐛 Garden critter reference card

Why these critters matter: Many of them help the garden ecosystem by breaking down dead material, mixing soil, recycling nutrients, feeding other animals, or showing that the habitat is holding moisture.

🐜 Ants + larvae

Ants

You may see worker ants moving pale larvae or eggs if a colony is nearby. Ants move soil and organic bits, but if you see larvae, look quickly and gently put the habitat back.

🪵 Woodlice

Woodlice / pill bugs

These crustaceans love damp places and help chew dead plant matter into smaller pieces.

🪱 Worms

Earthworms

Worms mix soil, help air and water move through it, and turn dead material into richer soil.

🐛 Millipedes

Millipedes

Millipedes are classic detritus-feeders. They help break down rotting leaves and wood.

🪲 Beetles

Small beetles

Some beetles hunt tiny pests; others help with decomposition. Beetles add balance to the habitat.

🐌 Slugs / snails

Slugs and snails

They often appear in damp places. They are part of the habitat too, though gardeners may need to manage them around tender plants.
