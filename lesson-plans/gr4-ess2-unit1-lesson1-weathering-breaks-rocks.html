Weathering Breaks Rocks

Grade 4 Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface | Unit 1, Lesson 1 of 6

📚 Grade: 4th Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🔬 Subject: Earth Science — Shaping Earth’s Surface
🪨🌬️💧
Wind, water, ice, and roots can slowly break rock into smaller pieces.
📋 NGSS Standard:
4-ESS2-1 — Make observations and/or measurements to provide evidence of the effects of weathering or the rate of erosion by water, ice, wind, or vegetation. / 4-ESS2-2 — Analyze and interpret data from maps to describe patterns of Earth’s features.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will explain how weathering changes rock at Earth’s surface and use evidence from simple observations to describe that change.

💡 The Big Idea

Weathering is the process of breaking rock into smaller pieces. It can happen slowly through water, ice, temperature changes, and living things.

🔬 The Science

Weathering changes Earth materials at the surface. Rocks can crack, crumble, dissolve, or break apart into sediment.

Unlike erosion, weathering does not need to move the material far away. It focuses on the breaking and changing step first.

Grade 4 students should begin naming causes and effects clearly: a change in water, temperature, or roots can cause a visible change in rock.

🧪 Investigation

Step 1 — Look closely at a rock sample (10 min)
Observe shape, texture, cracks, and edges. Record what you notice before anything changes.
Step 2 — Model rock breaking (10 min)
Gently break a cracker or dry bread piece and compare the larger and smaller pieces.
Step 3 — Connect the model to real weathering (10 min)
Discuss how roots, water, freezing, and heating can make cracks bigger over time.
Step 4 — Sort causes and effects (10 min)
Students match each weathering cause to the kind of rock change it can produce.
Step 5 — Write an evidence sentence (10 min)
Students explain one cause-and-effect example using words like because and so.

💬 Discussion Questions

How is weathering different from a rock just being moved?Weathering is about breaking or changing the rock first.
What evidence shows that a rock has weathered?Students may notice smaller pieces, cracks, rough edges, or crumbs.
How can water cause weathering?Water can enter cracks, freeze, or slowly wear minerals away.
Why does weathering matter for landforms?Weathering creates the smaller material that erosion can later move.

🚀 Extensions

Extension: Look for one example of weathering outside, such as cracked concrete, tree roots lifting a sidewalk, or rough worn stone.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching move: Keep weathering and erosion separate in student language. Today is about the breaking and changing step.

Misconception to catch: Students often think weathering only happens in dramatic storms. Remind them that slow changes over long times also count.
