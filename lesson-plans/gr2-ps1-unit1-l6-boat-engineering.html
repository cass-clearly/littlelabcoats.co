🌍 Background

Engineers solve problems using science. Today you are going to be an engineer! Your challenge: design and build a boat from aluminum foil that can hold as many pennies as possible before sinking. This uses everything you have learned about matter. Foil is a solid with specific properties -- flexible, waterproof, and shapeable. Water creates an upward force on floating objects (called buoyancy). The shape of your boat determines how much weight it can support before water comes over the sides.

What makes a foil boat hold more pennies? Three things matter: width (a wider boat spreads the weight over more water), depth (taller walls keep water from spilling over the sides as pennies are added), and leak prevention (even a tiny gap or hole lets water in, and the boat will slowly sink). Good engineers think about all three before they start building.

This lesson is your chance to be a real engineer. You have three sheets of foil, so you have room to try multiple designs or iterate on one great idea. Keep track of how many pennies each design holds and what you changed between versions. Engineers actually learn more from failure than from getting it right the first time. If your boat sinks on the first test, that is not a setback -- that is data! It tells you exactly what to fix on the next try.

🏆 The Challenge

Goal: Build a foil boat that holds as many pennies as possible before sinking.

Rules: You may use only aluminum foil (no tape, no other materials). You must do at least TWO design iterations (try, improve, try again).

Success: Your best penny count after improving your design!

🔬 Procedure: Design, Build, Test, Improve!

Step 1 -- DESIGN (5 minutes): Before touching the foil, sketch your boat design on paper. Think about shape, wall height, and base size. Consider: wider or narrower? Tall walls or short walls? Flat bottom or pointed bow? Label your sketch.
Step 2 -- BUILD (10 minutes): Use your first sheet of foil to build your design. Take time folding the edges up to create walls. Make sure there are no gaps or holes at the corners -- those are where water sneaks in.
Step 3 -- TEST 1: Gently place your boat on the water. If it floats, start adding pennies one by one. Place them carefully in the center of the boat first. Count how many pennies it holds before sinking!
Step 4 -- RECORD: Write down your Test 1 penny count. Make a quick sketch of your boat design and note its shape, wall height, and any issues you noticed.
Step 5 -- ANALYZE: Why did your boat sink when it did? Were the walls too short (water came over the top)? Did it tip sideways (unstable, base too narrow)? Did it spring a leak at a corner? Each answer tells you what to fix.
Step 6 -- IMPROVE (5 minutes): Based on what you observed, plan one or two specific changes to your design. Write them down. What are you changing and why?
Step 7 -- BUILD 2: Use your second sheet of foil to build the improved design. Apply your analysis from Step 5 to make the changes you planned.
Step 8 -- TEST 2: Float your improved boat and load pennies. Record your Test 2 penny count. Compare to Test 1 -- did the improvement help?
Step 9 (Bonus): If you have a third sheet of foil and time, make a third iteration. What is your personal best penny record?
💡 Penny loading tip: Place pennies in the very center of the boat first, then work outward evenly. This keeps the boat balanced and prevents it from tipping to one side.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: What design features helped hold the most pennies? Why does a wider or deeper boat help?

Suggested answer: Width spreads the weight over more water surface, which increases buoyancy. Deeper (taller) walls keep water from splashing over the sides as pennies are added. A flat bottom also distributes the penny weight more evenly. The combination of wide base plus tall walls is usually the winning approach!

🗣️ Question 2: In Lesson 3, you learned that flat foil floats but a crumpled ball sinks. How did you use that information in your boat design?

Suggested answer: The flat sheet works because it has lots of surface area in contact with water and traps air underneath. The boat should be flat on the bottom (large area) and have walls to create an air pocket inside. Keeping the foil as flat and even as possible, not crumpled or leaky, applies the same principle that makes a flat foil sheet float.

🗣️ Question 3: If your first boat did not work well, how did you decide what to improve?

Suggested answer: You watch carefully when it fails -- that is the key engineering insight. Did the boat tip sideways? Then make the base wider for stability. Did water spill over the walls? Then make the walls taller. Did it spring a leak? Then fold the corners more carefully. Each failure tells you exactly what to fix. A failure is not a mistake -- it is information!

🗣️ Question 4: Could you build an even better boat if you used a different material? What properties would that material need?

Suggested answer: The material would need to be waterproof (water cannot pass through it), flexible enough to be shaped into a boat, and strong enough not to tear. Foil works because it is waterproof and very flexible, but it is thin and can tear at corners. A thicker waterproof material (like a plastic bag or heavy plastic sheet) might hold even more pennies. Engineers always ask "is this the best material for the job?"

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • This is the unit culminating engineering challenge. Encourage at least two full design-test-improve cycles. The iteration is the lesson -- not the penny count itself.
  • Penny loading tip: Place pennies in the very center of the boat first, then work outward. This keeps the boat balanced. Dropping pennies from height or at an angle will cause splashing and premature sinking -- add them gently.
  • Common mistakes to watch for and discuss: Gaps in foil corners (water seeps in), walls that are too low (water washes over), boat that is too narrow (tips sideways). Help students identify these through questioning -- do not just tell them the fix. Ask: "What do you notice? What might cause that?"
  • Reframing failure: If a student gets frustrated after the first boat sinks, say: "That tells us so much -- now we know exactly what to fix. That is what engineers do." Model the language: "That's data, not failure."
  • Typical results: First-attempt 2nd graders typically hold 5-15 pennies. With one thoughtful improvement (wider base or taller walls), 20-40+ pennies is very achievable.
  • Third sheet opportunity: If time allows, the third sheet is a great bonus. Students who really understand the principles can sometimes hit 50+ pennies with a very wide, shallow boat with tall walls.
  • For younger siblings: Let them add pennies and count along. They can participate meaningfully without building the boat themselves.
  • Connecting to unit vocabulary: Ask students to use properties vocabulary: "The foil is _______ (flexible, waterproof) which makes it good for a boat because _______."
