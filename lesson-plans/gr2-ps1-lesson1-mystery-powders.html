🔍 Mystery Powders: What's in Your Kitchen?

Physical Science — Matter Unit, Lesson 1

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Matter & Properties
🧪 🥄 🫙
Four mystery powders, three tests, one big scientific discovery — all from your kitchen!
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-PS1-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to describe and classify different kinds of materials by their observable properties.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will OBSERVE and RECORD the properties of 4 mystery powders using 3 different tests, and USE their data to identify each powder.

💡 The Big Idea

Scientists identify unknown substances by testing their properties — color, texture, and how they react with water, vinegar, and heat. You can do the same thing right in your kitchen! Today, your child becomes a real scientist investigating four mystery powders hidden in plain sight: things you probably cook with every week.

🔬 The Science

Every material has properties — characteristics you can observe and measure. Some properties you can see (color, shape) or feel (smooth, grainy, fluffy). Others only show up when you test the material — like whether it dissolves in water, or fizzes when vinegar touches it.

When a material reacts with something (like baking soda reacting with vinegar to make bubbles), that reaction is called a chemical reaction. The fizzing you see is carbon dioxide gas being released — the same gas in soda bubbles! Scientists use these reactions as tests to identify unknown materials.

One of today's powders — cornstarch mixed with water — creates something truly strange: a non-Newtonian fluid. It acts like a solid when you push or squeeze it quickly, but flows like a liquid when left alone. Scientists call this "oobleck," and it doesn't fit neatly into either the solid or liquid category. That's what makes science so exciting: nature always has surprises!

💡 Key vocabulary: property, observe, dissolve, react, solid, liquid, non-Newtonian fluid, chemical reaction

📦 Materials

🏠 All From Your Kitchen Pantry

  • Baking soda
  • Salt (table salt)
  • Sugar (granulated white sugar)
  • Cornstarch
  • White vinegar
  • Water

🧰 Setup Supplies

  • 4 small cups, plates, or muffin tin sections (labeled A, B, C, D with tape and marker)
  • Pipette, eyedropper, or small spoon for adding liquids
  • Paper for recording observations (or the student worksheet on page 3)
  • Pencil and crayons
  • Optional: 1 large bowl for the oobleck bonus activity

🔬 Let's Get Started

Before You Begin — Setup (5 min, adult only)
Label 4 small cups or sections A, B, C, D with tape and a marker. Add a small amount (about 1 teaspoon) of each powder:
  • Cup A = baking soda
  • Cup B = salt
  • Cup C = sugar
  • Cup D = cornstarch
⚠️ Do NOT tell your child what's in each cup. These are "mystery powders" — they will figure it out through testing!
Step 1 — Introduce the Challenge (2 min)
Say: "I have four mystery powders here. Your job is to figure out what each one is — but you can only use science! You'll do three tests and record your observations. At the end, you'll make your best guess for each powder."
Give your child the student worksheet (page 3) and something to write with.
Step 2 — Test 1: Look & Feel (5 min)
Ask your child to carefully observe each powder — without tasting! For each cup, record:
  • What color is it?
  • What does the texture look like? (smooth, grainy, fluffy, chunky?)
  • Does it clump together or stay loose?
Encourage them to gently touch each powder between two fingers to feel the texture. Record observations in the data table.
Step 3 — Test 2: Water Test (8 min)
Using a pipette, eyedropper, or small spoon, add a few drops of water to each powder. Watch carefully! Ask:
  • Does it dissolve (disappear into the water)?
  • Does it make a paste?
  • Does anything unusual happen?
Record observations for each powder. ⭐ WOW MOMENT — Watch what cornstarch does! It forms a thick paste that feels solid when pressed but flows like a liquid when still.
Step 4 — Test 3: Vinegar Test (8 min)
Add a few drops of white vinegar to each powder. Watch carefully!
  • Does anything happen immediately?
  • Do any bubbles form?
  • Is any reaction bigger than others?
Record observations. 🎉 REVEAL MOMENT — One powder will fizz dramatically! That fizzing is a chemical reaction — baking soda reacting with the acid in vinegar to make carbon dioxide gas (the same gas in soda bubbles!).
💡 Tip: Save the vinegar test for last — the baking soda reaction is the most dramatic and makes for a great reveal! Build the suspense.
Step 5 — Make Your Guesses (5 min)
After all three tests, ask: "Based on your data, which powder do you think is which?" Have your child fill in their guesses on the worksheet, then reveal the answers together. How many did they get right?
Step 6 — Bonus: Oobleck! (5–10 min)
Once they've identified the cornstarch, make oobleck in a bowl! Mix 1 cup cornstarch + ½ cup water. Then:
  • Slap the surface quickly — it acts like a solid!
  • Let your hand rest in it — it flows like a liquid!
  • Pick up a handful and squeeze — it solidifies in your hand
  • Let go — it melts and drips away
This is a non-Newtonian fluid — it doesn't follow the normal rules of solids and liquids. Scientists are still studying materials like this!

📊 What to Expect — Reaction Guide (for adult reference)

Powder Test 1: Look & Feel Test 2: Water Test 3: Vinegar
A — Baking Soda White, very fine and smooth, slightly fluffy, may clump Dissolves slowly in plain water; no dramatic reaction FIZZES dramatically! Lots of bubbles — CO₂ gas released
B — Salt White, grainy, chunky crystals, sparkly Dissolves clearly and quickly; water looks clear No reaction — nothing happens
C — Sugar White, grainy (smaller grains than salt), slightly sparkly Dissolves very quickly and clearly; water may look slightly sweet-smelling No reaction — nothing happens
D — Cornstarch White, very fine and silky-smooth, squeaky between fingers Forms thick, gooey paste — feels solid when pressed, liquid when still! (Non-Newtonian fluid) Slight thickening; no dramatic reaction

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: "The baking soda fizzed when you added vinegar, but salt didn't. What does that tell you about these two powders?"

Suggested answer: They have different chemical properties! Baking soda reacts with vinegar (an acid) to make carbon dioxide gas — that's the fizzing. Salt doesn't react with vinegar at all. Scientists use reactions like this to identify unknown substances — it's called a chemical test.

🗣️ Question 2: "The cornstarch and water mixture acts strange — it's solid when you squeeze it and liquid when you let go. Is it a solid or a liquid?"

Suggested answer: It's actually BOTH — scientists call it a non-Newtonian fluid. It doesn't follow the normal rules of solids and liquids. This shows that some materials have unusual properties that don't fit neatly into categories — which is why scientists have to keep observing and testing!

🚀 Extensions

  • 🧪 Iodine Test: If you have iodine solution (available at pharmacies), add a drop to each powder. Iodine turns dark purple-black on starch — it will dramatically confirm cornstarch and give no reaction to the others!
  • 🍰 Baking Connection: Baking soda in recipes reacts with acids (buttermilk, lemon juice, vinegar) to make baked goods rise — the same reaction you just observed! Try making simple soda bread together and watch it happen in real life.
  • 🔍 Mystery #5: Try testing flour, baking powder, or powdered sugar. Can your child use the same three tests to identify a new mystery powder?
  • 📚 Read Aloud: "What Is Matter?" by Don L. Curry or "Bartholomew and the Oobleck" by Dr. Seuss (the origin of the word "oobleck"!)
  • 🌡️ Heating Extension: With adult supervision, heat small amounts on a metal spoon over a candle or stove. Sugar will melt and caramelize; cornstarch will toast and smell nutty; salt and baking soda will not melt easily. This introduces the property of melting point!

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Setup is key: Label cups A/B/C/D with tape and marker before your child arrives. Do NOT reveal what's in each — the mystery is the whole motivation!
  • Save the vinegar test for last: The baking soda + vinegar reaction is dramatic and delightful. Building up to it makes the reveal more satisfying.
  • Safety: All materials are food-safe kitchen ingredients, but encourage your child to stay in "scientist mode" — no tasting during the investigation!
  • Oobleck tip: Mix 1 cup cornstarch + ½ cup water. Slap it — it's solid. Let it rest — it flows. Kids are genuinely amazed. Search "non-Newtonian fluid" online to find videos that explain the physics in fun ways.
  • Salt vs. sugar: These two look very similar — both are white crystals that dissolve clearly. Salt crystals are generally larger and blockier; sugar crystals are finer. Kids may need to look very closely, and it's okay if they mix these up — that's real science! The discussion about how subtle differences matter is valuable.
  • Vocabulary to reinforce: property, observe, dissolve, react, solid, liquid, non-Newtonian fluid
  • Cross-subject connection: This lesson connects directly to cooking and baking! Baking soda in recipes reacts with acid ingredients to produce carbon dioxide, making baked goods rise. Same reaction, different context.
  • Assessment: Can your child correctly identify at least 3 of 4 powders? Can they explain what property helped them figure it out? Can they describe the cornstarch-water mixture using the word "non-Newtonian fluid"? That's success!

🔑 PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to student

Mystery Powder Identities

  • ✅ Powder A = Baking Soda
  • ✅ Powder B = Salt
  • ✅ Powder C = Sugar
  • ✅ Powder D = Cornstarch

Science Sentence Answer

Scientists identify materials by testing their properties.

Key Identifying Properties

  • Baking Soda (A): Very fine and smooth powder; fizzes dramatically with vinegar (CO₂ gas released); dissolves slowly in water.
  • Salt (B): Coarse, blocky crystals; sparkling; dissolves clearly in water; no reaction with vinegar.
  • Sugar (C): Fine crystals (smaller than salt); dissolves quickly and clearly in water; no reaction with vinegar. Sugar and salt look similar — texture difference is subtle.
  • Cornstarch (D): Very silky, squeaky-smooth texture; forms thick non-Newtonian paste with water (solid when pressed, liquid when still); slight thickening with vinegar but no dramatic reaction.

💡 Teaching Notes

  • Salt vs. Sugar: These are the trickiest pair — both white, both dissolve clearly in water, both show no reaction with vinegar. The main distinguishing property is crystal texture (salt is larger and blockier) and taste — though in a real scientist investigation, we don't taste! If your child mixes these up, discuss: this is exactly why scientists use multiple tests and look at subtle differences.
  • Baking Soda with water: Baking soda dissolves slowly in plain water without dramatic fizzing. The fizzing only occurs with an acid — like vinegar. This is an important distinction: the dramatic fizzing with vinegar is the definitive test for baking soda. Plain water will not produce the same reaction.
  • The Non-Newtonian Fluid moment: Cornstarch oobleck is the most memorable part of this lesson. When your child encounters it during the water test, you can just let them explore without explanation — save "non-Newtonian fluid" for the discussion after all tests are done.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Child can correctly identify at least 3 of 4 mystery powders using their recorded data.
  • Child can explain what property helped them figure out a specific powder (e.g., "the fizzing told me it was baking soda").
  • Child can describe the cornstarch-water mixture using words like "acts like a solid sometimes and a liquid sometimes."
  • Bonus: Child uses the word "properties" to describe what scientists test to identify materials.
