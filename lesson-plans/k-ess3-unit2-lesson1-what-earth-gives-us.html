🌿 What Does Earth Give Us? 🌿

Earth Science — Earth & Human Activity | Unit 2, Lesson 1

📚 Grade: Kindergarten ⏱️ Duration: 30–45 minutes 🌍 Subject: Earth Science — Earth & Human Activity 🧰 Kit Lesson: No
🌳 💧 🪨 ☀️ 🌾
Earth gives us everything we need — can you spot what's natural and what's human-made?
📋 NGSS Standard:
K-ESS3-1 — Use a model to represent the relationship between the needs of different plants or animals (including humans) and the places they live.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will investigate what Does Earth Give Us?, make observations during the lesson activity, and explain what they learned using evidence from the lesson.

🎯 The Big Idea

Everything we use comes from Earth! Trees give us wood. Soil grows our food. Water keeps us alive. Even plastic — which looks totally human-made — started as oil deep underground. In this lesson, students explore the difference between natural resources (things Earth makes) and human-made items (things people create), and discover that almost everything traces back to something natural.

🔬 The Science

A natural resource is something from nature that living things need or use. Air, water, sunlight, soil, plants, and rocks are all natural resources. Humans use these natural resources to build houses, make clothes, grow food, and create products. Even things that look completely human-made — like glass, plastic, or cotton clothes — started as natural materials. Glass comes from melted sand. Plastic comes from oil. Cotton comes from a plant. Understanding where things come from helps us appreciate Earth and take care of it!

💡 Key vocabulary: natural resource, human-made, material, soil, water, wood, rock, cotton, oil, glass

📦 Materials

🏠 Items to Gather for the Sorting Game

  • Wood items: wooden spoon, wood floor, pencil (wood + graphite)
  • Plastic items: plastic toy, plastic cup, plastic bag
  • Natural food: apple, carrot, or any fresh fruit/vegetable
  • Processed food: candy, crackers, chips
  • Fabric: cotton shirt or towel (cotton is a plant!)
  • Glass window or glass cup
  • Stone or rock from outside

🧰 For the Worksheet

  • Printed sorting mat (included at end of lesson)
  • Printed picture cards — cut apart
  • Scissors, glue stick
  • Crayons or markers

🔬 Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Introduce Natural Resources (5 min)
Hold up a glass of water. Ask: "Where did this come from?" (A river, a well, rain — from Earth!) Hold up a wooden spoon: "Where did this come from?" (A tree — from Earth!) Explain: "Earth gives us everything we need to live. These are called natural resources — things from nature."
Step 2 — Walk Around the House (10 min)
Go on a "nature detective" walk around your home. Point to objects and ask: "Is this natural — did Earth make it? Or did people make it?"
  • 🪵 Wood floor → Natural (from a tree)
  • 🧸 Plastic toy → Human-made (but plastic = oil from Earth!)
  • 👕 Cotton shirt → Both! (cotton plant + human stitching)
  • 🪟 Glass window → Processed natural (sand melted into glass)
  • 🍎 Apple → Natural (from a tree)
  • 🍬 Candy → Human-made (but sugar comes from sugar cane!)
Start two piles: one for natural, one for human-made.
Step 3 — The "Where Did It Come From?" Game (10 min)
Pick up each human-made item and trace it back to Earth. "This plastic toy — where did the plastic come from?" → Oil, which comes from deep in the Earth! "This glass cup — where did the glass come from?" → Sand from the ground! Even human-made things started as natural materials. Humans just changed them.
Step 4 — Student Worksheet Sort (10–15 min)
Cut out the picture cards together. Sort each card into "Natural" or "Human-Made" on the sorting mat. Some cards have a note: "started natural!" — these go in the human-made pile but kids can add an arrow showing where they came from.
Step 5 — Discuss & Share (5 min)
Go through the worksheet together. Celebrate discoveries! Ask the discussion questions below. Kids write or draw their favorite natural resource on the back.

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Talk about these together:
  • Can you name 5 things that come from Earth?
  • What would happen if we ran out of trees? What would we lose?
  • Is plastic natural or human-made? (Trick question — where did the oil come from?)
  • Which natural resource do you use the most every day?
  • What do animals need from Earth? (Food, water, shelter — same as us!)
💡 Suggested answer — plastic discussion: Plastic is human-made, but it started as oil — a natural resource from deep underground. This is a great "both" answer! Plastic started natural, then humans changed it. That's why it's important: once we use oil to make plastic, we can't put the oil back.

🚀 Extensions

  • 🌲 Trace a pencil: A pencil is wood (tree) + graphite (a mineral from Earth) + metal (mined) + rubber eraser (rubber tree). Everything traces back to Earth!
  • 🗺️ Nature walk: Go outside and collect 5 natural items (leaves, rocks, sticks, soil, seed pods). Make a nature table display.
  • 📚 Read aloud: "Dirt: The Scoop on Soil" by Natalie Rosinsky or "Why Should I Save Energy?" by Jen Green
  • 🎨 Art project: Draw a picture of your favorite natural resource and how you use it.
  • 🧪 Science challenge: Make a list of everything you eat in one day. Which ingredients came directly from nature? Which were processed by people?

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • The "both" category: Many items will be BOTH natural + human-made (cotton shirt, glass, paper). Praise kids who notice this — it shows nuanced thinking. Add a third "both" pile if kids want.
  • Plastic is tricky: Kids often say plastic is human-made. It is! But it started as oil — a natural resource. Use this to introduce the idea that humans transform natural resources into new things.
  • Connect to animal needs (K-ESS3-1): Birds need trees for nests (wood/natural). Fish need water (natural). This connects K-ESS3-1 — the relationship between animal needs and where they live. To directly address the "use a model" performance expectation, have students draw one animal and label what that animal needs from Earth (food, water, shelter material). The sorting mat already models which things come from nature; adding the animal-needs drawing completes the K-ESS3-1 connection.
  • Assessment: Can students identify at least 5 natural resources by name? Can they correctly sort most items and explain their reasoning? That's success.

💡 Notes on Tricky Cards

  • 🧸 Plastic toy: Plastic is human-made, but it started as oil — a natural resource from Earth. Oil formed from ancient plants and animals underground over millions of years.
  • 🪟 Glass window: Glass is made by melting sand at very high temperatures. Sand = natural. Glass = processed by humans. A great "started natural" example.
  • 👕 Cotton shirt: Cotton fiber grows on a cotton plant (natural!), but humans harvested it, spun it into thread, wove it into fabric, and sewed the shirt. This is "both" — and both answers are worth praising.
  • 🍫 Chocolate bar: Made from cacao beans (plant = natural), but processed with sugar and milk by people. "Both" is a great answer.

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student can name at least 5 natural resources (water, soil, trees, rocks, sunlight, air, etc.).
  • Student can sort most cards correctly and explain basic reasoning.
  • Student understands that even human-made things often started as natural resources.
