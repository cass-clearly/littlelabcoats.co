Researching Constraints and Materials

Engineering — Design Process | Unit 1, Lesson 2 of 6

📚 Grade: 3rd Grade⏱️ Duration: 45–60 minutes🛠️ Subject: Engineering — Design Process
📏🧺🧻
Engineers study their limits and materials before they choose a design.
📋 NGSS Standard:
3-5-ETS1-1 — Define a simple design problem reflecting a need or a want that includes specified criteria for success and constraints on materials, time, or cost.

🎯 Learning Objective

Students will identify important constraints and compare material properties for the fragile-object protection challenge.

💡 The Big Idea

Good engineering choices depend on limits. Engineers must understand the allowed materials, time, size, and cost before they decide what to build.

🔬 The Science

Science Snapshot
Material research helps turn guessing into informed design choices.

What is happening?

Before building, engineers compare materials and learn the limits of the challenge. A material is useful only when its properties match the job the design needs it to do.

Why it works that way

For a fragile-object challenge, cushioning, stiffness, lightness, and shape all matter. Researching materials helps students predict which ones may absorb impact, spread force, or hold the object in place before they start building.

Look for this in the lesson

Students should talk about why a material might help. The lesson gets stronger when they compare properties instead of just naming favorite craft supplies.

Ask while you read: Which material property seems most important for protecting a fragile object?

Let's Get Started

Step 1 — Revisit the Problem Statement: Restate the unit challenge clearly: design something that protects a fragile object during a drop from table height, using only the allowed materials. Then ask what rules or limits the design must follow.
Step 2 — List Constraints: Identify time, size, material, and cost-style limits that shape the build.
Step 3 — Handle and Compare Materials: Look at a few sample materials and describe which are soft, stiff, light, bendy, or protective.
Step 4 — Match Material to Need: Students connect a material property to a challenge need, such as cushioning a fragile landing.
Step 5 — Explain a Choice: Students write which material or limit matters most and why it should influence the design.

Discussion Questions

What is a constraint in this challenge?Look for answers like: A rule or limit, like only using certain materials or fitting inside a size limit.
Why is cardboard different from cotton balls in a protection challenge?Look for answers like: Cardboard can hold shape while cotton balls can cushion impact.
How can a time limit affect the design?Look for answers like: It may require a simpler plan or faster-to-build materials.
Why do engineers study materials before building?Look for answers like: Different materials do different jobs and affect success.
How are criteria and constraints different?Look for answers like: Criteria say what success looks like; constraints are the limits the design must follow.

Extensions

Extension: Create a home “material museum” with three objects labeled soft, stiff, and flexible.

Extension: Ask a family member which material they would trust most for cushioning and have them explain why.

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: Concrete material handling helps this lesson feel real, especially for homeschool learners.

Teaching note: Keep reminding students that one material rarely does every job well on its own.

✂️ PARENT ANSWER KEY — Detach before giving worksheet to students

1️⃣ Identify the Limits — Sample Use

Accept constraints such as only using allowed materials, finishing within a time limit, or keeping the design small enough for the drop test.

2️⃣ Compare Materials — Sample Ideas

Accept pairings such as cardboard/holds shape, cotton balls/cushions impact, tape/holds parts together, fabric/soft padding.

3️⃣ Make a Reasoned Choice — Model Response

“I think soft padding matters for this design because it can cushion the landing and help protect the fragile object.”

✅ What Success Looks Like

  • Student identifies at least two relevant constraints.
  • Student matches material properties to a real design need.
  • Student explains a material or constraint choice with reasoning.

🛠️ Troubleshooting / Teaching Notes

  • If a student confuses criteria and constraints, ask whether the idea describes success or a limit.
  • If a student picks a material without reasoning, ask what job that material is supposed to do.
