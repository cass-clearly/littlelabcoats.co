🧪 What Is Matter? Investigating Properties and States

Physical Science Unit, Lesson 1

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45–55 minutes 🔬 Subject: Physical Science — Matter 🧰 Materials: Household items only
🪨💧🎈🧊🔍
Second graders act like scientists when they classify materials using evidence from what they observe.
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-PS1-1 — Plan and conduct an investigation to describe and classify different kinds of materials by their observable properties.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Classify materials as solids, liquids, or gases using observable evidence
  • Describe and compare matter using properties such as shape, texture, color, hardness, and ability to flow
  • Explain how the same material can change state when conditions change, such as ice melting into liquid water

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need (All Household Items)

  • A small rock or pebble
  • A cup of water
  • An inflated balloon
  • An ice cube
  • A spoonful of cooking oil or syrup in a cup
  • A plastic cup, bowl, or clear jar
  • Paper and pencil or crayons

📖 Vocabulary

Matter
Anything that takes up space and has mass
Property
Something you can observe or test about a material
Classify
To sort things into groups using evidence
Solid
Matter that keeps its own shape
Liquid
Matter that flows and takes the shape of its container
Gas
Matter that spreads out to fill available space

🌍 The Science

Second graders are ready to do more than memorize the words solid, liquid, and gas. In this lesson, students act like scientists by observing materials closely, noticing their properties, and using evidence to decide how to classify them. Observable properties are things we can notice with our senses or simple tests, such as shape, texture, color, hardness, whether something pours, and whether it spreads out to fill a space.

Solids keep their own shape. Liquids do not keep their own shape — they flow and take the shape of the container they are in. Gases can be harder to notice because many gases are invisible, but they still take up space and can push on objects, like the air inside a balloon. Students should learn that classification is not guessing; it is using what you observe to support a decision.

This lesson also introduces the idea that matter can change state. An ice cube and liquid water are both matter, but they behave differently because one is a solid and the other is a liquid. That gives Grade 2 students a stronger bridge from simple sorting into real scientific thinking about materials and change.

💡 Scientist tip: Do not ask only "What is it?" Ask "What evidence tells you that?" That pushes the lesson from basic naming into real classification.

🔬 Investigation: Property Detectives

Step 1 — Observe like a scientist: Place the rock, water, balloon, ice cube, and oil or syrup on the table. Ask students to describe what they notice first: shape, feel, color, and whether the material stays put or moves.
Step 2 — Set up three groups: Label three spaces or papers SOLID, LIQUID, and GAS. Explain that scientists sort by evidence, not by guessing.
Step 3 — Test for shape: Pick up the rock and then the ice cube. Ask: Do these keep their own shape when we move them? Record the evidence and classify them as solids.
Step 4 — Test for flowing: Pour the water into a different container. Then slowly pour the oil or syrup. Ask: What stayed the same, and what changed? Students should notice that both liquids take the shape of the container, even if one flows faster than the other.
Step 5 — Test for gas evidence: Squeeze the balloon gently. Ask: Can you see the gas clearly? What evidence shows it is there anyway? Students should notice the balloon is full, takes up space, and pushes back.
Step 6 — Compare properties within a group: Ask students how the rock and ice cube are alike and different. Both are solids, but one may be rough while the other is smooth and cold. This helps students see that materials in the same state can still have different properties.
Step 7 — Watch a state change: Hold the ice cube for a minute or leave it on a plate. Ask: What evidence shows the material is changing? Is it still keeping its own shape? Students should notice that melting changes the solid into a liquid.
Step 8 — Make a claim with evidence: Have students finish a sentence such as: “I know oil is a liquid because it poured and took the shape of the cup.”

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: Why are both a rock and an ice cube solids even though they do not look or feel the same?

Suggested answer: They have different properties, but both keep their own shape when moved, which is the key evidence for classifying them as solids.

🗣️ Question 2: Water and oil are both liquids. What evidence shows that, and what properties make them different from each other?

Suggested answer: Both flow and take the shape of the container. They may differ in color, thickness, or how quickly they pour.

🗣️ Question 3: If we cannot easily see air, why do scientists still say it is matter?

Suggested answer: Air takes up space, fills the balloon, and pushes back when the balloon is squeezed, so we have evidence that it is there.

🗣️ Question 4: What happened when the ice cube started melting, and what does that tell us about matter?

Suggested answer: The ice stopped keeping its own shape and became liquid water. This shows the same material can change from one state of matter to another.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Why this is stronger for Grade 2: Students are not just naming states of matter. They are comparing materials, classifying by observable properties, and explaining their thinking with evidence.
  • Good evidence language to coach: “It kept its shape.” “It poured.” “It spread out.” “It filled the balloon.” “It changed from solid to liquid.”
  • Important distinction: A state of matter is not the same as a single property. A material can be smooth or rough and still be a solid.
  • Melting connection: Keep the focus on behavior change. Ice and liquid water are the same material, but they behave differently in different states.
  • Strong student response example: “I know the balloon has gas in it because the air filled the balloon and pushed back when I squeezed it.”
  • If students need support: Give sentence frames, but still require a piece of evidence in every answer.
  • Extension: Add another mystery material such as sand, honey, juice, or steam from warm water and ask students to defend their classification.
