🪨 What Is Matter? Solids, Liquids, and Gases 💧🎈

Physical Science Unit, Lesson 1

📚 Grade: 2nd Grade ⏱️ Duration: 45 minutes 🔬 Subject: Physical Science -- Matter 🧰 Materials: Household items only
🪨💧🎈🧊🥤
Everything around us is matter -- and it comes in three amazing forms!
📋 NGSS Standard:
2-PS1-1 -- Plan and conduct an investigation to describe and classify different kinds of materials by their observable properties.

🎯 Learning Goals

  • Identify solids, liquids, and gases as the three states of matter
  • Describe matter using observable properties such as color, texture, shape, and hardness
  • Sort everyday objects into their correct state of matter

📦 Materials

🏠 What You Need (All Household Items)

  • A small rock or pebble
  • A cup of water or juice
  • A balloon (inflate it with air before the lesson)
  • A wooden block or thick hardcover book
  • A handful of ice cubes
  • Paper and crayons for recording

📖 Vocabulary

Matter
Everything around us that takes up space and has mass
Solid
Matter that keeps its own shape; you can hold it
Liquid
Matter that flows and takes the shape of its container
Gas
Matter that spreads out to fill all available space
Property
A describing word for how something looks, feels, or behaves
Mass
How much stuff (matter) is in an object

🌍 Background

Matter is everything around us. The chair you sit on, the water you drink, the air you breathe -- all of it is matter. Scientists define matter as anything that takes up space and has mass. Most of what we encounter every day falls into one of three states: solid, liquid, or gas.

Solids keep their own shape. You can pick them up, stack them, and they do not change shape unless you break or bend them. A rock, a wooden block, an ice cube, and even a crumpled piece of paper are all solids. Liquids, on the other hand, flow. Pour water into a tall glass and it takes the shape of the glass. Pour it into a bowl and it fills the bowl. Liquids always take the shape of their container.

Gases are trickier to see but they are everywhere. Air is a gas. When you blow up a balloon, you fill it with air (a gas), and the gas fills every bit of space inside. If you let the balloon go, the gas rushes out in all directions. Some gases have color or smell (think of steam from a kettle), but most are invisible. That is what makes gases so fascinating -- they are always around us, even when we cannot see them!

🔬 Procedure: The Matter Sort

Step 1: Lay out all your materials on the table. Ask: "What do all of these things have in common?" (They are all matter -- they all take up space!)
Step 2: Make three labels on paper: SOLID, LIQUID, GAS. Place them as column headers on your table.
Step 3: Pick up the rock. Feel it. Does it keep its own shape when you hold it? Yes! Place it under SOLID.
Step 4: Pour a little water into a cup. Does the water keep its own shape, or does it take the shape of the cup? Place the cup under LIQUID.
Step 5: Hold the inflated balloon. Squeeze it gently -- can you feel the air pushing back? Air is a gas that fills the whole balloon. Place it under GAS.
Step 6: Now sort the wooden block and ice cube. Where does each belong? (Both are solids -- they hold their shape.)
Step 7: For each object, write or draw 2 properties next to it: one about how it looks, one about how it feels. For example: rock = gray, rough. Ask: "What did you observe that helped you decide where it belongs?"
Step 8 (Bonus Challenge): Hold an ice cube in your warm hand. Watch what happens. The solid slowly turns into a liquid! Ask: "What changed, and what do we call it now?"

💬 Discussion Questions

🗣️ Question 1: What makes a solid different from a liquid?

Suggested answer: Solids keep their own shape -- you can hold them without them changing form. Liquids flow and take the shape of their container. A key test: can you pick it up with your fingers without it running away? Probably a solid. Does it pour? Probably a liquid.

🗣️ Question 2: Can you think of something that can be in two different states of matter?

Suggested answer: Water is the perfect example! Water is a liquid, ice is a solid (frozen water), and steam is a gas (heated water). Chocolate can be a solid or a liquid when melted. Butter too! These changes happen because of heating and cooling.

🗣️ Question 3: We can see solids and liquids. How do we know gases are really there?

Suggested answer: Feel your breath on your hand -- that is gas (air) moving. Blow up a balloon -- the gas takes up space and pushes the sides out. Smell fresh cookies baking -- gas molecules from the cookie travel to your nose. We cannot always see gases, but we can feel and smell them!

🗣️ Question 4: If you had a solid, a liquid, and a gas in three containers, how could you tell them apart without labels?

Suggested answer: Tap the container -- a solid inside will feel heavy and will not slosh. A liquid will slosh and you might hear it. A gas container will feel very light. You could also carefully open each: the solid stays put, the liquid might spill, the gas escapes immediately.

👩‍🏫 Parent/Teacher Notes

  • Key insight for this lesson: State of matter is about SHAPE behavior. Solids hold their shape, liquids flow and conform, gases expand to fill space. Keep returning to these definitions throughout the activity.
  • Ice/water/steam: This is the classic example of one substance in all three states. If possible, show ice melting in a warm cup during the lesson. It makes the concept very concrete.
  • For younger or struggling learners: Focus just on solid vs. liquid first. Gas can be a stretch concept -- the balloon is the best concrete anchor. Ask: "What is inside this balloon?" (Air. Air is a gas.)
  • Mass: Introduced but does not need deep explanation at this level. It is simply "how much stuff is in it." Weight works as an informal equivalent for now.
  • Fill-in-the-blank answers: Matter takes up space / has mass / shape / container / expand (or spread out).
  • Model sentence to listen for: “I know water is a liquid because it changed shape to fit the cup, but the rock stayed the same shape, so it is a solid.”
  • Gifted extension: Ask "Is fire a state of matter?" (No -- it is a chemical reaction producing light and heat, not a state of matter itself. But it is a great conversation starter!)
  • For the matter sort table: Any valid examples work. Encourage students to think of items not used in the lesson.
