The Big Idea

Movement is not just about going somewhere. Movement structures help animals get food, escape predators, migrate, care for young, and reach safer places.

An animal’s movement structure is shaped by where it lives and what it needs to do. The structure-function link becomes clearer when we compare water, land, and air examples.

The Science

Movement structures include legs, wings, fins, flippers, tails, and muscles working with the skeleton. Different structures support different patterns of movement such as swimming, flying, hopping, climbing, or gliding. A structure can support both behavior and survival at the same time.

Key vocabulary: movement structure, fin, wing, flipper, glide, migrate, escape, balance

Materials

Gather Before You Start

  • printed lesson pages and Student Worksheet
  • animal movement photo cards or short descriptions
  • open floor space for gesture-modeling if desired
  • pencil

Let's Get Started

Step 1: Begin with a quick compare: Which structure would work best in water? on land? in the air?
Step 2: Ask students to connect movement to a survival reason such as catching food, escaping danger, or reaching a nesting place.
Step 3: Model how one structure can support more than one job: a tail may help balance, steer, or communicate.
Step 4: Students complete a compare table for two or three animals with different movement structures.
Step 5: Finish with a short argument: “This structure helps survival because ...”

Discussion Questions

Q: Why do swimming structures look different from flying structures?Look for environment and job differences.
Q: How can movement support both behavior and survival?Examples: migration, hunting, escaping, caring for offspring.
Q: What evidence would you use to argue that a tail matters?Balance, steering, braking, signaling, or support examples.

Extensions

Watch birds, pets, or playground movement and make your own structure-function notes. What body part seems most important to the movement you observe?

Parent/Teacher Notes

Teaching note: This lesson should stay grounded in structures, not turn into a pure locomotion unit. The central question remains: which structure helps which survival job?

🔑 Parent Answer Key

Detach note: Student wording may vary if the structure-function science is correct.

  • What Success Looks Like: Student names a movement structure, explains how it moves the animal, and links that movement to survival or behavior.
  • Acceptable examples: Fins help fish swim to food and away from predators; wings help birds fly to nests or food; strong back legs help kangaroos move quickly; tails can help steer or balance.
  • Common fix: If a student writes only “wings help fly,” ask “Why does flying matter for this animal?”
